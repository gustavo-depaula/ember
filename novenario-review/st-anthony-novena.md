# St. Anthony of Padua — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** St. Anthony of Padua, faithful disciple of Christ and servant of the poor, intercede for us before God. Teach us to love the Gospel, seek holiness in ordinary life, and trust in divine providence in every need. Amen.

**Day 1 — Called to follow Christ in simplicity:** St. Anthony left honors and comforts to follow Christ in the Franciscan path of poverty and joy. He teaches us to seek God above possessions and to live with a free and trusting heart.  _(Intention: For freedom from attachment and a deeper desire to follow Jesus.)_

**Day 2 — Love for Sacred Scripture and truth:** Known as a great preacher and teacher, St. Anthony loved the Word of God and proclaimed it with clarity. He invites us to read Scripture prayerfully and to shape our lives by the truth of the Gospel.  _(Intention: For hunger for God's Word and fidelity to Catholic truth.)_

**Day 3 — Humility in service and preaching:** Though gifted and admired, St. Anthony remained humble, seeking only God's glory. His witness teaches us to serve quietly, speak with charity, and let Christ be seen in all we do.  _(Intention: For humility, docility, and purity of intention in our work.)_

**Day 4 — Charity toward the poor and suffering:** St. Anthony cared for the poor with concrete compassion, seeing Christ in those in need. He calls us to generous mercy, practical help, and a heart attentive to those who suffer.  _(Intention: For greater charity toward the poor, sick, and abandoned.)_

**Day 5 — Purity of heart and conversion:** St. Anthony preached conversion with zeal and tenderness, leading sinners back to God's mercy. Through his intercession, we ask for a clean heart and the courage to repent sincerely.  _(Intention: For true repentance and growth in purity and holiness.)_

**Day 6 — Intercession for families and daily work:** In ordinary life, God sanctifies homes, labor, and responsibilities. St. Anthony encourages us to entrust family needs and daily burdens to the Lord with confidence.  _(Intention: For peace in families, honest work, and providence in material needs.)_

**Day 7 — Hope in what is lost and forgotten:** Many invoke St. Anthony when something is lost, but his deepest gift is leading souls back to God. He reminds us that divine grace can restore what seems broken, forgotten, or far away.  _(Intention: For the recovery of what is truly needed and the return of hearts to God.)_

**Day 8 — Peace and reconciliation:** St. Anthony worked for justice and reconciliation among divided people. Under his patronage we ask for healed relationships, forgiven offenses, and hearts renewed in peace.  _(Intention: For reconciliation in families, friendships, and communities.)_

**Day 9 — Perseverance in holiness and mission:** As this novena ends, we thank God for every grace received through St. Anthony's intercession. We ask to remain faithful, to love Christ above all, and to serve the Church with joy until the end.  _(Intention: For final perseverance and a life that gives glory to God.)_

(After every day: one Our Father, one Hail Mary, one Glory Be.)

**Closing prayer:** St. Anthony, friend of Jesus and helper of all who call upon you, obtain for us steadfast faith, generous charity, and perseverance in prayer. May we seek first the Kingdom of God and give thanks for every grace received. Amen.

_(A pt-BR version also exists in the app.)_

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas

Please hear the prayers he brings before Your throne on our behalf." The Day 1 intention asks God to inspire more men and women toward religious and priestly vocations and for eagerness to serve God and grow spiritually each day. Closing prayer (daily): "St. Anthony of Padua, pray for us! In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen." Structure is identical opening and closing each day, with only the day-specific virtue/petition changing.

## Fidelity note
Both versions are nine-day novenas to the same saint built on the same Our Father / Hail Mary / Glory Be frame, and both close with the invocation "St. Anthony, pray for us." The wording differs throughout: PrayMoreNovenas addresses its opening to God ("Dear Lord, we thank You…"), while Ember addresses St. Anthony directly, and the daily themes are independently composed (Ember leads with Franciscan poverty and Scripture; PrayMoreNovenas leads with vocations). Ember's meditations stay faithful to St. Anthony's authentic profile — Franciscan preacher, friend of the poor, Doctor of the Church, patron of lost things — so the adaptation is sound, but it is an original text rather than the PrayMoreNovenas prayers verbatim.
