# Our Lady Undoer of Knots — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O Mary, Undoer of Knots, with the same patient hands that untied the knot of our first parents' disobedience, take into your care the tangles of my life. There is no knot too tight for your maternal fingers, no situation beyond the reach of your Son's mercy. I come to you trusting, and I will not lose heart. Amen.

**Day 1 — The knot of disbelief:** The first knot is the secret doubt that God can or will help me. Mary, who believed when the angel spoke the impossible, gently loosens this knot and teaches my heart to trust again.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of my doubt; untie it and restore my faith.)_

**Day 2 — The knot of broken relationships:** Some knots bind us to people in resentment, estrangement, or wounded love. Mary, who held the family together at Cana and beneath the cross, takes these tangled bonds and works to make them whole.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of a wounded relationship; untie it with your peace.)_

**Day 3 — The knot of worry and fear:** Anxiety knots the heart until it can no longer rest, magnifying every shadow. Mary takes these fears one strand at a time and reminds me that her Son holds the future I cannot see.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of my fears; untie it and give me your peace.)_

**Day 4 — The knot of sin and habit:** Sin tightens into habits that we cannot loosen by willpower alone. Mary, refuge of sinners, draws these knots toward the mercy of her Son, where every chain is at last undone.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of a sin I cannot break; untie it by grace.)_

**Day 5 — The knot of my particular intention:** There is a tangle in my life that I scarcely know how to name, the very burden that led me to this novena. Today I place it, just as it is, into Mary's patient hands and let her begin to loosen it.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of my particular intention; untie it in God's wisdom.)_

**Day 6 — The knot of impatience:** I want every knot undone at once, but Mary works patiently, one careful turn after another. She teaches me that waiting on God is not idleness but trust, and that His timing is part of the answer.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of my impatience; untie it and teach me to wait.)_

**Day 7 — The knot of discouragement:** When knots resist for a long time, the heart grows weary and tempted to give up. Mary, who stood firm through the darkest hour, lifts my discouragement and steadies my hope in her Son.  _(Intention: I bring you the knot of my discouragement; untie it and renew my hope.)_

**Day 8 — The knots of others:** I am not the only one whose life is tangled; those I love carry knots of their own. Today I bring their burdens to Mary as well, trusting that her hands are wide enough for every heart.  _(Intention: I bring you the knots of those I love; untie them by your mercy.)_

**Day 9 — Surrendered into her hands:** On this last day I let go of my anxious grip and leave every knot, undone and still-tied alike, in Mary's care. Whatever she cannot yet loosen, she will hold; and whatever God permits to remain, she will help me carry in peace.  _(Intention: I surrender all my knots into your hands; do with them as God wills.)_

(After every day: one Our Father, one Hail Mary, one Glory Be.)

**Closing prayer:** O Blessed Mother, I thank you for taking my knots into your gentle hands. Untie them in the order and the time that God knows is best, and where they cannot yet be loosed, grant me the patience to wait in peace. Lead me, freed and unburdened, ever closer to your Son. Amen.

## Fidelity note
The Undoer of Knots devotion is modern (a Baroque Augsburg painting, rooted in St. Irenaeus' teaching that Mary's obedience untied the knot of Eve's disobedience, popularized worldwide by Pope Francis), and there is no single fixed traditional novena text to verify against — circulating versions vary. Ember's history note is accurate, and its opening prayer correctly grounds the image in the Irenaean "knot of our first parents' disobedience." Rather than copy a particular published version, Ember organizes the nine days around concrete kinds of "knots" (disbelief, broken relationships, fear, sin/habit, the personal intention, impatience, discouragement, others' knots, and final surrender), which is faithful to the spirit of the devotion. As a modern devotion with no canonical source, this is a sound, well-structured original treatment.
