# Novena to St. Benedict — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US

**Opening prayer:** Glorious St. Benedict, father of monks and patron of a peaceful death, you sought God above all things in silence and labor. As I begin this novena, obtain for me a heart ordered to God and freed from the snares of the evil one. Teach me to seek nothing dearer than Christ. Amen.

**Day 1 — Seeking God alone:** As a young man Benedict left Rome's pleasures to live hidden in a cave at Subiaco, desiring God alone. He teaches us that holiness begins when we truly seek the one thing necessary. _(Intention: For the grace to desire God above every earthly good.)_

**Day 2 — Ora et Labora:** Benedict ordered the monastic day around prayer and work, so that the hands labored while the heart adored. He teaches us that ordinary tasks, offered to God, become holy. _(Intention: That I may sanctify my daily work by offering it to God.)_

**Day 3 — Nothing dearer than Christ:** The Rule commands the monk to prefer nothing whatever to Christ. Benedict shows us that every choice of the day is finally a choice for or against the Lord. _(Intention: For the grace to put Christ before all things, every day.)_

**Day 4 — The school of humility:** Benedict made humility the foundation of his Rule, charting twelve steps by which the soul climbs to God by descending into truth. He teaches that self-knowledge before God is the root of every virtue. _(Intention: For a humble heart that knows itself before God.)_

**Day 5 — Stability of heart:** Benedict bound his monks to stability, that they might persevere in one place and one community rather than fleeing every difficulty. He teaches us that faithfulness is proven by staying. _(Intention: For perseverance and stability in my commitments and vocation.)_

**Day 6 — Welcoming Christ in the guest:** The Rule commands that every guest be received as Christ Himself, and the poor and the pilgrim with special care. Benedict teaches us to see the Lord in everyone who knocks at our door. _(Intention: For a spirit of hospitality and charity toward strangers.)_

**Day 7 — The cross against evil:** Tradition holds that Benedict made the sign of the cross over a poisoned cup, which shattered before it could harm him. His medal, marked 'May the Holy Cross be my light,' is a constant prayer for protection from evil. _(Intention: For protection against the snares and temptations of the evil one.)_

**Day 8 — Peace, that none may be troubled:** Benedict gave his order the motto Pax, peace, and ordered the monastery so that no one would be saddened or troubled in the house of God. He teaches us to be makers of peace in our homes and hearts. _(Intention: For peace in my family, my community, and the world.)_

**Day 9 — A holy and watchful death:** Benedict died standing in the chapel, his arms upheld by his brethren, after receiving the Body of the Lord. Patron of a happy death, he teaches us to keep death daily before our eyes and to die in the Lord's embrace. _(Intention: For the grace of final perseverance and a happy death.)_

(Each day the user also prays one Our Father, one Hail Mary, and one Glory Be.)

**Closing prayer:** St. Benedict, whose cross puts the enemy to flight, defend me against every temptation and lead me in the way of peace. May the balance of prayer and work order all my days, and may I persevere in the school of the Lord's service. Bring me at last to the kingdom to which your Rule points. Amen.

_(A pt-BR version also exists in the app.)_

## Source — FishEaters
FishEaters carries a single prayer repeated once a day for nine days (12–20 March, eve of the feast):

"O glorious St. Benedict, sublime model of all virtues, pure vessel of God's grace! Behold me, humbly kneeling at thy feet. I implore thy loving heart to pray for me before the throne of God. To thee I have recourse in all the dangers which daily surround me. Shield me against my enemies, inspire me to imitate thee in all things. May thy blessing be with me always, so that I may shun whatever God forbids and avoid the occasions of sin. Graciously obtain for me from God those favors and graces of which I stand so much in need, in the trials, miseries and afflictions of life. Thy heart was always so full of love, compassion, and mercy toward those who were afflicted or troubled in any way. Thou didst never dismiss without consolation and assistance anyone who had recourse to thee. I therefore invoke thy powerful intercession, in the confident hope that thou wilt hear my prayers and obtain for me the special grace and favor I so earnestly implore (mention your intentions here), if it be for the greater glory of God and the welfare of my soul. Help me, O great St. Benedict, to live and die as a faithful child of God, to be ever submissive to His holy will, and to attain the eternal happiness of heaven. Amen."

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
PMN's St. Benedict novena uses the same prayer text each of the nine days, framed by the Sign of the Cross. It opens "Holy St. Benedict, I ask that you pray with me now to our God, using your words for the intentions that are written on my heart," then quotes Benedict's own Rule ("What can be sweeter to us, dear ones, than this voice of the Lord inviting us?..." and the prologue's "let us ask the Lord that He supply by the help of His grace what is impossible to us by nature"), leaves a space for intentions, and dedicates each day to a different group (religious, professionals, parents, children, the poor, the imprisoned, the military, the enslaved, the infirm).

## Fidelity note
Ember's St. Benedict novena does not reproduce either source's prayer text — neither FishEaters' single traditional invocation nor PMN's Rule-based daily prayer. Structurally Ember diverges from both: it uses nine distinct meditation-plus-intention days, where FishEaters repeats one prayer and PMN repeats one prayer with rotating beneficiaries. The content is faithful to Benedict's authentic spirituality and well-grounded (Subiaco, Ora et Labora, the twelve steps of humility, stability, hospitality, the medal and "Crux sacra sit mihi lux," Pax, a holy death), and Day 3's "prefer nothing whatever to Christ" and the closing echo the Rule much as PMN does. So it is themically faithful and draws on the same Benedictine sources, but it is an original composition rather than a transcription of either page.
