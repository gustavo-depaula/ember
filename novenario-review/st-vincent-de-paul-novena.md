# St. Vincent de Paul — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** St. Vincent de Paul, father of the poor and apostle of charity, you who recognized Christ in the least of His brethren, turn your fatherly heart toward me. Teach me to love God in deeds and to serve Him in those who suffer. Amen.

**Day 1 — Christ in the poor:** St. Vincent taught that in serving the poor we serve Jesus Himself, who hides beneath their rags and wounds. He bade his followers to turn the medal and see the suffering Christ in every beggar's face.  _(Intention: For the grace to recognize Christ in every person in need.)_

**Day 2 — The poor are our masters:** Vincent called the poor his lords and masters, owed not condescension but reverence and prompt service. He saw their dignity so clearly that he would beg pardon of them if he served them poorly.  _(Intention: For the grace to serve the needy with humility and respect.)_

**Day 3 — Conversion of heart:** Vincent's youth was marked by ambition for a comfortable benefice, until grace turned his eyes outward to the souls and bodies of the abandoned. His own conversion teaches that God can redirect any life to His purposes.  _(Intention: For the grace of a true conversion away from selfish comfort.)_

**Day 4 — Humility:** Vincent considered humility the foundation of all Gospel charity, calling himself nothing and ascribing every good to God alone. He warned that pride spoils even the best works done for the poor.  _(Intention: For the grace of humility that gives all glory to God.)_

**Day 5 — The Daughters of Charity:** With St. Louise de Marillac, Vincent founded the Daughters of Charity, whose cloister was the streets and whose chapel was the parish church. He sent them out to nurse the sick poor in their own homes when no one else would.  _(Intention: For all consecrated to the service of the sick and the poor.)_

**Day 6 — Care for abandoned children:** Vincent gathered the foundlings of Paris, left to die in doorways, and pleaded for their rescue as though for his own children. He reminded benefactors that to let these little ones perish would be to kill them.  _(Intention: For the protection of abandoned, unborn, and forgotten children.)_

**Day 7 — Zeal for priests:** Vincent founded the Congregation of the Mission to evangelize the rural poor and to form holy priests through retreats and seminaries. He knew that good shepherds were the surest gift he could give to a hungry flock.  _(Intention: For holy and zealous priests devoted to the poor.)_

**Day 8 — Patience in adversity:** Through wars, plagues, famine, and his own painful illnesses, Vincent labored on without bitterness, trusting Providence at every turn. He counseled that good works often ripen slowly, and that patience is itself a charity.  _(Intention: For patient trust in God amid trials and delays.)_

**Day 9 — Charity that endures:** To his last days Vincent gave himself to the works of mercy, leaving behind not riches but a living charity that still serves the poor across the world. He shows that love spent on others is the one treasure death cannot take.  _(Intention: For the grace to spend my life in lasting works of love.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. Vincent de Paul, who never turned away from a soul in need, present my petition to God who is rich in mercy. Obtain for me the grace I ask, and a heart enlarged to share His love with the poor. Amen.

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
A 9-day novena. Each day: Sign of the Cross; a thanksgiving prayer "Dear Lord, we thank You for giving us Your priest, St. Vincent de Paul, as an example of holiness"; an address to St. Vincent with a day-specific meditation on one of his virtues (openness to God's will, charity, organizational leadership, self-mastery, etc.); a personal-intention slot ("(mention your intentions here)"); "St. Vincent de Paul, pray for us!"; closing Sign of the Cross. The daily meditations trace his life arc: initial reluctance → spiritual awakening → charitable works → personal transformation.

## Fidelity note
Ember's version is an original composition. It shares the standard one-frame-plus-nine-meditations form with PMN and overlaps on the core Vincentian themes both sources naturally cover — charity to the poor, his founding of the Congregation of the Mission and (with St. Louise de Marillac) the Daughters of Charity, his conversion from a comfort-seeking youth, and humility. Ember's content is fuller and more specific than PMN's summary suggests, adding the "turn the medal" / Christ-in-the-poor saying, "the poor are our masters," and the Paris foundlings. Historically accurate and thematically aligned, but the wording is Ember's own rather than drawn from the source.
