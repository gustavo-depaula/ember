# Novena to St. Louis IX, King of France — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O St. Louis, holy king and faithful father, you wore an earthly crown without losing sight of the heavenly one. Teach me to serve God in the duties of my state, to love justice, and to seek first the Kingdom that does not pass away. Amen.

**Day 1 — Formed in the faith:** Louis often repeated his mother's words that she would rather see him dead than guilty of a single mortal sin. He shows that holiness is sown in childhood and that a parent's faith shapes a life.  _(Intention: For families, that they may raise their children in the love of God.)_

**Day 2 — The dignity of baptism:** Louis loved best the parish church where he was baptized, saying he prized that place above his coronation, for there he was made a child of God. He teaches us that our deepest identity is found not in rank but in grace.  _(Intention: For the grace to live worthily of my baptismal calling.)_

**Day 3 — Justice for all:** Louis sat beneath an oak at Vincennes to hear the pleas of the humblest subjects, refusing to let power excuse injustice. He teaches that authority is a trust to be exercised for the good of others, never for oneself.  _(Intention: For all who hold authority, that they may govern with justice.)_

**Day 4 — Love of the poor:** The king fed hundreds of the poor daily, served them with his own hands, and even tended lepers whom others shunned. He shows that the greatness of a soul is measured by its tenderness toward the least.  _(Intention: For generosity and compassion toward the poor and suffering.)_

**Day 5 — Penance and self-mastery:** Beneath his royal robes Louis wore a hair shirt and practiced fasting, ruling his own passions before he ruled a kingdom. He teaches that no one governs others well who has not first learned to govern himself.  _(Intention: For self-discipline and the spirit of penance in my daily life.)_

**Day 6 — Honoring the Passion of Christ:** Louis received the Crown of Thorns walking barefoot in penance, and built the radiant Sainte-Chapelle as a worthy shrine for the relics of the Passion. He teaches us to reverence the sufferings by which Christ redeemed us.  _(Intention: For a deeper love of the Cross and Passion of Our Lord.)_

**Day 7 — A devoted husband and father:** Louis was a faithful husband to Queen Margaret and a careful father who wrote his son a famous letter urging him above all to love God and shun sin. He shows that holiness begins within the home and its daily duties.  _(Intention: For holiness lived faithfully in the duties of family and work.)_

**Day 8 — Courage in the Crusade:** Louis bore captivity, defeat, and disease on Crusade with unshaken faith, refusing to abandon his men or his trust in God. He teaches us to persevere with courage when our best efforts seem to fail.  _(Intention: For perseverance and trust in God amid failure and hardship.)_

**Day 9 — Faithful unto death:** Dying of plague near Tunis, Louis asked to be laid on a bed of ashes and breathed his last with the words, 'Father, into your hands.' He invites us to make our whole life a preparation for that final surrender to God.  _(Intention: For the grace to surrender my life to God at the hour of death.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. Louis, who counted your baptism a greater honor than your throne, intercede for me before the King of kings whom you served so faithfully. Obtain the grace I seek in this novena, and a heart that rules its own desires for the love of God. Amen.

_(A pt-BR version also exists in the app.)_

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
PrayMoreNovenas hosts a St. Louis King of France novena. Each day is bracketed by the Sign of the Cross and follows a fixed template: thanksgiving to God for Louis as an example, an address to him recalling a specific virtue or event, the request "Please bring my petitions before God…", a "Pray for me, that I may…" petition, a space for intentions ("Please also pray for…"), and the refrain "St. Louis King of France, pray for us!" Its daily themes run: (1) upbringing in faith, (2) using temporal power to glorify God, (3) marriage and fatherhood, (4) promoting peace as arbiter, (5) judicial reform and justice, (6) penitential practices, (7) charity toward the poor and sick, (8) reverence for holy things, (9) overall devotion to God and Church.

## Fidelity note
Ember and PMN draw on the same biography and overlap strongly on themes — formation in the faith, justice, charity to the poor, penance, family life, reverence for holy things — though Ember orders and frames them differently and adds vivid, accurate detail (Blanche of Castile's words, prizing baptism over the coronation, the oak at Vincennes, the hair shirt, the barefoot reception of the Crown of Thorns and the Sainte-Chapelle, the letter to his son, the "Father, into your hands" death). PMN's "promoting peace as arbiter" day has no direct Ember counterpart, and Ember's Day 9 deathbed scene is fuller. The wording is wholly Ember's own. A faithful, well-sourced original composition that parallels rather than copies the available source.
