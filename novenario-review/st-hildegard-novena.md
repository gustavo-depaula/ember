# St. Hildegard of Bingen — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O God, living Light who shone within Your servant Hildegard, kindle in me the wisdom and wonder she received. Through her intercession, open my eyes to behold Your glory in all creation and grant me the grace I now humbly seek. Amen.

**Day 1 — The living Light:** Hildegard described her visions as a 'reflection of the living Light' that she perceived not with bodily eyes but in her soul, awake and aware. She teaches us that God speaks within the heart that is still enough to listen.  _(Intention: For the grace to perceive God's light within the soul.)_

**Day 2 — Courage to speak God's word:** Though she called herself a 'poor little figure of a woman,' Hildegard preached publicly and reproved bishops, popes, and the emperor when truth required it. She shows us that humility and bold witness can dwell in the same heart.  _(Intention: For the courage to speak the truth with humility and love.)_

**Day 3 — Viriditas, the greening power:** Hildegard saw all creation charged with 'viriditas,' the green, life-giving freshness of the Holy Spirit that makes meadows bloom and souls flourish. She teaches us that grace is not dry duty but a sap that brings the whole person to life.  _(Intention: For renewal where our spirit has grown dry and weary.)_

**Day 4 — Wonder at creation:** Hildegard studied plants, stones, and the workings of the body, seeing the whole cosmos as a harmony reflecting its Maker. She reminds us that to know creation rightly is to be led in praise toward God.  _(Intention: For eyes to find the Creator's hand in the world around us.)_

**Day 5 — The music of heaven:** Hildegard composed soaring chants and a sung morality play, holding that music echoes the harmony Adam knew before the fall. She teaches us that beauty is not a luxury but a path by which the soul rises to God.  _(Intention: For all artists and musicians who serve God through beauty.)_

**Day 6 — Healer of body and soul:** Hildegard tended the sick of her abbey and wrote on remedies, viewing health of body and soul as bound together in God's design. She shows us a compassion that cares for the whole person, never the spirit alone.  _(Intention: For the sick, and for all who care for them.)_

**Day 7 — Faithful obedience to the Church:** Hildegard withheld her visions until they were approved by her superiors and the Pope, submitting her gift to the Church's judgment. She teaches us that genuine inspiration humbly seeks the discernment of the Body of Christ.  _(Intention: For docility to the Church in the use of our gifts.)_

**Day 8 — A woman of wisdom:** Kings, abbots, and ordinary souls sought Hildegard's counsel, for her wisdom flowed from a life steeped in prayer. She reminds us that true wisdom is born not of cleverness but of long friendship with God.  _(Intention: For wisdom to guide our decisions and counsel others well.)_

**Day 9 — Doctor of the Church:** More than eight centuries after her death, Hildegard was named a Doctor of the Church, her hidden cloistered voice still teaching the faithful. She shows us that a life given wholly to God bears fruit far beyond its own age.  _(Intention: For the grace to give our whole life to God, whatever its hiddenness.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. Hildegard, Sibyl of the Rhine and Doctor of the Church, who heard the music of heaven and saw the greening power of the Spirit, intercede for me. Make my soul green again with the freshness of grace, that I may bloom and bear fruit for God. Amen.

_(A pt-BR version also exists in the app.)_

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
A 9-day novena. Each day: Sign of the Cross; an introductory paragraph thanking God for St. Hildegard "as an example of holiness"; a meditation on an aspect of her life; a petition with a daily intention; an intercessory prayer; a personal-intention slot ("And we especially ask in this novena for (mention your intentions here)"); "St. Hildegard of Bingen, pray for us!"; closing Sign of the Cross. The nine daily intentions are mostly petitions for groups/states: children; deeper friendship with God; vocations; holiness of nuns; students; theologians; growth in charity; teachers; religious superiors.

## Fidelity note
Ember's version is an original composition. It and PMN both follow the standard one-frame-plus-nine-meditations pattern and both draw on Hildegard's real life (visions/"living Light," obedience to the Church, learning and wisdom), so the genre and several themes overlap. The content differs in emphasis: Ember foregrounds Hildegard's distinctive theology — "viriditas / the greening power," her music, her cosmic wonder at creation, and the "Sibyl of the Rhine" / Doctor-of-the-Church titles — and ties the closing prayer to the "green again" motif, whereas PMN's daily intentions are generic group petitions. Accurate and well-researched, but textually independent of the source.
