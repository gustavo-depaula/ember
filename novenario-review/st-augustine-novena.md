# St. Augustine — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O God who made the heart for Yourself, hear me through the intercession of St. Augustine, who sought You long and far before he found You within. Grant me the courage of his conversion and the fire of his love, that my restless heart may come to rest in You. Amen.

**Day 1 — The restless heart:** Augustine confessed that he chased pleasures, honors, and false philosophies, yet found no rest in any of them. He teaches us that every restlessness is secretly a hunger for God, who alone can fill the heart He made.  _(Intention: For all who feel an emptiness no created thing can fill.)_

**Day 2 — Honesty about sin:** In the Confessions Augustine laid bare his sins with searching honesty, even the boyish theft of pears done only for the thrill of doing wrong. He shows us that conversion begins when we stop excusing ourselves before God.  _(Intention: For the grace of an honest and contrite confession.)_

**Day 3 — Seeking truth with the mind:** Augustine wrestled for years with the Manichees and the philosophers before the truth of Christ overcame him. He reminds us that faith does not fear reason, and the searching intellect is itself a gift drawing us toward God.  _(Intention: For all who seek God through study and honest questioning.)_

**Day 4 — Take up and read:** Weeping in a Milan garden, Augustine heard a child's voice chanting 'Take up and read,' opened St. Paul, and was finally freed from his old self. He teaches us that grace can break through in a single moment after years of resistance.  _(Intention: For the grace to recognize and obey God's call when it comes.)_

**Day 5 — The power of a mother's prayer:** Augustine owed his conversion in great part to the tears and prayers of his mother Monica, who never gave up on him. He shows us that no one is saved alone, and intercession is a true labor of love.  _(Intention: In thanksgiving for those who have prayed us toward God.)_

**Day 6 — Late have I loved You:** Augustine grieved that he had loved God so late, having sought His beauty everywhere but within. He teaches us that it is never too late to begin, and that God has been near all along, waiting to be found.  _(Intention: For all who think themselves too far gone to return to God.)_

**Day 7 — Shepherd of his people:** As bishop of Hippo, Augustine preached, judged disputes, and fed the poor for thirty-five years, spending himself for his flock. He reminds us that holiness is proven not in feeling but in the daily service of others.  _(Intention: For bishops, priests, and all who shepherd God's people.)_

**Day 8 — Defender of grace:** Against those who claimed man could save himself by his own strength, Augustine taught that every good begins in the free gift of God's grace. He shows us to glory not in our own merit but in the mercy that lifts us.  _(Intention: For the humility to depend wholly on God's grace.)_

**Day 9 — Resting in God at last:** Augustine died as the Vandals besieged his city, the penitential psalms pinned to his wall, his heart finally at rest in the God he had so long sought. He teaches us that the whole of life is a homeward journey into the peace of God.  _(Intention: For the grace of final perseverance and a holy death.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. Augustine, who turned from the love of the world to the love of God, obtain for me the grace I now implore and a heart wholly given to the Truth. Late may I have loved You, O Beauty ever ancient and ever new, but let me love You without end. Amen.

_(A pt-BR version also exists in the app.)_

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
A 9-day novena. Each day opens with the Sign of the Cross, then an identical core prayer — "O holy Saint Augustine, who has famously declared that 'Our hearts were made for you, O Lord, and they are restless until they rest in you'" — asking for wisdom, courage, and relief from burdens. Each day adds a unique daily meditation/intention, an invitation to "State the nature of your problem or the special favor you seek," then Our Father, Hail Mary, Glory Be, "St. Augustine, Pray For Us!", and a closing Sign of the Cross.

## Fidelity note
Ember's text is an original composition, but it is the closest in spirit of the set: it shares PMN's signature framing device — Augustine's "restless heart" (Confessions I.1) — building the entire opening prayer around it, and echoes the "Late have I loved You, O Beauty ever ancient and ever new" line in both Day 6 and the closing prayer. Structurally Ember matches PMN's pattern of one fixed prayer plus nine distinct daily meditations, though all wording is Ember's own and the nine meditations follow a tighter biographical arc than PMN's looser daily themes.
