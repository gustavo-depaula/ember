# Novena to St. Ignatius of Loyola — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US

**Opening prayer:** Glorious St. Ignatius, who once sought the glory of earthly kings and then gave your whole life to the King of Heaven, obtain for me a generous heart. Teach me to desire nothing but the greater glory of God and the salvation of souls. Amen.

**Day 1 — From soldier to saint:** A cannonball at Pamplona ended Ignatius's dreams of military glory and began God's work in his soul. He teaches us that God can turn our greatest defeats into the doorway of grace. _(Intention: For the grace to find God's call hidden within my failures and wounds.)_

**Day 2 — Holy reading:** Confined to his sickbed, Ignatius read the life of Christ and the saints, and felt a lasting peace that worldly daydreams never gave him. He shows us how the right books can quietly reshape a soul. _(Intention: For a love of spiritual reading that draws my heart toward Christ.)_

**Day 3 — Discernment of spirits:** Ignatius learned to notice the movements of his heart, distinguishing the consolation that comes from God from the false promises of the enemy. He teaches us to read our inner life with honesty and prayer. _(Intention: For the wisdom to discern God's voice amid the many voices within me.)_

**Day 4 — The Spiritual Exercises:** At Manresa, Ignatius composed the Exercises to lead souls to know, love, and follow Christ more closely. He reminds us that the soul, like the body, must be trained through disciplined prayer. _(Intention: For the discipline to give myself regularly to serious prayer.)_

**Day 5 — The First Principle and Foundation:** Ignatius taught that we are created to praise, reverence, and serve God, and that all else is given to help us toward that end. He frees us by teaching holy indifference to everything but God's will. _(Intention: For freedom of heart to desire only what leads me to God.)_

**Day 6 — For the greater glory of God:** Ad maiorem Dei gloriam was the motto by which Ignatius measured every choice and labor. He challenges us never to settle for the merely good, but to seek the greater good for God. _(Intention: For the desire to do all things for the greater glory of God.)_

**Day 7 — Finding God in all things:** Ignatius saw the Creator present and laboring in every creature and circumstance of his day. He teaches us to meet God not only in church but in our work, our duties, and the people before us. _(Intention: For the grace to recognize God present in every moment of my day.)_

**Day 8 — Generous service:** Ignatius prayed to give and not to count the cost, to labor and not to seek reward except that of knowing he did God's will. His generosity rebukes every half-hearted following of Christ. _(Intention: For a generous heart that serves God without counting the cost.)_

**Day 9 — The Suscipe — total surrender:** At the summit of the Exercises, Ignatius surrenders his memory, understanding, and will, asking only for God's love and grace. He shows us that the highest prayer is to give God everything we are. _(Intention: For the grace to surrender my whole life into the hands of God.)_

(Each day the user also prays one Our Father, one Hail Mary, and one Glory Be.)

**Closing prayer:** St. Ignatius, faithful soldier of Christ, help me to set the world rightly in my heart, choosing always what leads me closer to God. With you I pray: take, Lord, all that I have, for You have given it; give me only Your love and Your grace, and that is enough for me. Amen.

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
PMN's St. Ignatius of Loyola novena is nine distinct days framed by the Sign of the Cross, each with a day-specific theme, petition, and space for personal intentions, closing "St. Ignatius of Loyola, pray for us!" Day themes: (1) deeper conversion ("help me fall deeper in love with Christ"), (2) discernment of God's voice amid the world's noise, (3) evangelization and zeal for souls, (4) the Spiritual Exercises and discipline in prayer, (5) learning and intellectual growth / wisdom, (6) humility and obedience, servant leadership, (7) divine providence and surrendering fears, (8) total self-offering ("Take, Lord, and receive"), (9) the greater glory of God (Ad Majorem Dei Gloriam).

## Fidelity note
Ember and PMN match closely in structure (nine themed days, each with a meditation and intention, framed by the Sign of the Cross) and in several core Ignatian themes that both hit: conversion/the cannonball, discernment, the Spiritual Exercises, providence/surrender, the Suscipe ("Take, Lord, and receive" / Ember's closing "take, Lord, all that I have... give me only Your love and Your grace"), and the AMDG motto on the climactic day. Ember's day arc is more explicitly tied to the Ignatian corpus — adding "holy reading," the First Principle and Foundation, "finding God in all things," and the Suscipe as the ninth day's centerpiece — where PMN substitutes more general themes (evangelization, learning, humility). Wording is Ember's own; the closing paraphrases the Suscipe rather than quoting any PMN line. Faithful and richly Ignatian; an original composition in the PMN format that, if anything, leans harder into Ignatius's own writings.
