# Novena to St. Clare of Assisi — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O glorious St. Clare, mirror of holy poverty and bride of Christ, you left all things to follow the poor and crucified Lord. Draw my heart away from the passing things that weigh it down, and teach me to gaze, as you did, upon the face of Jesus. Amen.

**Day 1 — Leaving all for Christ:** On Palm Sunday night, Clare slipped from her noble home through a sealed door and let Francis cut her hair, exchanging silk for a rough habit. She shows us that following Christ begins with a decisive break from whatever holds us back.  _(Intention: For the courage to renounce whatever keeps me from belonging fully to God.)_

**Day 2 — Love of Lady Poverty:** Clare so loved poverty that she begged the Pope for the 'privilege of poverty,' the right to own nothing at all. She teaches that the soul empty of possessions has room for the riches of God alone.  _(Intention: For freedom from greed and a spirit content with God alone.)_

**Day 3 — The contemplative gaze:** In her letters to Agnes of Prague, Clare urged her to gaze upon Christ, consider Him, contemplate Him, and desire to imitate Him. Her whole spirituality flows from this loving look fixed upon the Crucified.  _(Intention: For the grace to fix my eyes on Jesus amid the noise of the day.)_

**Day 4 — Devotion to the Eucharist:** When Saracen soldiers scaled the walls of San Damiano, Clare, though gravely ill, rose and carried the monstrance to the threshold, and the attackers fled. She trusted that her true defense was the living presence of her Lord.  _(Intention: For deeper reverence and trust before the Blessed Sacrament.)_

**Day 5 — A mother to her sisters:** As abbess Clare washed the feet of her sisters and served the sick with her own hands, ruling not by command but by example. She teaches that authority in the Church is the willingness to stoop and serve.  _(Intention: For a humble, serving heart toward those entrusted to me.)_

**Day 6 — Patience in long suffering:** For twenty-eight years Clare bore illness without complaint, often unable to rise, yet still spinning cloth for altar linens from her bed. She shows that a hidden, patient suffering offered in love bears more fruit than great deeds.  _(Intention: For patience to bear my own crosses in union with Christ.)_

**Day 7 — Defending the Rule:** Two days before her death, Clare received papal approval of her own Rule, the first written by a woman for women. Her steadfastness teaches us to guard with holy firmness the gift God has given us to keep.  _(Intention: For fidelity to my vocation and the duties of my state in life.)_

**Day 8 — Joy in simplicity:** Though her life was austere, Clare radiated a deep gladness, calling herself the 'little plant' of Francis and rejoicing in the smallest gifts of God. She teaches that true joy is not found in abundance but in gratitude.  _(Intention: For a grateful heart that rejoices in God's small daily mercies.)_

**Day 9 — A holy death:** As she lay dying Clare was heard to bless her own soul, saying, 'Go forth in peace, for you have followed the good road.' She invites us to so live that death may be only the threshold of the vision we have longed for.  _(Intention: For the grace of a peaceful death in the friendship of Christ.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. Clare, who held the Blessed Sacrament before the enemy and saw your sisters delivered, hold me before your Lord and shield me in every danger of soul and body. Obtain for me the grace I now implore, and a heart wholly given to Christ as yours was. Amen.

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
PrayMoreNovenas hosts a St. Clare novena. Each day opens with the Sign of the Cross and a repeated address to the Trinity: "Dearest Holy Trinity, Father, Son and Holy Spirit. We praise you and the fruits of your grace that manifested themselves in your poor and gracious servant, Saint Clare." Each day then adds a short reflection on a distinct virtue — its themes run virtue, poverty, light, happiness, contemplation, peace, fearlessness, love, and prayerfulness — with a space for intentions and the refrain "Saint Clare, Pray for us! That we may be worthy of the promises of Christ," closed by the Sign of the Cross.

## Fidelity note
Both are nine-day, virtue-per-day novenas, and the two overlap on real Clare themes — poverty, contemplative gazing on Christ, fearlessness before the Saracens, joy/happiness — but Ember's prose and selection are its own. Ember's opening directs its prayer to Clare herself rather than PMN's address to the Trinity, and Ember anchors several days in concrete biography (the Palm Sunday flight, the letters to Agnes of Prague, the monstrance at San Damiano, the twenty-eight years of illness, the deathbed words) that PMN states more abstractly. It is a faithful, biographically accurate original composition that parallels the source's structure without copying its wording.
