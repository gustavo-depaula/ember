# Novena to St. John of the Cross — fidelity comparison

## Ember (this app) — en-US
**Opening prayer:** O glorious St. John of the Cross, Mystical Doctor, who loved Christ crucified above every consolation, come to my aid as I begin this novena. Teach me to seek God alone, and obtain for me the grace I now ask through your powerful intercession. Amen.

**Day 1 — Seeking God alone:** From his youth John desired one thing only: to belong wholly to God. He teaches us that the soul finds peace not in many goods but in the single-hearted pursuit of the Lord above all things.  _(Intention: For the grace to desire God above every created thing.)_

**Day 2 — The reform of Carmel:** Joined to St. Teresa of Ávila, John embraced a poorer, more silent, more prayerful Carmel, even at great personal cost. He shows us that true renewal begins with our own conversion and willingness to suffer for what is good.  _(Intention: For the courage to reform my own life before judging others.)_

**Day 3 — Joy in suffering:** Imprisoned and mistreated by his own brethren, John did not grow bitter but turned his suffering into song. He teaches us that the cross embraced in love becomes the very door through which God enters the soul.  _(Intention: For patience and love in the midst of injustice and pain.)_

**Day 4 — The dark night of the soul:** John gave the Church the great teaching of the dark night, when God withdraws felt consolation to purify the soul and deepen its faith. He assures us that the darkness is not absence but the secret nearness of a God who is leading us home.  _(Intention: For faith that holds fast to God when consolation is withdrawn.)_

**Day 5 — Nada — detachment from all:** On his sketch of Mount Carmel John wrote 'nada, nada, nada' — nothing, nothing, nothing — to teach that the way to the All is to be free of attachment to every lesser thing. His detachment was not contempt for creatures but a heart kept clear for God.  _(Intention: For freedom from disordered attachments that hold me back.)_

**Day 6 — The Spiritual Canticle:** In the Spiritual Canticle John sang of the soul searching for her Beloved like the bride of the Song of Songs. He reveals that prayer is ultimately a love story, the longing of the heart for the One who first loved us.  _(Intention: For a heart that longs for God as the bride longs for her beloved.)_

**Day 7 — The living flame of love:** John described the soul transformed by God as a log set ablaze, no longer merely burning but become flame itself. He calls us to let the fire of the Holy Spirit consume what is selfish until we are wholly love.  _(Intention: For the fire of divine love to transform my whole heart.)_

**Day 8 — Put love where there is no love:** John counseled that where we find no love, we should put love, and there we will find love. He teaches that we are not to wait for others to deserve our charity, but to become a source of the love we hope to receive.  _(Intention: For grace to bring love into the places where it is lacking.)_

**Day 9 — In the evening, examined on love:** John taught that in the evening of life we will be examined on love alone. He invites us to make every day a preparation for that final reckoning, learning now to love as we hope to be found loving then.  _(Intention: For the grace to make love the measure of all my days.)_

**Closing prayer:** St. John of the Cross, who passed through the dark night and was set ablaze by the living flame of love, lead me along the narrow path of faith. Detach my heart from all that is not God, and let me rest at last in Him who is my All. Amen.

## Source — PrayMoreNovenas
Each of the nine days opens "In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen." and closes "St. John of the Cross, pray for us!" with the Trinity invocation. Each day follows a set pattern: thanks to God for St. John as an example; an address to St. John referencing a specific aspect of his life; the petition "Please bring my petitions before God…"; a narrative reflection on a virtue or trial; a prayer request; and space for personal intentions ("Please also pray for (mention your intentions here)"). Daily progression: Days 1–3 — his early holiness, poverty, and priestly vocation; Days 4–6 — his reform work and initial persecution; Days 7–9 — his imprisonment, perseverance, and lasting service.

## Fidelity note
Ember and PrayMoreNovenas share the saint and his core biography — his early single-hearted desire for God, the reform of Carmel with St. Teresa, and his imprisonment/persecution by his own brethren — but Ember's day structure leans heavily into his *writings and doctrine* (the dark night, "nada nada nada" of Mount Carmel, the Spiritual Canticle, the Living Flame, "put love where there is no love," and "in the evening you will be examined on love"), whereas PMN's reflections stay closer to a chronological life narrative. Several of Ember's days quote or paraphrase John's own famous sayings rather than the PMN text, and the opening/closing prayers are Ember's own composition. This is a faithful, theologically rich treatment rooted in the saint's authentic works rather than a port of the PMN prayers.
