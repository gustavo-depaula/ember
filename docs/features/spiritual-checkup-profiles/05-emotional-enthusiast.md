# 05 — The Emotional Enthusiast

> **Primary archetype:** Discipulus (on prayer axis)
> **Secondary (tension pair):** `{ Quaerens — "I realize I don't actually know what the Church teaches", Proficiens — "I want to move from feeling to steadiness" }`
> **Overlays possible:** scrupulosity (rare), convert (common — often from charismatic Protestantism), traditional-leaning (rare), *charismatic context* (extremely common; see note)

## Who this is

A soul who **prays a lot and with genuine warmth** — often through music, charismatic forms, emotional Eucharistic devotion, tearful Adoration, felt experiences of God. Attendance at Mass is regular, sometimes fervent. Has pictures of saints on her phone, probably journals, probably has experienced something she calls a conversion.

And whose **doctrinal and moral formation is thin**. The Creed is known as a prayer (recited at Mass) but not as a set of truths studied. The distinction between mortal and venial sin is blurry. The sacraments are loved in their sensory reality but not well understood in their form. She has little exposure to Catholic thought older than c. 1970 and no familiarity with the tradition's language of asceticism.

This profile is the second reason the v1 linear scoring broke. On the questionnaire, she selects "daily prayer," "consolation in prayer," "openness to silent prayer" — and scores high on Discipulus or Proficiens, receiving a track aimed at deepening what she has, when the actual pastoral need is **grounding** what she has.

Signals: "I feel so close to Jesus when I pray" — "I cry at Adoration" — "I don't really understand what [mortal sin / transubstantiation / the Magisterium] is but I love the Church."

## Route through the tree

L1=D ("I practice regularly") → L2 branches on prayer life description. This profile is reached specifically by **"I pray a lot, sometimes intensely, but don't have a structure."**

L3 skipped. L4 tension pair picks the secondary.

## Starting tension

Emotional enthusiasts read their *consolations* as the measure of their prayer life. When the consolation fades — which it will, because consolation is a gift and not a state — they conclude something is wrong with them, or with the parish, or with the Church. They shop for parishes. They drift toward whichever form gives the most felt experience. This is not mere shallowness; it is the lack of a formation grammar that *names* what consolation is and is not.

The correction is not to cool the affect — affective prayer is a real gift and the apologetics nerd (profile 04) desperately needs it. The correction is to **pour catechetical and doctrinal content into the space where felt experience alone was load-bearing**. Not to replace warmth; to ground it.

## What to offer first

- **Her daily prayer, unchanged.** Do not modify what is working. If she prays the Rosary with intensity, continue. If she has a daily Adoration habit, continue.
- **A catechetical weekly reading.** Week 1: `base/chapters/seven-sacraments`. Week 2: `base/chapters/what-the-mass-is`. These chapters ground emotional devotion in sacramental theology without shaming the affect.
- **A short scripture passage in lectio form daily** — slowing the prayer down, connecting the felt experience to the Word.
- **`base/chapters/how-to-make-a-good-confession` in week 1** and confession by week 2. The enthusiast's moral life often has blind spots (sexual morality, gossip, emotional attachments) hidden under the cover of felt fervor. Confession exposes them gently.
- **One chapter from `ten-commandments` or `beatitudes` by week 3.** Framed as *the map of love*, not as law.

## What NOT to offer first

- **Not an ascetical starter track.** Fasting schedules, aggressive examen, Jesuit rigor, *Ascent of Mount Carmel* — all will feel cold and alien, and may provoke a retreat to the charismatic community that "gets her."
- **Not contemplative prayer as a technique.** She will turn it into another emotional occasion; silence will become another kind of consolation-seeking.
- **Not apologetic refutations of her background** (if from charismatic Protestantism or a sentimental Catholic parish). Affirm the grace received; add what was missing.
- **Not correction of taste.** Her hymn preferences, her devotional art, her prayer style — these are not doctrine. Correcting them in week 1 alienates her before the formation has taken root.
- **Not excessive mental prayer.** She will perform it emotionally. Start her with silence *short* (5 min, not 20), and pair it with a text.

## Two-week shape

**Week 1 — "naming what you already have":**
- Continue her current prayer rhythm.
- Add: 5 minutes of lectio on a brief Gospel passage (e.g., Luke 1:26–38).
- Chapter: `seven-sacraments`.
- Read: `how-to-make-a-good-confession`.
- Sunday Mass (regular).

**Week 2 — "the Mass you love, understood":**
- Same rhythm.
- Chapter: `what-the-mass-is`.
- **Confession** (especially if it has been >6 months).
- Introduce the morning offering as a fixed frame for the day's emotions.

**Month 2+:**
- Chapter: `ten-commandments` or `beatitudes`.
- Introduce one short, non-sensory practice: the Angelus at noon (it is text, not feeling).
- Consider spiritual direction. Specifically: *not* someone from her current community; someone older, steadier — ideally a priest in a parish with a formative catechetical culture.

## Pastoral warnings

- **The "desolation = something wrong" pattern.** When the enthusiast hits her first stretch of dryness (and she will), her instinct is to treat it as a problem. Week-1 copy must address this in advance: *St. Ignatius's discernment of spirits names desolation as something normal and even useful*. Not a flaw, not a failure, not a sign that the "real" prayer life is happening elsewhere.
- **The parish-shopping pattern.** Enthusiasts whose home parish does not match the emotional register of their prayer often rotate parishes looking for the "right" one. Suggest *staying put* for one liturgical season (Advent, Lent) even when the parish doesn't feel warm — and pray for the parish rather than about it.
- **The subjective-authority pattern.** Having had a felt experience of God, enthusiasts sometimes treat subsequent felt impressions as directive ("I feel the Spirit is telling me to..."). This is a specific trap. The antidote is not doctrinal browbeating; it is the *order* of Christian discernment — scripture, magisterium, spiritual director, interior impression — with the first three weighted far more heavily than the last. Name this in month 2.
- **The charismatic context.** Many in this profile attend or come from charismatic prayer groups. These are legitimate expressions of lay piety and can be deeply Catholic. The checkup should not pit the enthusiast against her community. It should give her more ground to stand on within it.
