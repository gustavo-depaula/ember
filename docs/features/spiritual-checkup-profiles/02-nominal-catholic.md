# 02 — The Nominal Catholic

> **Primary archetype:** Quaerens (early) or Redivivus (if any memory of practice)
> **Secondary (tension pair):** `{ Quaerens — "I never really knew what the Church teaches", Redivivus — "I knew some of this as a child, it just never stuck" }`
> **Overlays possible:** scrupulosity, traditional-leaning (rare at this stage), convert (no)

## Who this is

A soul who was **baptized as an infant**, may have been confirmed in adolescence under family pressure, and has not meaningfully practiced since. Unlike the lapsed returner (profile 03), they were never really *formed* to begin with — they have nothing robust to return to. Unlike the curious unbaptized (profile 01), they carry a real sacramental identity and a real moral memory.

Signals: "I'm technically Catholic but..." — "my grandmother was devout" — "I was confirmed but nothing ever clicked" — "I have a childhood memory of a First Communion." Often appears in early-mid adulthood at a hinge point: marriage, a child, a death, or a crisis of meaning that sends them back to what they inherited but never opened.

## Route through the tree

L1=B ("baptized but never really formed") → L2 asks about catechetical knowledge.

- "Not really, and I'd like to learn." → this profile, **catechetical emphasis**.
- "Yes, from childhood/RCIA, but I never practiced." → this profile, **devotional emphasis** (the missing piece is practice, not content).

L3 skipped. L4 (tension pair) picks the secondary.

## Starting tension

Nominal Catholics routinely **overestimate what they know** because the cultural residue of catechesis creates a false floor ("of course I know the Ten Commandments"), while the actual content has often evaporated. They also **underestimate their sacramental identity** — baptism is real, present, and grace-bearing, and they often relate to it as if it were a certificate rather than a bond.

The first move is to honor the baptism (you are already Christ's) and to rebuild catechetical foundations from scratch *without* the condescension of beginning at "there is a God." Begin at the Creed.

## What to offer first

- **The Apostles' Creed, prayed slowly, once a day.** This is what baptism spoke for them. Now they speak it.
- **`base/chapters/seven-sacraments`** — the Catholic life is not a philosophy; it is sacramental. Name the reality.
- **`base/chapters/ten-commandments`** — not as an arrival point, but as moral scaffolding that probably needs rebuilding.
- **`base/chapters/how-to-make-a-good-confession`** — **confession is the portal back to practice**, and likely has not been made since childhood. This is non-negotiable for this profile within the first 4 weeks.
- **Sunday Mass attendance, with a specific parish chosen by the user.** Not daily. Not "whenever you feel like it." Sunday.

## What NOT to offer first

- **Not the full Rosary yet.** Introduce it in week 3. Earlier, it piles up without the Gospels to anchor the mysteries.
- **Not mental prayer / lectio / examen** before confession. The interior life cannot be rebuilt *before* the sacraments return — it gets rebuilt *by* them.
- **Not consecration devotions** (Montfortian, Sacred Heart consecration) — these are for the practicing.
- **Not a "learn everything in the CCC" track.** A nominal Catholic trying to catch up on 2000 years of doctrine all at once is the way to burnout.
- **Not theological debates (Catholic vs Protestant, pre- vs post-Vatican II, Novus Ordo vs TLM).** The interior house is not yet built; these debates accelerate tribal identification and bypass conversion.

## Two-week shape

**Week 1 — "return to the foundations":**
- Morning: Apostles' Creed (from `base/prayers/apostles-creed`), said slowly.
- Evening: Our Father + one verse of the Gospel read contemplatively.
- Chapter: `base/chapters/seven-sacraments`.
- Action: pick a parish, find confession times. Read `base/chapters/how-to-make-a-good-confession`.

**Week 2 — "back to the sacraments":**
- Same morning + evening rhythm.
- Chapter: `base/chapters/ten-commandments` and `base/chapters/returning-to-confession` (yes, both apply even though this is not strictly a "return" — the chapter is written precisely for the long-unprepared).
- Action: **go to confession.** This is the week's central task. If timing doesn't allow, week 3.

At weeks 3–4: introduce the Rosary (just one decade), add a short night examen once the post-confession state of grace is the baseline to examine against.

## Pastoral warnings

- **The "I'm a good person" trap.** Nominal Catholics often carry a moral self-evaluation built outside the sacramental framework. They can be surprised at confession and feel the gap is too wide. The "Returning to Confession" chapter addresses this explicitly — week 2 content must prepare for it.
- **The "my grandmother would be happy" motive.** Conversion to please a deceased relative is not, on its own, conversion. It is a *starting grace* — honor it — but the work is to let it become their own.
- **Spouse or partner alignment.** Many in this profile are returning while in relationships with people who do not share the faith (or who share it at the same nominal level). The starter track must acknowledge this — without making demands that are not properly the app's to make. Point to the priest.
- **Do not accelerate.** If this soul completes week 2 and confesses, the temptation (both theirs and ours) is to give them four more weeks of intensity. Instead: slow down. The gains are fragile. Week 3 should feel easier than week 2, not harder.

## Notes for the chapter author

- A chapter specifically titled "**The Baptism You Already Have**" would be useful for this profile — treating baptism not as a childhood ritual but as an abiding reality.
- A chapter on "**Sunday Mass as the Weekly Anchor**" — practical, not theological — helps anchor the habit.
