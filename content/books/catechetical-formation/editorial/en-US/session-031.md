**Mortal sin is the door slammed on grace.** Today's ekphrasis is theologically exact, and the catechism above is asking the modern reader to recover a distinction that has been quietly dropped from Catholic conversation.

The Church distinguishes between **mortal sin** and **venial sin** with care. The doctrine, refined by Aquinas and codified at Trent, identifies three conditions all of which must be present for a sin to be mortal:

  1. **Grave matter.** The sin involves something *seriously* wrong — murder, adultery, theft of significant amount, deliberately missing Mass on Sunday, blasphemy. The Decalogue (Sessions 035–052) is the standard list. Trivial things are *not* mortal even when sinful.

  2. **Full knowledge.** The sinner knows the act is gravely wrong. Ignorance, when not deliberately cultivated, can lessen culpability. *Invincible ignorance* — genuine inability to know — excuses entirely.

  3. **Full consent.** The sinner's will fully chooses the act. Acts done under compulsion, severe psychological pressure, addictive drives, or partial consent in the heat of strong passion can be less than mortal even when the matter is grave.

When all three are present, the sin **kills the soul's friendship with God** — hence *mortal*, *deadly*. Sanctifying grace is lost. The soul, dying in this state, is irrevocably outside the kingdom (Mt 25:46, 1 Cor 6:9-10).

When any of the three is absent, or when the matter is light, the sin is **venial** — a real wound, a real disorder, but one that does not kill grace. Venial sins, accumulated, harden the soul against the prompting of grace and make mortal sin more likely.

How is mortal sin recovered?

  * **Through perfect contrition** — sorrow for sin from love of God — *plus* the firm intention to confess sacramentally as soon as possible. This restores grace immediately; the sacrament *seals* the reconciliation.

  * **Through sacramental confession with attrition** — sorrow even from a lesser motive (fear of judgment, recognition of disorder). Combined with absolution, this restores grace fully.

The catechism's pastoral message: **the door slammed has a knob; it can be opened from inside, by sorrow.** La Tour's *Magdalene by candlelight* (today's image) is the icon. The skull on the table; the steady flame; the hand resting on the cheek. *She has done it before. So can you.*

What this asks of you:

  * **Take mortal sin seriously, but not anxiously.** It is real and it kills, but the way back is also real and the door is held open by Christ Himself. *Do not stay outside the household.*
  * **If you suspect you may be in mortal sin, go to Confession this week.** Do not receive Communion in the meantime. The catechism is unambiguous; so is St. Paul (1 Cor 11:29).
  * **Cultivate the small habits that prevent mortal sin** — daily examen, frequent confession, the rosary, regular Communion well-received. These are the **fortifications** by which the door stays closed against bigger collapse.

*The flame steady.* It can be relit. The Magdalene knows the way.
