Yesterday's session walked the doctrine of Baptism (Session 062). Today's session takes up the practical questions a parent or godparent will face: **who can baptize, what name to give, when to bring a child to the font, what godparents are for**.

Some of these questions sound legalistic. They are not. They are the Church's tradition of **doing right by the soul of an infant**, and the practical wisdom is rich.

  * **The minister.** The ordinary minister is the priest or deacon. *In case of necessity*, **anyone can baptize** — anyone, including a non-Catholic, including someone with no faith of their own — provided they intend to do what the Church does, and use water with the words *I baptize you in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit*. Pius X is unambiguous. There are children in heaven now because a parent or nurse, in a hospital corridor, did exactly this. **If you are ever near a dying unbaptized child, you may be the one God uses.**

  * **The name.** The Catholic tradition has always given the child the name of a saint, *because the saint becomes the child's lifelong patron and intercessor*. The name is not just identification; it is a *vocation pointed at*. If you are choosing a name for a child you may baptize, choose with this in mind. The child's *namesake* will pray for them all their lives.

  * **When?** *As soon as possible after birth*, the Code of Canon Law specifies (within the first weeks of life, where ordinary). Modern Catholic parents sometimes delay to accommodate scheduling or to "let the child decide later." Both motives miss what Baptism actually is. The infant *cannot* decide later for the very reason that they need Baptism *now* — to enter the family of grace before they can do anything to merit or refuse it. *Suffer the little children to come unto me* (Mark 10:14) — Christ Himself wanted them brought.

  * **Godparents.** Not honorary uncles and aunts. **Spiritual sponsors.** The Church requires at least one godparent to be a fully-initiated, practicing Catholic in good standing — because the godparent's role is to *guarantee, before the Church, that the child will be raised Catholic if the parents fail or die*. Choose them as if you might really die tomorrow. They are insurance against the worst case.

The Bloch image (today's ekphrasis) is theologically exact: *Christ kneels at a child's level, hand on a head.* He came down to where children stand. The sacrament we administer for them is Him doing the same — kneeling to find them, taking them into His own life.
