Yesterday's session named the Decalogue as the *geometry of love*. Today's catechism asks the more concrete question: **why am I bound to keep the commandments at all?**

Aquinas — and the Trent material above following him — give five reasons, each carrying a different kind of weight on the soul. They are worth slowing down on, because the modern Catholic conscience often defaults to *because rules*, and that is the answer that does not survive a hard day.

  * **Because God is God.** This is the first and the deepest. The commandments are not arbitrary tests; they are the *expression of who God is* — the Creator's own description of the order of being He has made. To break them is not to fail an external standard; it is to lie about *what is real*.
  * **Because grace makes them possible.** Pius X yesterday: *the commandments can all be observed, at all times, even in the strongest temptations, by the grace which God never denies.* Today's session adds the grammatical mood: *can*, not *might*. Grace is sufficient. The Christian who pleads *I cannot* has not yet pleaded for the grace.
  * **Because Christ taught them with His own life.** *Sermon on the Mount* is the day's image. The Incarnate Son did not abolish the Decalogue; He intensified it (*you have heard it said... but I say to you*). Christ Himself kept the commandments perfectly *as a man*. His humanity is the proof that grace can do this in a human life.
  * **Because love wants to.** This is Aquinas's deepest move. The Decalogue is not what God demands of someone who does not love Him; it is what *love* itself demands of someone who does. *If you love me, keep my commandments* (John 14:15). Christ frames obedience as the **language of love**, not its substitute.
  * **Because eternity is at stake.** *Heaven and hell, both eternal* — Session 023 already named the gravity. The commandments are not the entrance ticket to heaven (Christ's mercy is); but persistent, unrepented violation of them is the slow self-removal from the kingdom we were made for.

What this asks of you today: when a commandment feels heavy, take the heaviness as a *prompt to ask for grace*, not as evidence the commandment is unreasonable. *Christ on a hillside with open hands* — today's image — is exactly the figure to address. He has the grace. He gives it to whoever asks.
