The Fourth Commandment is the **first** commandment of the second tablet — the first one toward neighbor, and the first neighbor is the one who *gave you life*. The Hebrew word translated *honor* (*kavod*) literally means *weight*. To honor someone is to *give weight* to them — to count them as substantial, to take them seriously, to refuse to treat them lightly.

Aquinas walks the three things parents give: **being** (you exist because of them), **nourishment** (you are alive now because they fed you), and **instruction** (you know how to walk, eat, speak, read because they taught you). The debt is structurally **un-repayable**: you cannot give them being or childhood back. *An offended parent can send the child away*, the Philosopher said; *the child has no such right.* The asymmetry is not unjust. It is the architecture of family.

The Commandment, however, is wider than parents. Pius X is explicit: the duty extends to *whoever has authority over us — our superiors in authority*. Aquinas, citing Romans 13, names the political reach: *there is no authority except from God.* But neither saint imagined this as a blank check for every authority. Aquinas himself elsewhere holds that an unjust law is *no law at all* and that obedience to authority is owed *in matters proper to its competence*, never against the law of God. *We must obey God rather than men* (Acts 5:29) is the Christian reservation that has held since the apostles, and that has produced every saint who refused a tyrant.

So the Commandment binds in three concentric ways:

  * **Toward parents.** Even imperfect parents. Even parents who hurt you. *Honor* does not require pretending the wound was not real — that would be a lie, which the Commandment elsewhere forbids. *Honor* requires *giving them weight* in the truth of who they were and are: the people who gave you being. If the relationship has been damaged, the Christian work is reconciliation if possible, forgiveness if not, intercession always.
  * **Toward elders, teachers, employers, the Church's pastors.** Each in its proper sphere. The doctrine on bishops in particular has not changed: even a bishop whose actions you find disappointing or scandalous is still *the bishop*, and the Christian gives him the weight of his office while praying for him as a sinner.
  * **Toward civil authority.** Pay taxes. Obey just laws. Pray for those in power, including those you did not vote for. *Resist* — meaning conscientious refusal, with the saints — only when the law commands what God forbids.

And the Commandment is also a *vocation* for those who become parents themselves. Aquinas: *parents should give instruction without delay to their children.* Today, give weight where you owe it. If your parents are alive, call.
