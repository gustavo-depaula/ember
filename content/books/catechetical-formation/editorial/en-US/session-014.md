The catechism above takes up the *most consequential paradox in Christian doctrine*: that Jesus Christ is **true God and true man**, in *one Person*, with *two natures*, *unmixed and unseparated*. The Council of Chalcedon (451) gave the formula the Church has held ever since.

Three early heresies the doctrine pushes back against — each surfacing again in modern guises:

  * **Arianism** (4th century, ongoing in unitarian forms): Christ was a great teacher, perhaps the greatest, but *not God*. The Church definitively rejected this at Nicaea (325): *true God from true God, consubstantial with the Father*. Modern mystical-spirituality pieties that treat Jesus as one master among many are, theologically, soft Arianism.

  * **Docetism** (1st-2nd century): Christ only *appeared* to be human; his suffering and death were illusions. The Church, in 1 John itself, rejects: *every spirit which confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh, is of God* (1 John 4:2). Modern spiritualisms that treat Jesus' body as merely a vehicle for spiritual teaching repeat the error.

  * **Nestorianism** (5th century): Christ is two persons in some loose union — a divine Logos joined to a human Jesus. The Church at Ephesus (431) rejected this and gave Mary the title *Theotokos* — *Mother of God* — precisely because *the One she bore is one Person, divine and human in inseparable unity*. To call Mary only *the mother of Christ's humanity* is the Nestorian compromise the Church does not allow.

The catechism's Christology is therefore exact: **Christ is one Person — the eternal Son — possessing two natures, divine and human**. He has a divine intellect and a human intellect; a divine will and a human will (the doctrine of *dyothelitism* defended at the Sixth Council, Constantinople III, in 680–681); a divine love and a human love. Both are real. Neither is mixed with the other. Both are *His*.

Why this matters for the Christian life:

  * **You can pray to Jesus as fully God.** When you say *Lord*, you mean it. He is.
  * **You can confide in Jesus as fully man.** The temptations, the loneliness, the grief at Lazarus's tomb, the dread at Gethsemane — *He has been where you are*. Hebrews 4:15: *we have not a high priest who cannot have compassion on our infirmities, but one tempted in all things like as we are, without sin*. This is doctrine, not metaphor.
  * **The Incarnation has not ended.** *The man Jesus is now where God is, with His scars* (Session 019's ekphrasis). His humanity went up at the Ascension; His humanity is at the right hand of the Father; His humanity returns at the end. We have a *human* in heaven advocating for us. **One of us is there now.**

Today, address Christ both ways: *My God* (because He is). *My Brother* (because He is). The same Jesus is both, in the same prayer.
