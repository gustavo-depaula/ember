The catechism above takes up the **examination of conscience** — the act by which a Christian, before going to confession, takes honest inventory of *what has actually happened in the soul* since the last time. Without it, confession becomes generic and shallow. With it, the sacrament reaches deep.

The *Pharisee and the Publican* (today's image) is exactly the right framing. Both went up to the temple to pray; only one prayed truthfully. The examination of conscience is **the publican's prayer made visible** — *Lord, be merciful to me, a sinner* spoken with specificity. *I lied. I drank too much. I refused that conversation. I let resentment grow. I missed Mass for nothing.* The sins are named. The naming is the door.

The classical method of examination, given its lay-formation shape by Ignatius (*Spiritual Exercises* §43) and Francis de Sales, has three movements:

  1. **Place yourself in God's presence.** *Lord, in Your light let me see what I have done.* The light comes from outside; you do not generate it. Ask for it.

  2. **Walk through your life since the last confession.** Not as a spectator. Not as a judge looking down on yourself. As a *witness* to your own conduct. The traditional structure, recommended for adults, is to walk the **Ten Commandments** in order, asking for each: *have I broken this? in thought, word, deed, or omission?* The Decalogue you have just walked in this book (sessions 035–052) is the natural map.

  3. **Distinguish mortal from venial.** Mortal sin requires *grave matter, full knowledge, and full consent*. Venial sin is a real wound but does not kill grace. The distinction matters in the confessional: mortal sins **must be confessed by name and number** (as best you can recall); venial sins are recommended but not strictly required. Tell the priest your *patterns*, not just isolated acts. *I have struggled with anger this past month, especially with my wife.* The pattern is more useful to absolve than the count.

Practical helps:

  * **Use a written examination guide.** Most missals contain one. There are many online (Magnificat publishes a monthly examination). The guide will *find* sins your memory has hidden.
  * **Be specific.** *I sinned in thought* tells the priest very little. *I dwelt on revenge fantasies for a week against my brother* tells him what to absolve.
  * **Do not confess what isn't yours.** The sins of others are not your matter. Stay with what *you* did.
  * **Do not be scrupulous.** Aquinas warns: scrupulosity makes a tyrant of conscience. If you are unsure whether something was sin, name it briefly and let the priest discern.

The Pharisee's prayer is everything we already have. The Publican's prayer is everything we have done with it. **Make the list. Walk in. Speak the names.**
