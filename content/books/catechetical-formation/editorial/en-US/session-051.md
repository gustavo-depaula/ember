The Ninth Commandment is the **interior twin** of the Sixth. Where the Sixth (Session 048) forbids adulterous and impure *acts*, the Ninth forbids the *thoughts and desires* that precede them. Aquinas, following St. John, puts this commandment under the heading of *the concupiscence of the flesh* — not the body itself (which is good) but the disordered pull of the body that whispers what the will has not consented to.

The catechism above is precise: the Ninth Commandment forbids *evil thoughts and desires*. It is not asking the impossible. **The arrival of a thought is not a sin.** What you do *with* the thought is.

St. Thomas's tradition, refined by Augustine and the Desert Fathers and articulated for laity especially by St. Francis de Sales (*Introduction to the Devout Life*), distinguishes three moments:

  1. **The *suggestion***. The first appearance of the impure thought or image — perhaps unbidden, perhaps triggered by sight. *Not yet a sin.* This is the temptation; everyone has it. Christ Himself was tempted in the wilderness.

  2. **The *delight***. The dwelling on the suggestion, the lingering with it, the imagined entering-into. **Here begins the sin.** It is venial when fleeting, mortal when full consent is given.

  3. **The *consent***. The will's *yes*: I agree, I will pursue this, I will plan toward it, I will return to the image. Mortal sin proper. From here, action is a small further step.

The Christian discipline is therefore not to **never have the thought** (impossible) but to **interrupt at moment 2**. The classical practice is short, blunt, repeated, and practiced. Three forms of it:

  * **Turn the eyes.** *Avert the gaze* is older than St. Benedict. The thumb on the screen. The deliberate look-away in the gym, on the train, in the algorithm.
  * **Pray the Holy Name.** *Jesus*, *Mary*, said as a single word in the moment, displaces the imagination by occupying it. Joseph and Potiphar's wife (today's image) is the icon: he runs. Sometimes the body must move.
  * **Confess promptly.** What is hidden grows; what is named loses its grip. Frequent confession — even monthly — is the medicine of the Sixth and Ninth Commandments together.

Pius X's positive command is striking: the Commandment *orders us perfect purity of soul, and the greatest respect — even in the depths of the heart — for the sanctuary of the family.* The deepest *sanctuary* is the one no one else sees. To revere there is the work of holiness in private.

The clean heart is not the absence of desire. It is **desire ordered toward what is real and good**. The pure heart still loves; it loves rightly. *Blessed are the pure of heart, for they shall see God* — Christ does not promise that the pure will *be free from temptation*. He promises that they will *see Him*. That is what purity is for.
