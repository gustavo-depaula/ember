Hope is not optimism. Optimism is a temperament; hope is a virtue. Optimism predicts the weather; hope leans on a Person. St. Thomas teaches that hope reaches toward two things together — *eternal beatitude* as its end, and *the divine help* that makes that end possible. We hope **for** God, but only **with** God. The promise is His; the strength to walk to it is His; the courage to keep going after we have failed today is His.

That courage is what hope tastes like in ordinary life. The Christian who has fallen many times and gets up the seventy-seventh — that is hope. The Christian who confesses again the same sin she has confessed since adolescence — that is hope. The Christian who refuses to believe his own despair when it whispers *you are too far gone* — that is hope.

Charity, St. Thomas writes, is **friendship with God**. Not metaphorically. He grounds it on the words of Christ:

> *I will not now call you servants ... but I have called you friends.* (John 15:15)

Friendship requires shared life and shared affection, and grace makes both possible. From this friendship with God, every other love is reordered. We love God for His own sake. We love ourselves rightly because He loves us. We love our neighbor because they are made for Him too — and yes, even our enemies, because Christ named them as the testing ground of charity.

Charity is also what St. Thomas calls the **form** of the other virtues. Without it, faith and hope can exist (a person can believe and hope without yet loving), but the virtues do not yet *save* — they are like organs in a body where the heart is not beating. Today, then, attend to charity. Refuse despair. Refuse to step over the neighbor. Let one act of charity — small, deliberate, unseen — be the heartbeat that makes the other virtues alive.
