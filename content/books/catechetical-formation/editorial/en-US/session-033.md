If you ever say *I'll go to heaven and finally be free of this body*, the catechism above is asking you to take it back. Christianity does **not** teach that the body is a prison the soul will one day escape. Christianity teaches the **resurrection of the body** — your body, *recognizable*, *transformed*, *eternal*. The Signorelli image (today's ekphrasis) is theologically exact: bodies climb out of the earth at the end of time, surprised, gymnastic, shining.

This is the doctrine that most clearly separates Christianity from every other religion of antiquity. The Greeks dreamed of the soul liberated from the body. The Hindus dreamed of the soul transmigrating until released from the body. The Christian dreams the body *raised*. Christ Himself, after the Resurrection, ate fish on the shore (Luke 24:42) and let Thomas put fingers in His wounds (John 20:27). The body is not a costume; it is part of who we are.

What will the resurrected body be like? Aquinas, following 1 Corinthians 15 and Romans 8, walks four qualities the Latin tradition has called the *dotes corporum gloriosorum* — the **dowries of the glorified body**:

  * **Impassibility** — incapable of suffering, decay, or death. Pain ends. Aging ends. The thousand small slow betrayals of the body end.
  * **Brilliance** — *clarity*, the visible radiance of the soul shining through the body. *Then shall the just shine forth as the sun in the kingdom of their Father* (Matthew 13:43). Holiness, finally visible.
  * **Agility** — the body perfectly responsive to the soul's will, no longer a sluggish or rebellious instrument. Move at the speed of thought. Go where the soul goes.
  * **Subtlety** — the body penetrating other matter as Christ entered the locked room. Real, but no longer subject to the limits of solid matter.

For the damned, the body rises too — but with the inverse of these qualities. *The wicked will rise* says Aquinas, *to the resurrection of judgment, not of life* (cf. John 5:29). This is the gravity that should sit underneath every choice you make with the body now. Not because God is keeping a ledger, but because the body you are forming, by every habit, is the body that will rise.

This makes everything you do with the body **theologically serious**. The fast that feels small. The chastity that feels impossible. The bodily reverence at Mass — kneeling, bowing the head at the Holy Name, receiving Communion deliberately — these are not mere conventions. They are the body **rehearsing eternity in advance**. You will see God face to face one day, with eyes you have already trained to look elsewhere or not. Train them now to look toward Him.

*Your body is going somewhere — not into the ground forever, but back, restored, recognizable. What you do with it now is not throwaway.*
