**Worthiness** is the word that has stopped a thousand Catholics from receiving Communion. The catechism above is exact about what worthiness *is*, and what it *is not*.

Worthiness in the technical sense — what the Church requires before you receive — is **the state of grace**: the absence of mortal sin on your conscience. If you are aware of mortal sin, the rule is unambiguous: *go to Confession first*. Receiving the Body of the Lord while in mortal sin is not a small offense; it is, in St. Paul's words, eating *judgment* upon oneself (1 Corinthians 11:29). The sin of sacrilege is added to the sin already there.

Worthiness in *any other sense* — *am I a good enough person? have I prayed enough this week? do I deserve this?* — is a category the Church does not recognize. **No one is worthy of the Eucharist.** The Eucharist is given precisely to the unworthy. The Church puts these words on your lips at every Mass:

> *Lord, I am not worthy that Thou shouldst enter under my roof, but only say the word and my soul shall be healed.*

This is not rhetorical apologizing. It is *doctrine*. The centurion in the Gospel said it of his servant; the Church says it of every soul about to receive. **Only say the word.** The word He says, when you receive Him, is *come*.

The catechism above also names the **second** disposition: *to know and to think about whom one is going to receive*. Aquinas would call this *recollection* — the soul's deliberate attention. Here are three small disciplines that change the encounter:

  * **The walk to the altar.** Do not look around. Do not think about lunch. Repeat one phrase silently — *my Lord and my God*, or *Jesus, I trust in You*, or just the Name *Jesus* on each footstep.
  * **The reception itself.** *Amen* — Hebrew for *truly, faithfully, so be it* — is the strongest word a Christian has. When the priest says *the Body of Christ*, your *amen* is your faith offered. Mean it.
  * **The thanksgiving.** Three minutes after, even before the dismissal, with your eyes closed if you can. Christ is sacramentally within you for at least the time the species take to dissolve. **Speak with Him while He is closer than your breath.**

Cristóbal Rojas's image (today's ekphrasis) — *a girl in white veil, hands folded, kneeling at a rail* — is the model the catechism is asking us to recover. *The first Communion is also the model of the next ten thousand.*
