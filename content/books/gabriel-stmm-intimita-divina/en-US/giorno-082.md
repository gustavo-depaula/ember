## 6 — RULES OF STRIPPING

**Presence of God.** — *O Lord, my blindness and my weakness still need Your light and Your strength to undertake generously the way of nothingness.*

### Meditation

**1** — Jesus said: "the kingdom of heaven is like
to a merchant who goes in search of fine pearls; and, having found


one pearl of great price, he goes, sells all that he has and
buys it" (Mt. 13, 45). The pearl of great price is union
with God; if you wish to acquire it you must sell all
you have, that is, detach yourself from every disordered appetite. This is
why St. Teresa of Avila, speaking of detachment, was able
to say that, "lived perfectly, it is everything" (Way 8, 1).
Certainly, the spiritual life is not reduced to detachment and does not
end there, but detachment practiced with perfection does
effectively bring it to its term: union with God.
Only the Lord can lead you to this union, but He
will do so only if you, like the merchant of the Gospel, sell
everything, that is, renounce every least attachment that still
binds you to yourself or to creatures.

Here are the golden rules St. John of the Cross
proposes to you to bring about this total detachment: "The soul
must be inclined: not to what is easier, but to what is harder;
not to what is tastier, but to what is more insipid; not to what is more delightful, but to what is more distasteful;
not to rest, but to toil; not to what consoles, but to what disheartens; not to more, but to less;
not to things more noble and precious, but to things
more lowly and despised; not to wanting anything, but to
wanting nothing" (A. I, 13, 6). By doing this, little by little
you will accustom yourself to contradict that disordered tendency to enjoy which is the root of all attachments. It is a matter
of going against the current: it is therefore a harsh and toilsome work
carried out only by blows of the will; one must act in a sense
contrary to nature and push oneself exactly where nature recoils. But for the soul in love with God this
work is sweet: it knows that all it refuses itself
it gives to the Lord, and that when it has arrived at denying itself
in all, at selling all, God Himself will place in its hands
the precious pearl of divine union.


**2** — "It is necessary that [the soul] embrace from the heart such an exercise of self-denial and virtuous works, training in it the will with order and discretion" (A. I,
13, 7). Of the soul that wishes to enter the way of nothingness, John of the Cross asks two things: first, decision
and generosity, because one who does not have the courage to deny himself
in all will never reach total detachment and therefore
not even union with God. But at the same time he
also asks for "order and discretion." The Saint does not claim that
always and in all things you choose what is harder, more painful
or more toilsome - which would not even be possible, both with regard to circumstances proper to one's state, and with regard to physical strength which always needs
a certain relief - but he asks you to be inclined to
this choice, that is, to cultivate the desire for it. He asks you to
create in yourself the inclination and habit of doing things
contrary to your tendencies, so that, when some opportune
occasion presents itself, you do not draw back because of nature's repugnances. Above all at the beginning
of the spiritual life it is necessary to proceed with discretion
and to regulate oneself by the counsel of confessor and superiors,
particularly regarding bodily mortification. But it is of highest importance that you be firmly resolved
to train your will in this exercise of renunciation,
that you never stop because of your cowardice and when, either by duty
or propriety, you must allow yourself certain reliefs, you do so always with detachment, that is, keeping your will
detached from the taste and pleasure it may find in them.

It is clear that if you do not make yourself strong against your attachments
and do not resolve once and for all to make them all die,
you will never reach the goal. It is a true death
to selfish and earthly satisfactions, but from this
death life will be born. Jesus said it: "Unless a grain
of wheat falling into the earth dies, it remains alone; but
if it dies, it bears much fruit... And he who hates his own
life in this world shall keep it unto life eternal"


(Jn. 12, 24-25).

### Colloquy

O Lord, in the light of Your teachings, even the
hard and rugged way of total stripping becomes lovable


and everything invites me to undertake it with courage. Yet,
You know that I am weak and that by nature I spontaneously shrink from all that is difficult, toilsome, disheartening; You know that my nature always tends to minimal
effort, to what is easier, more pleasant, more comforting. But Your love, O my God, is omnipotent and You,
who out of love created me from nothing, can by that same
love overturn even my tastes and my tendencies.
Yes, I feel it: only Your love can urge me along this
way, can give me the courage to undertake this profound reform of myself; only Your love, O Lord,
is the weight that drags me toward total renunciation. Only Your
love draws me and will be my reward. But You, my God,
deign to draw me ever more powerfully, because
my weakness always tends to stop me, to hold me back;
it is this that I fear.

"But why, O Lord, should I worry over my fears
and lose courage because of my weakness?
You make me understand that I must fortify myself in humility,
persuading myself that I can do very little alone and that
without Your help I am nothing. I therefore wish to trust
in Your mercy and utterly distrust my own strength,
convinced that my weakness comes entirely from leaning upon myself. You teach me that I must not be surprised at
my struggles, because when a soul wishes to give itself to mortification, it meets difficulties everywhere.
Does it wish to renounce its comforts? What pain! Neglect
honor? What torment! Suffer an insulting word?
An intolerable torture: in short it finds itself besieged by deadly sorrows. But when it resolves to die to the world,
all anguish will cease" (cf. T.G. P.3, 12).

You died for me, O Lord; grant that I may know,
for love of You, how to die to myself, to my tastes, to my
satisfactions. To die to myself, to live for You, to reach
union with You.
