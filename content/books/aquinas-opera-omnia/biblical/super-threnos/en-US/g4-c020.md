# Lecture 20

^2:20^ Res. Behold, O Lord, and consider whom you have thus made a vintage of: shall women then eat their own fruit, their children of a span long? Shall the priest and the prophet be slain in the sanctuary of the Lord?  

> **42**. Here he presents the prayer.

And first, he calls forth mercy because of the cruelty of the punishment;  

second, because of its universality, at ***the child and the old man lie without on the ground*** (Lam 2:21);  

third, because of the impossibility of escape, at ***you have called as to a festival*** (Lam 2:22).  

For the first, he does two things.  

First, he gets his attention: ***whom have you thus made a vintage of***, as if to say, “No one other than me”; or, “Who is there like as I am,” because I was chosen from among my fathers. ***The Lord has made a vintage of me in the day of his fierce anger*** (Lam 1:12).  

Second, he presents the cruelty of the punishment against natural piety: ***shall women then eat their own fruit***, as if to say, “Can you really allow this?” We read in Josephus and Hegesippus that this was done at the siege of the Romans, and in 2 Kings 5 and 7 at the siege of Samaria. ***The hands of the pitiful women have boiled their own children*** (Lam 4:10).  

And also against good religion: ***shall the priest and the prophet be slain in the sanctuary of the Lord***, as if to say, “Can you really allow them to be killed?” *Begin at my sanctuary* (Ezek 9:6).
