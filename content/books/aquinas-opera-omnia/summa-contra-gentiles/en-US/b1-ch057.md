# Chapter 57 — That God’s knowledge is not discursive

Furthermore, we gather from the foregoing that God’s thoughts are not argumentative or discursive.  

Our thoughts are argumentative when we pass from one thought to another, as when we reason from principles to conclusions. For a person does not argue or discourse from the fact that he sees how a conclusion follows from its premises, and considers both together: for this happens not by arguing, but by judging of an argument. Even so neither does material knowledge consist in judging of material things. Now, it was shown *(Ch. 55.)* that God does not consider one thing after another successively as it were, but all things simultaneously. Therefore, his knowledge is not argumentative or discursive, although he understands all discourse and argument.  

Again. Anyone who argues views the premises by one consideration and the conclusion by another: for there would be no need after considering the premises to proceed to the conclusion, if by the very fact of considering the premises one were to consider the conclusion also. Now God knows all things by one operation, which is his essence, as we have proved above. *(Ch. 46.)* Therefore, his knowledge is not argumentative.  

Further. All argumentative knowledge has something of potency and something of act, since conclusions are potentially in their premises. But potency has no place in the divine intellect, as we have shown above. *(Ch. 16.)* Therefore, his intellect is not discursive.  

Moreover. In all discursive knowledge something must be caused, since the premises are, so to speak, the cause of the conclusion; hence a demonstration is described as a syllogism that produces knowledge. *(Aristotle, *Posterior Analytics* 1.2, 71b17–18.)* But nothing can be caused in the divine knowledge, since it is God himself, as shown above. *(Ch. 45.)* Therefore, God’s knowledge cannot be discursive.  

Again. Those things which we know naturally are known to us without our discoursing about them, as in the case of first principles. Now knowledge in God cannot be otherwise than natural, nor in fact otherwise than essential; since his knowledge is his essence, as we proved above. *(Ch. 45.)* Therefore, God’s knowledge is not argumentative.  

Further. Whatever is moved must be reduced to a first mover that is mover only and not moved. *(Ch. 13.)* Therefore, that from which comes the first source of movement must be absolutely a mover unmoved. Now this is the divine intellect, as we have shown above. *(Ch. 44.)* Therefore, the divine intellect must be an absolutely unmoved mover. But argument is a movement of the intellect in passing from one thing to another. Therefore, the divine intellect is not argumentative.  

Again. That which is highest in us is inferior to that which is in God: for the inferior does not touch the superior except in its summit. Now the summit in our knowledge is not reason, but understanding, which is the source of reason. Therefore, God’s knowledge is not argumentative, but purely intellectual.  

Moreover. All defect is far removed from God, because he is simply perfect, as proved above. *(Ch. 28.)* But argumentative knowledge results from an imperfection of the intellectual nature. For what is known through another thing is less known than what is known in itself; nor does the nature of the knower suffice to reach what is known through something else without this thing through which the other is made known. Now in argumentative knowledge, one thing is made known through another, whereas what is known intellectually is known in itself, and the nature of the knower suffices for the knowledge thereof without any means from without. Hence it is clear that reason is a defective intellect, and consequently the divine knowledge is not argumentative.  

Again. Without any discourse of reason those things are understood whose species are in the knower: for the sight does not discourse in order to know a stone, the image of which is in the sight. Now the divine essence is the likeness of all things, as we have proved above. *(Ch. 54.)* Therefore, it does not proceed to know a thing by a discourse of reason.  

It is also clear how to solve the arguments that would seem to prove the presence of discourse in the divine knowledge. First, because he knows other things through his essence. For it has been proved that this does not involve discoursing, since his essence is related to other things not as the premises to a conclusion, but as species to things known. Second, because some might think it unfitting that God should be unable to argue. For he has the knowledge of arguing as judging, and not as discoursing by arguing.  

Sacred Scripture bears witness to this truth which we have proved by reason. For it is said: *all are open and laid bare to his eyes* (Heb 4:13). Because the things that we know by reasoning are not in themselves naked and open to us, but are opened out and laid bare by reason.
