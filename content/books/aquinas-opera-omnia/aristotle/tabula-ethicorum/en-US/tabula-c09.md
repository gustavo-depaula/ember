***Ignorance***

***Everything done through ignorance is not done voluntarily, but is not necessarily involuntarily.***

A man who acts through ignorance does not feel any saddenness in his action.  

To act through ignorance and to do something ignorantly seem to be different things. Such as a man drunk or enraged, who does not know what he is doing, even though ignorance is here not the cause, but drunkeness.  

Every wicked man is also somehow ignorant, but a man's ignorance does not make what he ought to do something involuntary, since it is not ignorance that urges him, but vice.  

Ignorance in particular cases causes an action to be involuntary, and in these cases one should offer mercy and pardon.  

Men are blamed if their ignorance is of a universal thing.  

No one is ignorant of every circumstance unless he be insane, no one is ignorant of an action if he is the one doing it.  

A father who kills his son, because he thought him to be an assailant against him, does so in ignorance. Just as Merope who killed his son thinking he was an enemy.  

Concerning ignorance one could have of an implement, as to to its proper use, and for what motive. He lays out examples.  

A man unaware of first principles seems to act without choosing, since his ignorance means what he does is involuntary. There must, however, be remorse and sadness present in his actions.  

Ignorant men are punished if they themselves are the cause of their ignorance.  

Men are punished if they are ignorant of universal laws, about which one cannot be ignorant, which are not difficult to understand.  

We punish men who are ignorant through carelessness since it was in their power to care about such things.  

It is a thoroughly senseless man who does not know that repeated activities of a like manner produce like habits.  

If a man, through no ignorance, takes some action which would render him unjust, and he does so willingly, he will be unjust. Even so, it is not true that if the unjust man could make himself just by wishing to be so.  

Ignorant men appear to be courageous, and they are not far from courageous men who have good hope, but ignorant men are worse off. Courageous men have some dignity because they make their attack knowingly, but the ignorant men have none.  

Some men are ignorant of what they say, when what they say are ineffable to them, such as in the mysteries of Aeschylus.

***Stinginess***

***Stinginess is always associated with those who are too anxious about wealth.***

Stinginess is a lack in giving, but an excess in receiving.  

Stinginess is incurable, since old age and every other disability make men stingy.  

Stinginess is more connatural to everyone than wastefulness.  

The error in stinginess does wrong by a deficiency in giving and an excess in receiving, but to be stingy one need not only go amiss in both ways, but a man may be divided at times.  

All stingy men who are called 'miserly' lack in giving and even in taking, because they neither wish to have others' goods nor take them if they are offered to them. These are also called 'close'.  

Some are stingy who do not want to give what they have, also refuse to taking anything from others, becuase of a kind of moderation and avoidance of disgrace, that, if they were to give, they might then have to profit another's shamefully.  

Some are stingy who do not want to accept anything so as not to be obligated to give in return.  

Some stingy men are immoderate in receiving anything at all. Such as those engaging in such ignoble enterprises as pimps and usurers.  

Stingy men subject themselves to disgrace for a small gain.  

Stingy men are counted with the gambler and the graverobber, and the thief, who deal unlawfully and are met with shame.  

All disgraceful gains are stingy, that is, they profit from friends who they should be giving to, and try to gain a profit when none is necessary.  

Stinginess is rightly said to be the opposite of generosity and is a greater evil than wastefulness, since many more men act immoderately in this way.

We may apply the proverb to the incontinent man: 'When water chokes, what shall we drink?'  

The incontinent and intemperate man differ in a way, because the intemperate man follows after the pleasant from choice as an end, but the incontinent does not perceive himself as doing so but he is overcome by the force of his passions.  

Incontinent men imitate the pronouncements of men with understanding without having it themselves.  

The ignorance of an incontinent man is removed after a movemenet of passion, just as the case of the wine-drunk man or of one asleep, but this has more to do with natural science than with ethics.  

The incontient man is wont to use scientific terminology just as a wine-drunk man recites the words of Empedocles.  

What, strictly speaking, constitutes an incontinent man.  

It is evident that the incontinent, the continent, the perservering, and the effiminate are all concerned with pleasure and pain.  

An incontinent man, strictly speaking, is concerned with bodily pleasures, about which one is temperate and intemperate, not as the temperate or intemperate man, but not in choosing or understanding these pleasures.  

The incontinent man and the intemperate man concern the same thing but not in the same way, the former choose something but the latter do not.  

A man is called incontinent who is overcome by pleasant things, pleasures which many men are able to resist.  

The incontinent man is better than the intemperate man, and is not evil in every sense, he is saved by having the right end as his goal.  

The incontinent and the intemperate man are alike in that they both seek after bodily pleasures, but they differ in that the former thinks he ought, while the latter does not.  

The incontinent man does the thing that he knows he should avoid, since passion pushes him toward it.  

The incontinent man is not prudent, because every prudent man is good and does good, and the incontinent man is not this way.

***Justice***

***Justice is a habit by which men are directed to just acts.***

Legal justice is a perfect virtue, but not absolutely, it is directed toward another, and that virtue would be the most excellent of the virtues. In such a way that neither the even star nor the morning star is as wonderful as their source.  

Infraction of justice in transactions has three types: when an unjust act is committed through ignorance, when it is not done with prior deliberation, and when it is done knowlingly and after deliberation, out of vice.  

Justice has every virtue and it is, an especially perfect one. It makes the man who has it good in himself and toward others.  

Among the virtues, justice seems to be the only one concerning the good for another, for it works good for thatother, whether for the prince or for the common good.  

Legal justice is not a part of some virtue but, it is rather a virtue in itself. Subsequently, its contrary is not only partly vicious but a vice of its own.  

Justice, inasmuch as it accomplishes something else, is part of the exercise of all the virtues, but it is a virtue in itself without qualification because it is a habit.  

Particular justice has the same name since it is a whole virtue in itself, insofar as each thing has the power to act toward another.  

A special kind of justice names what men do for pleasure in gaining and sadness in loss. Legal justice, however, regards all those things related to acting virtuously.  

Particular justice is divided into distrubutions and transactions.  

Distributive justice consists in the distribution of both honours and weath. Commutative justice, however, conisists in transactions, such as when one receieves something in exchange for another.  

Commutative justice includes some voluntary things, such as selling and other similar things, and some involuntary things, such as theft and others which he lists.  

In commutative justice the thing is only the difference in damage. It makes no difference if the good man robbed the evil one, or the other way round.  

The kind of justice mentioned only exists between those men governed by the law.  

A man is called just who acts by choice with the habit of justice and distributes both to himself and to another.  

There is a kind of justice that directs voluntary and involuntary transactions. This is distinct from distributive justice.  

Justice is called *dikaion* and the just man *dicastes* as it were, 'directed to *dican*,' examples follow. ( ref 1132a 31)  

Justice is called the good of another.  

We do not call justice the relationship of a lord to his servant or of the father to his son, at least, at any rate, until in any case he grows up to be a certain age.  

No part of justice is useful to evil and senseless men, but all of it is harmful to them.

***What is Just***

***What is just is what is called fair, which seems self-evident to all.***

What is just involves at least four terms: two parties in which justice is dealt, and two things which are dealt.  

All admit that what is just in distribution is corresponds to some merit.  

What is just with regard to distributive justice is proportional.  

The same proportion for what is just must included four terms, for it divides both persons and things by two.  

What is just in the distribution of common things to the community or society is dealt according to a kind of geometrical proportionality.  

In transactions, what is just is not fair with reference to geometric proportion, but according to arithmetric proportion.  

In directive justice, what is just is a mean equal of loss and gain. This is why people have recourse to a judge when distributions are made.  

What is just is not the same as reciprocation, as the Pythagoreans held, because it does not belong either to distributive justice or directive justice in tranasacting. You are not always obligated to give what is yours for what is mine.  

In dealings of exchange, what is just does contain reciprocation, proportionally but not equally.  

What is just for a master and a father are not the same in distributive and commutive justice, but they are similar.  

What is just and unjust in politics is nothing other than what is just and unjust between those subject to the law who have equal authority to rule and be ruled.  

Some things are called just by legal justice, acts that accord with every virtue, things prescribed by the law.  

What is just and unjust are not said of one man, they always involve others.  

Some think that one need not be wise to know what things are just or unjust, because the laws regarding these are not difficult to understand. But these are only just materially and incidentally.  

Things are just, formally speaking, when they are rightly applied to particular cases according to special modes of action. This is more than knowing what is good for one's health.  

Just things are found, strictly speaking, in those men who possess goods used by men. Some have excess of these and some lack, some cannot have excess, such as the gods.  

What is just in the home concerns more one's wife than to his children or possessions or slaves. This is domestic justice which is distinct from political justice.

***Justice, Natural and Legal***

***In political justice, there is natural justice and legal justice. Natural justice has the same force for everyone, and everywhere the same way, and it does not depend on what men may or may not think. The difference of legal justice, however, is that it differs in its application, as he shows.***

What is just in legal matters depends on the laws that are enacted and applied, just as measures are not always the same everywhere. Similarly, natural and human things are different among different peoples, but there is one nevertheless which is best.  

Each just thing, whether natural or legal, is as particular actions of a universal, since there are many actions and they are ruled by one universal.  

If a man returns a deposit unwillingly and on account of fear, it should not be called acting justly nor an act of justice (dycaioprage), except incidentally, and so too with the unjust.  

It is not really proper to speak of a man as being just to himself, except metaphorically, and out of a certain resemblance.  

Any man, *qua* man, can have a kind of justice toward another who can take part in a law or agreement, and the same is true of friendship.  

What is just is twofold: somthing unwritten, and something set down in law.

***What is Unjust / an injustice***

***An injustice can be disproportionate in two ways, in excess and defect, and this can even occur in acts of distributive justice. The one who commits the injustice has in excess, whereas the one suffering under it has less of a good.***

It is not possible for a man to do injustice to himself, because then he willingly suffers injustice (and here examples are given of particular injustices), no one commits adultery with his own wife, but another's, nor does he knock down his own door. The reason for this is that for injustice to occur, a man must suffer against his will.  

No one willingly commits injustice against himself.  

A man commits an injustice to the city if he kills himself, because the city suffers a loss, and the city's good name is somewhat tarnished. This is why its laws direct that such men not be buried.  

A man is not committing injustice against himself if he commits only one unjust act. He is not entirely evil, having vice in the abstract, which is part of illegal injustice.  

All injustice done is done so willingly and by choice.  

It seems that A man who suffers a wrong and resists it does not commit injustice.  

It happens more often that one who distributes, and who does so willingly, does an injustice, (because he originated that act), but the receiver of this does not always commit an injustice unless it is in his willingness, which he may more have by some scheming.  

A man can be said to act unjustly in many ways: whether by initiating it or as an accomplice, such as a hand, or as one acting independently such as a servant or a hatchet lends assists, but these do not act unjustly formally speaking, but materially.  

A man does not, formally speaking, commit injustice if he is ignorant of both legal and natural law, , since he does not judge with reference to either kind of justice. If however, he knowingly judges unjustly, his action is, properly covetous and unjust, he acts for the sake of someone's favour or out of fear of punishment.  

The man who judges unjustly because of a friendship has more than his due of the case, since the matter about which he judges does not yield land but money.  

Men judge it an easy thing to do what is unjust. This, however, is not so. True, it is easy and within their power to have intercourse with a neighbour's wife, or to kill their neighbour, but this, formally speaking, is not acting unjustly. Acting as unjust men do is, however, neither easy nor in their power, unless it springs from a habit of character.  

Formally speaking, 'to act unjustly' in itself, is to act as an unjust man does and to do so out of habit. Merely prescribing a drug, such as a laxative, does not constitute medical care, but doing the job of a physician does.  

Euripedes unseemingly maintained that suffering injustice is voluntary.  

Whether a man may do himself injustice.  

For a man to suffer injustice, he must be suffering against his will.  

No one willingly, not even the incontinent man, suffers injustice, it is rather against his will.  

A man who gives what is his own does not suffer injustice. A proof of this is given by citing the Homer: a man named Glaucus gave gold things to Diomedes in exchange for bronze, and another, one hundred oxen for ten. This is giving unjustly, in a material way, but it is in his power to not suffer this injustice. There must be another who commits the unjust thing. (Il. vi. 236)
