# Chapter 16

***Speusippus***

***Speusippus seems to follow more after than Pythagoreans than Plato in his view of mathematical principles.***

Term 2  

***Science***

***All sciences seek after some good, but while endeavoring to supply what they lack in the particular, they leave out the universal good. Such an approach, however, to set aside all other tools of knowing and not to use their knowledge, is not rational.***

Science is a certain knowledge and concerns necessary things that are eternal.  

Science is a demonstrative habit which is produced by demonstration and whatever other causes are put forth in the *Analytics.*  

Rectitude does not pertain to science.  

It is evident that nothing is stronger than knowledge.  

Science can be said in two ways, in habit and in act.  

He divides sciences three ways: between act and habit, between the universal and the particular, and between a restrained habit and an unrestrained one.  

Term 3  

***The Wise Man***

***The wise man, as Socrates says, is not drawn away by the passions.***

Term 4  

***Sleep***

***During sleep there is no apparent difference between good men and evil men. Hence the saying that happy men are no better off than miserable men for half of their lives.***

Sleep is rest in the soul's activity, which is what determines one's goodness or wickedness.  

Term 5  

***The Proud Man***

***The proud man and the one feigning courage appear reckless because of how they act in the face of dangers. But they do not stand up to them long, since many of these men are in fact cowards.***

Term 6  

***Health***

***Health comes to men in a greater or lesser degree, since all men do not share in one proportion of health.***

Term 7  

***Sophists***

***Sophists wish to argue indubitable positions so that they seem wise.***

Some sophists profess to teach political science, but do not practice it. Men who engage in civil matters have a greater part in this, they seem to have some power to act in politics, and more experience than merely thinking about it.  

If sophists were politicans then they would rightly be engaged in civil matters and turn their friends, or children, or whomever else into other politicians. In that case, they could do no better for their cities or themselves than to leave behind other statesmen for engaging in political matters after them.  

Sophists seem far off from not just acting, but even from knowing about political science, they do not know either its nature, or what things it considers.  

Sophists called political science the same thing as rhetoric.  

Sophists do not follow through with what they promise and so are rightly taken to task.  

Term 8  

***Speeches***

***If conjectures correspond to practical matters, they correspond to our actions, and we should heed them. If they do not, they are to be received lightly much as triffles sounding in the air.***

Speeches in this field of knowledge are not enough to bring about its end, that is, to make men good. Since, if they were enough, men who taught them would be deserving of great compensation.  

Speeches exhorting morals can encourage a liberal youth to the good, and can furnish a man who loves the good with virtue. Nevertheless, many men have the kind of nature that would not be deterred by shame but the fear and punishment would have a better effect. If they seek pleasures instead of goods, these cannot be changed to the good.  

Speech and teaching are not, in themselves, enough to make men good, because men who are living according to their own passions cannot be convinced. And so, the soul must have some training in taking joy in good things and a habit of hating what is evil, just as soil does not grow seed well unless it is cultivated well.  

Term 9  

***Statements***

***Statements should follow the nature of their subject matter.***

A statements argument about actions lacks certitude and does not fall under art.  

General statements are more generally applicable, but statements on particular matters are more certain.  

Statementss about passions and acts are less credible than acts themselves.  

True statements are most useful not just for knowing but for living, since when they are reflected in the actions of the one speaking they are convincing, and urge those who understand them to live in accordingly  

The statements of Eudoxus were believed more for his virtuous counduct than for their own sake.

Term 15  

***The Elderly***

***How the elders felt toward Helen.***

The elderly and the morose do not tend to be friendly, since there are few things they find pleasurable. No one can continually live with a gloomy person or a displeasurable person, since our nature flees what is sad and seeks after what is pleasurable.  

The elderly and the morose make fewer friends inasmuch the more ill-tempered they are and the less they delight in conversation, for these seem to be friendly and what make friendship. So it is that the young, who do take much joy in these make friends quickly, but not the elderly or the morose.  

We must show the elderly honour by rising and by bowing.  

Term 16  

***Perceiving***

***Perceiving and understanding are pleasurable, since whoever sees or hears or understands, perceives and realises that he is doing it. Now whoever perceives, perceives that he is perceiving and that he is living and that he *is,* and to be and to live are the most pleasurable things, since living is a good for its own sake.***

Term 21  

***Solon***

***Solon said that to know if a man is happy one must consider the end of his life.***

Solon said that the man who has a moderate amount of external goods must do what he can virtuously within his means.  

Term 22  

***Wisdom***

***Wisdom is the most certain kind of knowledge.***

Wisdom treats of universal things, not about some particular matter, just as Homer said in the *Margites.*  

Wisdom is the head of the sciences and therefore concerns the most honourable things.  

Wisdom does not regard useful things since, if it did, there would be many kinds of wisdom.  

Wisdom does not explore the means by which a man may be happy.  

Wisdoms is a part of virtue on the whole, and acting according to it makes a man happy.  

Wisdom is more honourable than prudence, as he demonstrates through reason and by giving an example.  

Term 23  

***A Philosopher***

***No one is a philosopher by nature.***

A philosopher is a contemplative, save for the necessities of life, which everyone needs, and he is content with having little of these. He is most self-sufficent and he is about contemplation, though he would benefit from having others to share in philosophy with him.  

Everything said about a happy and contemplative man belongs to the philosopher, so he will be most beloved by God and his happiness will be in accord with his activity.  

Term 24  

***Contemplating***

***Contemplation can sometimes be harmful to one's health.***

For a man of contemplation, no external goods are needed. These things are rather an obstacle to contemplation. But insofar as this contemplative is a man and lives among others, he will have need of such externals to live a human life.  

Term 25  

***Contemplation***

***Human contemplation, which most leads to happiness and shares most in the divine, is unlike all other things because of its excellence.***

The more one engages in contemplation, the greater share he has in happiness. Those with more contemplation are happier too, not because happiness merely accompanies it, but because of the contemplation itself.

***The Senses***

***We do not acquire our use of the senses by seeing and hearing many times over, but rather, the other way round, we use them because we have them.***

It is of no matter whether we say the sense acts or that the body in which it resides (say, an animal) , acts.  

Sense is not a thing that has power over action.
