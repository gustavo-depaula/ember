# Chapter 17

***Term 1***

***Temperance***

***Temperance and fortitude seem to be virtues of the irrational parts of the soul.***

Temperance does not regard pleasures of sight, hearing, and smell, except incidentally, such as the recollection of desirable things, like food.  

The temperate man moderately desires pleasures, whatever is conducive to his health and good habits.  

The temperate man desires the right things at the right times, as much as reason ordains.  

Temperance is a mean concerning pleasures and pains but not all, it is more concerned with the sense of touch, and less so concerning pains.  

Temperance and intemperance concern the sense of smell only incidentally, as much as smells represent the foods which are themselves the thing causing pleasure.  

Temperance and intemperance do not relate to all parts of the body, but only those relating to to food and sex.  

Term 2  

***The Temperate Man***

The temperate man occupies the middle way, since he does not inordinately delight in pleasures, but rather dislikes them, nor is he pained when they are gone. He desires them only in a measured way, not more than is necessary.  

We become temperate by abstaining from pleasures, and in thus being temperate we can more greatly abstain from them.  

Every temperate man is continent but not the other way round.  

The temperate man and the continent man are the same.  

Term 3  

***Fear***

***Fear is the anticipation of an evil thing, many examples follow.***

Fear makes men sad and dumbfounded.  

Fear in general is more voluntary than fear about a particular thing, since fear in general is painless, but fear about a particular thing, such as undergoing death, stuns a man.  

When a man is afraid about particular things, it seems like he is under compulsion.  

The kind of man who does not fear anything, neither and an earthquake nor floods, does not have a name, but he could be called a madman, as is said of the Celts, who are senseless.  

Term 4  

***The Coward***

The coward fears the wrong things or too much, and so he is classified more by an excess of fear, rather than a lack of courage.  

Term 5  

***What is Terrible***

Something that is terrible is above man's power something feared by everyone who understands it. But since the power of a man may differ in proportion, a thing can be terrible to a greater or lesser degree.  

Term 6  

***The Tyrant***

Tyrants are not called wasteful, since the greatness of their wealth does not lend itself to excess in giving.  

The tyrant is concerned with benefiting himself, but the king is concerned with benefitting with his subjects.  

Tyranny is the worst form of government, this is obvious because it is opposed to the best form.  

Tyranny is the perversion of monarchy.  

In tyranny, which is the worst form of government, there is no or very little friendship, because the one ruling and the one ruled do not share anything.  

The tyrant acts towards his subject as a lord to his slave, as the soul toward the body, and as the workman with a tool, those who use them benefit from them because of their utlity.  

Witty men are well-liked by tyrants, since they act favourably to what tyrants seek after.  

We do not call tyrants illiberal, but rather pernicious, impious, and unjust, such as when they plunder cities or defile sacred places.
