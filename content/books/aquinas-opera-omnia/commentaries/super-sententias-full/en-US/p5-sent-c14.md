# D. 13 - Perfections of human nature of Christ

***Perfections of human nature of Christ***

***Division of the text***

After the Master has considered those things that belong to God incarnate by reason of the union, here he considers the things that follow it in virtue of either of the natures. And since the things that belong to him by reason of his divine nature, which is not diminished in the Incarnation, have been treated in Book I, it now remains to consider here the things that belong to Christ in virtue of the nature that was exalted through that Incarnation.  

Now, this part is divided into three parts.  

In the first, he considers the things that he assumed along with his human nature.  

In the second, he considers the things that were worked through that human nature, in Distinction 17, at *after the above, it is good to consider whether Christ willed or prayed for anything which was not done*.*Sent.* III, 17.1(47).1.  

In the third, he considers the death that he endured in the human nature, in Distinction 21, at *after the foregoing, we must consider whether, at death, the soul or flesh was separated from the Word*.*Sent*. III, 21.1(68).1.  

The first is divided into two parts:  

first, he considers the perfections he assumed with the human nature, with regard to their fullness;  

second, he considers them through a comparison to the divine nature, in Distinction 14, at *since Christ’s soul was wise by a freely given wisdom, here it is necessary to ask whether it had wisdom equal to God’s*.*Sent*. III, 14.1(39).1.  

The first is divided into two:  

first, he considers the fullness of his perfection as regards the grace of the affection and the knowledge of the intellect that Christ received in the instant of his conception;  

second, he objects against this, at *but there appears to be a contradiction between this and what we read in the Gospel of Luke*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).4.  

And this latter is divided into two parts:  

in the first, he objects to the contrary, through authorities drawn from the canon;  

in the second, through authorities drawn from the saints, at *and yet elsewhere it is found to have been written that he made progress according to human sense*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).6.  

Regarding the first, he does three things:  

first, he makes the objection;  

second, he resolves it, at *to which it can be truly said*;*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).5.  

third, he confirms and explains the resolution through an authority, at *hence Gregory says in some homily*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).5  

*And yet elsewhere it is found to have been written*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).6. Here he objects through the authority of Ambrose, and he does three things:  

first, he states the authority;  

second, he explains and shows how the proposed authority partly strengthens the faith, and yet partly can be taken as providing matter for making an error, at *these words of Ambrose are to be examined with pious diligence*;*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).7.  

third, he shows how this should be interpreted so that no error result, at *but from what cause are we to take the meaning of that statement?**Sent*. III, 13.1(38).9.  

Here begins the question about Christ’s grace, since we will treat his knowledge in the following distinction. And three things are asked:  

first, about his grace insofar as he is an individual man;Q. 1.  

second, of his grace insofar as he is the head of the Church;Q. 2.  

third, of the grace of his union.Q. 3.  

Question 1  

***The grace of Christ as an individual man***

***Regarding the first, two things are asked:***

first, whether in him there was given a habitual grace whereby his soul was perfected, which is called the "grace of the individual man";  

second, about the fullness of that grace.  

Article 1  

***Whether in Christ there was habitual grace perfecting his soul***

To the first we proceed as follows.Parallels: *ST* III.7.1; *De veritate*, q. 29, a. 1; *Compend. theol.*, ch. 213; *Super Ioan.* 3, lect. 6. It appears that the aforesaid grace was not present in Christ.  

Obj. 1: For just as the sonship of adoption is related to natural sonship, so too is gratuitous goodness to natural goodness. But just as Christ had it from the union that he was the natural Son, so too he had it that he was naturally good. Therefore just as the sonship of adoption is not given to him, neither should gratuitous goodness be.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, grace is given to men so that they might become like God. But Christ was never unlike God. Therefore he never needed grace.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, where the light of the sun is present, the light of a candle is not needed, since the greater light overwhelms the lesser. But in Christ the light of divinity was like the light of the sun. Therefore there was no need to add the light of grace.  

Obj. 4: Furthermore, Damascene saysJohn Damascene, *On the Orthodox Faith*, bk. 3, ch. 15 (PG 94:1050). that just as the soul rules the flesh and works through it as through an instrument, so too the divinity ruled Christ’s soul. But an instrument does not need any habit by which it might be ruled in its activity, since it is ruled by the principal agent. Therefore neither did Christ’s soul need a habit.  

Obj. 5: Furthermore, grace is given to men so that sin might be avoided through it and glory might be attained. But from the very fact that Christ was God he could not sin, as was said in the preceding Distinction,Above, d. 12, q. 2, aa. 1, 2. and again, he had a full right in divine glory, as he said in Distinction 10.Above*,* d. 10, q. 2, a. 1, resp. 3. Therefore he did not need grace.  

On the contrary, just as the soul is the perfection of the body, so grace is the perfection of the soul. But no less should the soul be perfected than the body be perfected. Therefore just as he assumed a body with a soul, so too he ought to assume a soul with grace.  

Furthermore, every merit is through grace. But Christ merited for himself and for us, as will be explained below in Distinction 18.Below, d. 18, aa. 4–6. Therefore he had grace.  

Furthermore, of him it is written: *grace is poured upon your lips*, and *therefore God, your God, has anointed you with the oil of gladness* (Ps 45: 2, 7), all of which show the grace in him. Therefore he also had grace.  

I answer that grace principally does two things in the soul. For first, it perfects it formally in spiritual existence, in virtue of which one becomes like God. This is also why it is called the “life of the soul.” Second, it perfects the soul for its work, insofar as the virtues emanate from grace as powers from an essence. For an activity can be perfected only if it proceeds from a power perfected by a habit.  

And on account of these two things, one must assert that there is grace in Christ’s soul, because since it is most perfect in spiritual existence, there must be something perfecting it formally in that existence. Now, the Godhead does not formally perfect it, but does so as an agent. Whence some created form must be placed in it, by which it is formally perfected; and this is grace.  

Likewise also, since his activity in virtue of his humanity is other than that in virtue of his divinity, just as they are other in nature, his human activity must have a perfecting habit. Otherwise it would be imperfect. And therefore one must assert grace and virtues in Christ.  

Reply Obj. 1: Sonship belongs to a person, and since exteriority relative to the divine glory, which the name “adoption” imports, in no way belongs to this person, the name “adoption” is not granted for Christ. But grace is a perfection of the nature, since its subject is the soul, which is part of human nature. Therefore although grace indicates something that is not naturally inherent, still it can belong to Christ by reason of his human nature.  

Reply Obj. 2: It belongs to grace to make one like God, nor must it make the unlike like; rather, it must make one who is not like, like. Nor must a negation of likeness always precede the likeness in time, but it must precede it by its nature, just as potency is before act, and the sun precedes its light.  

Reply Obj. 3: The light of the sun and the light of a candle both actively illuminate, but the Godhead and grace are not so related. Rather, the one illuminates actively and the other formally. And therefore just as both the sun and the light of the sun in the air must exist simultaneously, and the one effects the other and does not overwhelm it, so too grace itself follows from Christ’s divinity and does not overwhelm it.  

Reply Obj. 4: As the Philosopher says,Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* 8.11, 1161b4. there are two sorts of instrument: the animate and the inanimate—for example, a saw is inanimate, and a servant who is moved at the command of his lord is an animate one. Therefore an inanimate instrument is acted upon in such a way that it does not itself act, and it is called “inanimate” relative to the rational soul; and therefore it does not need a habit ruling in its activity. But an animate instrument both acts and is acted upon, so just as a servant needs a habit so that he might execute his lord’s command in the appropriate way, so too Christ’s soul needs one so that he might do divine things perfectly.  

Reply Obj. 5: Just as every form is ordered through itself toward being perfected (but that it falls to the contrary happens to it incidentally), so it is also for grace relative to sin. Whence, although Christ never sinned, nor was he able to sin even if he did not have habitual grace from the union to the Word, nonetheless he needed grace for his perfection, as was said. Furthermore, although from the very fact that he was God, glory was owed to him, nonetheless there needed to be something formally perfecting his very soul for the act of glory—and this was grace, for grace grace consummated in the soul is the same thing as the light of glory—and perfecting it for the act on the way here below.  

Article 2  

***Whether Christ had the fullness of grace***

***Quaestiuncula 1***

To the second we proceed as follows.Parallels: below, d. 15, q. 1, a. 2, ad 5; *ST* III.7.9; *Compend. theol.,* cc. 213, 214; *Super Ioan.* 1, lect. 8. It appears that Christ did not have the fullness of grace.  

Obj. 1: For Christ did not have faith or hope, as will be shown below,*In III Sent*., d. 26, q. 2, a. 3. nor repentance, since he never sinned. But all of these pertain to grace. Therefore he did not have the fullness of grace.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, the Philosopher saysAristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* 10.8, 1178b10‒15. that the moral virtues are not present in the gods. But Christ was true God. Therefore he did not have the moral virtues, that is, temperance and the rest.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, virtues are ordered to their acts. But the acts of certain virtues do not befit Christ; for example, magnificence, which consists in great undertakings, since he was made poor for our sake. Therefore he did not have all the virtues, and so neither the fullness of grace  

On the contrary, *the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, full of grace and truth* (John 1:14).  

Furthermore, there was more grace in Christ than in any other man. But of some others it is written that they were full of grace, such as his mother (in Luke 1:28) and Stephen (Acts 6:8). Therefore much more did Christ have the fullness of grace.  

Furthermore, the Holy Spirit is said to dwell in a man through grace. But in Luke 4:1 it is said that Christ, *full of the Holy Spirit, returned from the Jordan*. Therefore he had the fullness of grace.  

Quaestiuncula 2  

Obj. 1: Moreover, it appears that his grace was infinite.Parallels: *In I Sent*., d. 17, q. 2, a. 3, ad 3; *ST* III.7.11; *De veritate*, q. 29, a. 3; *Compend. theol*., ch. 215; *Super Ioan*. 3, lect. 6. In John 3:34 it says that *it is not by measure that he gives the Spirit*. But everything finite has a measure. Therefore, since the Holy Spirit is said to be given to men insofar as they receive grace, it appears that his grace was infinite.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, for every finite thing something greater can be thought, and God can even make something greater. But in the text it says*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).3, 5. that God was not able to confer on him a greater grace, nor can a fuller one be thought. Therefore it is infinite.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, anything finite, when taken many times, becomes equal to another finite thing, or exceeds it. Therefore if Christ’s grace was finite, then the grace of some other man could be increased so much that it might equal Christ’s grace, or even exceed it—which is problematic. Therefore Christ’s grace is infinite.  

Obj. 4: Furthermore, an infinite effect does not come from a finite cause. But Christ’s merit was infinite, since it was sufficient for the redemption of the entire human race, which is an infinite power. Therefore also his grace, which is the cause of his merit, was infinite.  

On the contrary, nothing created is infinite. But Christ’s grace was created. Therefore it was not infinite.  

Furthermore, there is nothing greater than the infinite. Therefore if Christ as man had infinite grace, then as man he was not less than the Father—which is contrary to the faith.  

Quaestiuncula 3  

Obj. 1: Moreover, it appears that that grace was capable of increase.Parallels: *In I Sent*., d. 15, q. 5, a. 1, resp. 4; d. 17, q. 2, a. 4, ad 3; *ST* III.7.12. For to anything finite addition is possible. Therefore if Christ’s grace was finite, since it was created, some addition could be made to it, and thus it could be increased.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, to the degree that a greater perfection is added to an intellectual nature, so much more is its capacity increased. This is why, according to the Philosopher in *On the Soul* 3,Aristotle, *On the Soul* 3.4, 429a30; Aquinas, *Sent. De anima*, bk. 4, lect. 7, n. 688. to the degree that an intellect understands difficult things better, so far can it also understand things more. But the capacity for a greater grace makes a possibility of increase. Therefore however great is a man’s perfect grace, there remains a possibility for increase. And thus it appears that Christ was able to progress in grace.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, the visible sending is a sign of the invisible sending. But the Holy Spirit’s visible sending to Christ occurred in his thirtieth year, as Luke 3:22 indicates. Therefore at that time occurred the Holy Spirit’s invisible sending to him. But the Holy Spirit is sent invisibly to someone only by reason of a new grace being given, or by reason of a growth in grace. Therefore Christ grew in grace, since he had a prior grace.  

Obj. 4: Furthermore, just as the perfection of his soul was required for him, so too the perfection of his body. But he grew in the perfection of his body. Therefore he also grew with respect to grace, which is the perfection of the soul.  

On the contrary, Jeremiah 31:22 says, *a woman will encompass a man*,For “will encompass” the RSV reads “protects.” and here the saints say that Christ was filled with every grace from the beginning of his conception. Therefore the grace in him did not grow.  

Furthermore, Christ was a perfect comprehensor from the moment of his conception. But comprehensors are in a state of grace, not in an increase of grace. Therefore the grace in him did not grow.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 1  

To the first question, I answer that something is said to be had fully when it is had with respect to all its differences, modes, and effects. And in this respect Christ had the fullness of grace. However, this fullness can be considered according to the notion of a final, efficient, and formal causes.  

Now, the end of grace is that it join us to God. Therefore, since Christ, insofar as he is a man, was joined to the divinity not only through knowledge, love, and fruition, but also through a union in the person, so his grace attained its end most fully. And this is the fullness of the final cause.  

Further, we see that some things have bodily light only to the degree that they are luminous: for example, certain worms, rotting oak, or rubies; however, some have it to the degree that they illuminate other things: for example, the light of a candle; and some have it such that they make other things luminous; and something has it to the degree that every illumination is from it, as is the sun. So it is with the grace of Christ. For he has the grace through which he is perfect in himself; and from him it redounds to others, and of those into which it redounds, it makes some fellow workers with God—such that they themselves lead others to grace through their ministry, as is said in 1 Corinthians 3; and further, it redounds from him into all men, since *from his fullness have we all received* (John 1:16). And this is the fullness of the efficient cause.  

And likewise, some are formally perfected in grace with respect to all virtues, and with respect to the expulsion of all mortal sins. This is the fullness of sufficiency, which was in Stephen and other saints, insofar as someone is filled by God to the point that there remains nothing in him averse to God. However, in the whole Church is the fullness of the abundance [of grace], since no grace that is in some of its members is lacking from it [as a whole]. Of this fullness it is said, *that he might fill all things* (Eph 4:10). And to some a grace is also given that repels not only all mortal sins but even all venial. And this is the fullness of special prerogative, which was in the Blessed Virgin, insofar as she was full of God, to the point that there was nothing in her that was not ordered to God.  

But to Christ there is further given a grace perfecting him not only with respect to all the virtues, and with respect to all the effects of graces given gratuitously, but further with respect to the removal of every sin, not only actual but even original, and even of the power to sin. And this is the unique fullness of Christ, according to the notion of a formal cause.  

Therefore the first fullness relates to the grace of union, the second to the grace of the head, and the third to the grace of the individual.  

Reply Obj. 1: Those virtues have something of perfection—and this is so especially in Christ—and something of imperfection—and in this respect they do not belong to Christ. But we will make this clearer later.*In III Sent.,* d. 26, q. 2, a. 5.  

Reply Obj. 2: The moral virtues do not belong to Christ with respect to some uses that are in us, such as that by them the passions by which the flesh desires against the spirit are mastered—which desires were not in Christ. But with respect to other uses, according to which they will be present in heaven, they were in Christ most fully, and even with respect to certain uses on the path here below— which uses do not derogate from his perfection insofar as he was both a wayfarer and a comprehensor.  

Reply Obj. 3: To have magnificence, as it is taken by the Philosopher, it is not necessary that a man have great wealth, but that he is so disposed that, when he must or it is fitting, he wants to dispense it best. And this was present in Christ perfectly.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 2  

To the second question, it should be said that grace is said to be a gift bestowed gratuitously. Now, both a created and an uncreated gift were given to Christ the man without any preceding merits. The uncreated gift was the Holy Spirit himself, since he rested in his soul, as is said in Isa 2,See perhaps Isaiah 11:2. and also the very person of the Word, since he was given to the human nature such that the person of the Word would be its person; and in virtue of this is given to him *the name which is above every name* (Phil 2:9), and in this way the grace of Christ is infinite. However, his created gift was the very grace by which his soul was formally perfected; and this in a certain way was finite and in a certain way infinite, since according to its essence it was finite, but in three ways it can be said to be infinite.  

In one way, from its being joined to the divinity insofar as it comes together with it for the same act, such that an act informed by this grace is the act not only of a man but even of God.  

In a second, with regard to the notion of grace. For in things that are not great in size one there is not the finite or infinite in numerical or dimensive quantity, but in insofar as the thing is limited or not limited. Now, something is limited due to the capacity of the its recipient; whence what does not have an existence received in another, but a subsistent existence, does not have a limited existence, but an infinite one, as God does. However, if there is some simple subsisting form that is not its own existence, it would still have a finitude as regards its existence, which would be particular for that form, but that form would not be limited, since it would not be received in anything, for example, if there were thought to be a heat existing through itself. And in this respect even understood universal forms have an infinity. But if such a form is received in something, of necessity it is limited with regard to the existence owed to that form, and not only with regard to its existence simply speaking. For not only does it not have the fullness of being, simply speaking, but it does not have the entire existence that would perhaps be possible for that nature. But it is possible that it is not limited with regard to the notion of that form, namely, such that it have that form according to every manner of its completion, so that nothing of what pertains to that form’s perfection is lacking to it. And this will be the case if there is no defect on the side of the recipient or on the side of the agent. And in this way, the grace of Christ is said to be infinite, since whatever can pertain to the perfection of grace was wholly in Christ.  

Third, it is infinite with regard to his effects. For he is not limited to certain determinate effects; rather, through his grace he can accomplish the redemption of an infinity [of souls]; as it is said in the *Book of Causes*,Proclus (pseudo-Aristotle), *Liber de causis*, prop. 16. the power of an intelligence is infinite relative to what is beneath it. And this mode has to do with the grace of the head, but the second has to do with the grace of the individual man, and the first with the grace of the union.  

Reply Obj. 1: It appears that that authority can be understood in reference to the second stated mode. For he intends to say that in Christ the grace is not limited as regards some measure of his perfection, but has it as regards all of them. Or it can be understood to be about the grace of the union, through which [his soul] is lifted to the infinite good: namely, such that he is the true God.  

Reply Obj. 2: Those words should be understood with reference to the notion of grace, since everything that pertains to the perfection of grace was given to him. Whence God could do many other things, but they would not pertain to the notion of grace—just as he could add to a man many other natural and essential perfections, but these would not be of the notion of man, and then he would be a different species and not a man. Or it can be said that, although he could make a greater grace in and of itself, with regard to its essence, than there was in Christ, still there could exist no greater grace of which a created capacity was capable. Nor could he make another capacity that would not be created.  

Reply Obj. 3: Diverse quantities are suited to diverse species. This is clear for dimensive quantities, since there a certain size is determined for man, beyond which there is no greater size for a man, but that greater size is appropriate for a tree, and this size, although finite, a man cannot at all reach, however much he grow. Likewise for quantities of power: for although the heat of a fire does not exceed the heat of air infinitely, still there is a certain limit to air’s heat that it cannot exceed while remaining air. Whence in no way can it be intensified to the degree that it becomes equal to the heat of a fire, unless the air becomes fire. And it is the same in all qualities that follow other perfections, or dispose to them, since to specifically diverse perfections there correspond diverse grades of the perfections or dispositions.  

However, the perfection to which grace disposes one is being joined to God. And this is manifold: namely, in an enigma, and through the species. Whence, however much the grace of the wayfarer grows, it cannot be like the grace of the actual comprehensor, although it can be greater in its power; and for the same reason, however much the grace of a mere created thing might grow, it cannot reach the grace that is owed to the created thing assumed into the unity of the person, which in a certain way disposes it for the union, unless it also be assumed. And since a higher mode is not possible for a created thing by which it might be joined to God, than that through a unity of person, therefore it was said above that the created capacity cannot receive a fuller grace.  

Reply Obj. 4: The infinity of the efficacy in the merit arises from the fact that a divine person works in that action, since it is the action not only of a man, but of God and a man. According to this Dionysius names Christ’s action “theandric.”  

Response to Quaestiuncula 3  

To the third question, it should be said that growth does not belong to something insofar as it is at its term, but insofar as it is on the way to its term. For insofar as a thing is at its term, the entire potentiality of its capacity is fulfilled by the end fulfilling it, and therefore it does not have anything further to which would grow. And since Christ’s soul was from the beginning of its conception united to the ultimate end, not only through perfect fruition but even by communicating in the person of the Word, therefore his grace was not able to grow.  

Reply Obj. 1: To any finite thing there can be an addition, mathematically speaking, since the notion of quantity, which the mathematician considers by itself, does not prevent that something be added to the finite. However, the form that the natural scientist considers prevents a certain addition, and therefore according to the natural scientist this is not true, but merely according to imagination. It is similar in the proposed matter, as is clear from things said, since no grace can be greater than that of a comprehensor, nor can a glory be greater than that of a created thing united to God in the person.  

Reply Obj. 2: This should be understood of the intellectual nature’s capacity as regards its end, beyond which the capacity of no other thing extends. In the same way, no matter how much the intellect progresses in the wayfaring state here below, it never reaches to the mode of understanding it will have in heaven.  

Reply Obj. 3: The visible sending was a sign of the invisible sending, which was not accomplished at that time, but at the beginning of his conception.  

Reply Obj. 4: Christ did not assume a glorified body as it would be at his ultimate perfection. However, he did receive a glorified soul. And therefore he was able to progress in virtue of his body, but not in virtue of his soul.  

Question 2  

***The grace of Christ as head of the Church***

Next, we inquire about the grace of the head, and about this two things are asked:  

first, whether Christ, insofar as he is a man, has such a grace that he is the head;  

second, of whom is he the head.  

Article 1  

***Whether Christ is the head of the Church insofar as he is a man***

To the first we proceed as follows.Parallels: *ST* III.8.1; *De veritate,* q. 29, aa. 4, 5; *Compend. theol*., ch. 214; *Super 1 Cor. 11*, lect. 1; *Super Ephes.* 1, lect. 8; *Super Col.* 1, lect. 5. It appears that Christ is not the head of the Church insofar as he is a man.  

Obj. 1: For an animal’s spirit is diffused from its head to its members. But the Holy Spirit is not diffused to the Church from Christ insofar as he is a man, but insofar as he is God; for as Augustine says,Augustine, *On the Trinity*, bk. 15, ch. 19 (CCSL 50A; PL 42:1083). he received the Holy Spirit as man, and he pours it out as God. Therefore he is not the head as man, but as God.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, sensation and motion spread from the head to the members. But grace, which causes the motion of life in Church’s body, does not spread from one member to another. Therefore Christ as man is not the head of the Church.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, grace is created in us by God alone, since, according to Augustine,Augustine, *De diversis quaestionibus octoginta tribus*, q. 51, n. 4 (CCSL 44A; PL 40:33); see also *On the Literal Intepretation of Genesis*, bk. 10, cc. 3–5 (PL 34;CSEL 28.1:298–301). the nature of the human mind is formed immediately by God. But the life of the Church is through grace. Therefore, since it is of the notion of a head that it vivify its members for the sake of any act, it appears that Christ as man cannot be called the “head of the Church.”  

Obj. 4: Furthermore, a head does not have a head of its own. But Christ’s head is God (1 Cor 11:3). Therefore Christ is not a head.  

Obj. 5: Furthermore, the head receives an influence from another member, the heart. But Christ does not receive any influence from any other member of the Church. Therefore Christ should not be called a “head.”  

Obj. 6: Furthermore, a head can be called a “member of the body,” and a “fellow member with the others.” But Christ, it appears, cannot be called a “member of the Church,” since then his power would be partial, relative to the whole Church, and the Church would be more perfect than Christ. Therefore Christ should not be called the “head.”  

On the contrary, Ephesians 1:22–23 says, he *has made him the head over all things for the church, which is his body*. Therefore since the Church is his body in virtue of his human nature, insofar as it is conformed to him, it appears that he is the head in virtue of his human nature.  

Furthermore, a king is said to be the “head of the kingdom,” and a high priest the “head of the Church” committed to him. But Christ as man is the king and high priest of the entire human race. Therefore he, as man, is its head.  

Furthermore, that which is of greatest dignity in any genus can be called its head, as a lion is the head of the animals. But Christ, even in virtue of his human nature, is more excellent than all men. Therefore even as man he is the head of the whole Church.  

I answer that Christ is called the "head of the Church" through a likeness with a natural head. For in a natural head are found three conditions related to the other members, taken individually. The first is that it exceeds them in dignity in three ways: in height of position, in nobility of proper power, since the nobler powers—imagination, memory, and things of this sort—have a position in the head, and even in perfection, since in the head are gathered together all the senses, whereas only touch is in the other members. The second is that all the animal powers in the other members are from the head, and in this way it is said to be the principle of the other members, giving them sensation and motion. The third is that it directs all the members in their actions, on account of imagination and sensation, which overflow in it, as a spring. However, it has a fourth property in common with the other members: that in its nature it conforms with them.  

Therefore, by reason of the first property, namely of perfection, anything that is most perfect in any nature is called its “head,” like the lion among the animals. However, by reason of the second, any principle is called a “head,” the way a spring is the head of the streams, and we speak of the heading of a book, or the head of a road. However, by reason of the third property, any ruler is called a “head,” such as a king, or a general, or a high priest.  

Therefore relative to these three properties of the head, Christ can be called the head [of the Church] according to both his human and divine natures. For in virtue of his divine nature, in him is found the fullness of all Godhead, as the Apostle says (Col 1:19), and whence he *is over all, God blessed forever* (Rom 9:5), and likewise every spiritual grace in us is from him as God. Further, as God he directs us to his very self.  

But these things also belong to him in virtue of his human nature, which in Christ is of the greatest dignity by reason of its height, since it is exalted all the way to his union in the divine person, by reason of its proper activity, since it had the activity that had the greatest dignity within the Church, namely, to redeem it and to build it up in his blood, and even by reason of its perfection, since every grace is within it, just as all the senses are in the head. Likewise also, he is said to be the “head” by reason of the second property, since through him we receive the sense of faith and the motion of charity, since *grace and truth came through Jesus Christ* (John 1:17), and likewise, he directed us by teaching and example, since *Jesus began to do and teach* (Acts 1:1).  

But the fourth condition belongs to Christ in virtue of his human nature alone, and this completes the notion of the head in him. For in virtue of his human nature Christ has a perfection of the same kind as do the others, and is as it were a univocal principle, and a rule of the same form and of one genus with them. Whence, commonly speaking, Christ as God, together with the Father and the Holy Spirit, can be called the “head of the Church,” but properly speaking he is the head in virtue of his human nature. And this is called the “grace of the head,” in virtue of which the aforesaid properties belong to him chiefly insofar as he influences the other members.  

Reply Obj. 1: To give the Holy Spirit happens in two ways: as the author and as the minister. As an author it is of God alone, but as a minister even men are said to give the Holy Spirit, insofar as it is given by God through their ministry, as Paul says: *he who supplies the Spirit to you* (Gal 3:5), as the Master said above in Book I.*Sent*. I. 15. ????? And in this way Christ as man was able to give the Holy Spirit by his ministry, as it says in Romans 15:8: *I tell you that Christ became a servant to the circumcised*.  

Reply Obj. 2: In the fact that we all receive from the fullness of Christ there is something like a spreading, although it is not properly a spreading. Therefore there is a likeness with respect to the uncreated Holy Spirit himself, who is numerically the same in both the head and the members, and in a certain way descends from the head to the members, while not being divided, but remaining one. However, there is a dissimilarity with respect to the gift itself, insofar as the Holy Spirit dwells in us, since that does not spread from subject to subject.  

Reply Obj. 3: There can be a certain means in any action in two ways: with respect to perfection and with respect to mere disposition. For example, nature is a means in the work by which God produces a sensitive soul, since the ultimate perfection itself occurs by the mediation of the nature, but in the work by which he produces the rational soul, nature is only a means with respect to disposition. Likewise, I say that God immediately forms our mind with respect to the very perfection of grace, and yet here there can be a disposing means, and in this way grace flows from God by the mediation of Christ the man. For he disposed the entire human race for the reception of grace, and this in three ways. In one way, according to our activity towards him, since insofar as we believe him to be God and man, we are justified: *whom God put forward as an expiation by his blood, to be received by faith* (Rom 3:25). In another way, through his activity towards us, insofar as he removes the obstacle by making satisfaction for the sins of the whole human race, and even insofar as he merited grace and glory for us by his works, and insofar as he intercedes for us with God. In a third way, from his affinity with us, since from the very fact that he assumed a human nature, human nature is more acceptable to God.  

Reply Obj. 4: God is called the “head of Christ” not according to the complete notion of a head, for he is his head insofar as he is his principle, but he is his principle in virtue of his divinity and humanity, but insofar as he is his principle [only] according to his humanity. And in this way, he falls short of the fourth condition of a head, which is conformity in nature. Moreover, insofar as he is his principle according to his divinity, in this way he falls short of the first condition, since in this way he does not have a greater dignity.  

Reply Obj. 5: The heart is the principle of the vital powers in the whole body, and is the first principle of all the members as regards their existence, as the Philosopher says.Aristotle, *On the Generation of Animals* 2.6, 743b25‒30. But the head is the principle of the animal powers that pertain to sensation and motion. And because when we say Christ is the principle of the members of the Church, we do not intend this with respect to natural existence, insofar as they are men, but with respect to faith and charity, through which the members of the Church are united, therefore he is more fittingly called the “head” rather than the “heart.”  

Reply Obj. 6: In the name “head” is indicated something’s principle and origin, but in the name “member” is indicated being partial, which is regnant to the notion of a first origin. For in a first origin are, by power, everything found in the things that derive from the origin; and therefore it cannot be called a “member,” since it does not receive any influence from something else. For in a natural body the head not only influences the other members, but it also receives from them such as nourishment and other aid, and this is why it is called both a head and a fellow member. But Christ, as man, does not receive anything from the other members of the Church, but from God alone. Whence he cannot be called a “fellow member” insofar as he is called the head of the Church in virtue of his humanity. However, he can be called a “member” in virtue of his humanity insofar as he is the head of the Church in virtue of his divinity. And in this way the Apostle says: *you are the body of Christ and individually members of it* (1 Cor 12:26).  

Article 2  

***Whether Christ, insofar as he is a man, is the head of the angels***

***Quaestiuncula 1***

To the second we proceed as follows.Parallels: *In IV Sent.*, d. 9, a. 2, resp. 5; d. 49, q. 4, aa. 4, 5; *ST* III.8.4; *De veritate,* q. 29, a. 4; *Compend. theol.*, ch. 214; *Super 1 Cor.* 11, lect. 1; *Super Ephes.* 1, lect. 8. It appears that Christ, insofar as he is a man, is not the head of the angels.  

Obj. 1: For having a common form with its members is of the notion of a head, as was said. But in his nature Christ does not have a common form with the angels, since *it is not with angels that he is concerned but with the descendants of Abraham* (Heb 2:16). Therefore he is not the head of the angels.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, in Hebrews 2 Christ is called the head of men insofar as they receive from him a certain in-flowing, or influence. But the angels, since they are blessed, do not need anything to flow into them. Therefore Christ, insofar as he is a man, is not the head of the angels.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, as is said in *On the Motion of the Heart*,Moos says that *De motu cordis* was a treatise composed by Averroes; however, this may in fact refer to a treatise of the same title composed by Alfredus de Sareshel (c.1210), edited by C. Baeumker (BGPM v. 23). the intelligences are receptive of their illuminations from God in the first reception, but souls in the second. But what receives in the first reception does not receive from what receives in the second reception. Therefore the angels, who are called “intelligences,” do not receive from any soul, and thus neither from Christ as man. Therefore Christ, as man, is not the head of the angels.  

On the contrary, Colossians 2:10 speaks of one *who is the head of all rule and authority*, meaning Christ insofar as he is a man; and thus Christ insofar as he is a man is the head of the angels.  

Furthermore, Dionysius saysPseudo-Dionysius, *Celestial Hierarchy*, ch. 7, para. 3 (PG 3:210). that the seraphim ask of, and learn from, and are illuminated by Jesus Christ. Therefore he influences the angels, and thus he appears to be their head.  

Furthermore, one Church is established from the angels and men. But there cannot be two heads of one body. Therefore Christ is also the head of the angels.  

Quaestiuncula 2  

Obj. 1: Moreover, it appears that he is not the head of all men.Parallel: *ST* III.8.3. For one head refers to one body. But all men do not come together in any numerically one thing, since they themselves are different, and the faith, hope, and love by which they are connected to each other are numerically different in diverse men. Therefore he is not the head of all men.  

Obj. 2: Further, Christ’s true body is a figure of Christ’s mystical body. But in the true body there is no impure member. Therefore, since many men are corrupted by sin, it appears that Christ is not their head.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, Christ’s entire true body was glorified. But there are also many in a state of grace who will never be in a state of glory, since they do not persevere. Therefore Christ is not the head of all.  

Obj. 4: Furthermore, since a head is the members’ principle, it does not follow the other members. But many saints came before Christ’s Incarnation. Therefore Christ, insofar as he is a man, is not the head of all men.  

On the contrary (5), Christ made satisfaction for the whole of human nature. But all men share in human nature. Therefore Christ influences all of them, and thus he appears to be the head of all.  

Furthermore (6), all of us will rise through Christ. But this would not be unless he were the head of all. Therefore he is the head of all, insofar as he is a man.  

Quaestiuncula 3  

Obj. 1: Furthermore, it appears that he is the head only of souls.Parallel: *ST* III.8.2. For there must be continuity between a head and its members. But the bodies of other men are not continuous with Christ’s body. Therefore he is also not the head of men, with respect to the body, but with respect to their souls only.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, insofar as he is a head, he influences the spiritual senses. But bodies are not receptive of senses of this sort. Therefore Christ, as man, is not the head of men with respect to the body, but with respect to souls only.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, we do not differ from the beasts with respect to the body, but only with respect to the rational soul. But Christ as man is not said to be the head of the irrational animals. Therefore he is not our head with respect to bodies.  

On the contrary, he *will change our lowly body to be like his glorious body* (Phil 3:21), and thus he will influence us with respect to the body. Therefore he is not only the head of our souls.  

Furthermore, he is our head insofar as we are of the same form in nature. But human nature consists not only in souls but in the soul and body together. Therefore Christ is our head with respect to the body and not only with respect to our souls.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 1  

To the first question, I answer that Christ, insofar as he is a man, is the head of the angels, yet not properly, nor in the same way as he is of men, on account of two of the conditions [noted above]. First, with respect to conformity of nature, since he belongs with men in his nature, even in virtue of his species, whereas he does not belong with the angels in virtue of species, but in virtue of the genus of intellectual nature. Second, with respect to influence, since he does not influence the angels by removing an impediment, or by meriting grace or praying for them, since they are already blessed. Rather, in those things that pertain to the acts of their hierarchies, insofar as one angel illuminates another, he purifies and perfects them, as was said in Book II*In II Sent.*, d. 9, q. 1, a. 2.; for they receive this from Christ much more eminently.  

Reply Obj. 1: Although he is not conformed to them in the nature of species, he is nonetheless conformed to them in the nature of genus, as was said.  

Reply Obj. 2: The beatitude of the angels does not exclude that one receive it from another, or even from Christ. Rather, it excludes the need for receiving, since I grant that everything they need is already in them, lest they suffer any impoverishment.  

Reply Obj. 3: This is understood with respect to the reception of natural properties. And yet Christ’s soul, insofar as it was united to God even with respect to his natural properties, did not receive any light from the angels, but immediately from God.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 2  

To the second question, it should be said that in a natural body we find a fourfold union of its members with each other. The first is with respect to their conformity in nature, since all the members are put together from the same like parts, and are of one notion, as a hand and a foot from flesh and bone; in this way the members are said to be one in genus or species. The second is through their connection with each other through nerves and joints; in this way they are said to be one by continuity. The third is insofar as the vital spirit and powers of the soul are diffused through the whole body. The fourth is insofar as all the members are perfected by the soul, which is one in number in all the members.  

And these four unions are found in the mystical body. The first, insofar as all its members are of one nature either in species or genus. The second, insofar as they are connected to each other through the faith, since thereby they are connected in one object of belief. The third, insofar as they are vivified through grace and charity. The fourth, insofar as the Holy Spirit is in them, who is the ultimate and principal perfection of the entire mystical body, as the soul is in the natural body.  

However, the first of the stated unions is not a union without qualification, for that in which such a union exists is not one in number, as it is in the three later unions. For they are united through faith and charity in numerically one object of belief and love; likewise, numerically one Holy Spirit fills all men.  

Therefore men without faith do not pertain to the union of the body of the Church insofar as it is one without qualification, and therefore in reference to these, Christ is head only in potency—namely, insofar as they are able to be united to the body. However, believers who are sinners pertain to the unity of the Church in a certain way, insofar as they are connected to it through faith, which is a material unity. Yet they can be called members properly speaking only the way dead members are so called—that is, equivocally.  

And because the unity of the body consists in that of its members, therefore some say that they do not pertain to the unity of the Church's body, even though they pertain to the unity of the Church. And just as activities that are toward another can in a certain way happen through dried-out members, such as striking something or the like, yet they are not the activities that belong to the soul in those members, so too evil men do not receive the operations of spiritual life from the Holy Spirit; yet the Holy Spirit through them might activate the spiritual life in others, insofar as they administer the sacraments to others, or teach others.  

But believers in a state of grace are united with respect to the third union, which is formal relative to the second, and with respect to the fourth, which completes the whole. And therefore Christ is said to be the head of these properly.  

Reply Obj. 1: In a natural body, the powers diffused throughout its members differ numerically in their essences, but they agree in numerically their one root, and beyond this, they have numerically one ultimate form. In the same way also all the members of the mystical body have for their ultimate completion the Holy Spirit, who is numerically one in all; and the very charity diffused in them by the Holy Spirit, although it differs in diverse men by its essence, yet they agree in one root numerically. However, the root of an activity is, properly speaking, its very object—from which it takes its species; and therefore, insofar as the one loved and believed by all is numerically one, in this way is united the faith and charity of all in that numerically one root—not only the first [root], who is the Holy Spirit, but also the proximate one, which is its proper object.  

Reply Obj. 2: Through Christ’s true body are signified those who are members of his mystical body, but not those who are members equivocally—that is, only according to a likeness and position.  

Reply Obj. 3: Something is not judged to be a member in virtue of what can happen to it, but in virtue of what it is. Whence a hand, before it is severed, is a member even if in the future it will be severed. Likewise, one who is a state of grace is a member, even if later he will be severed.  

Reply Obj. 4: Although Christ was not yet incarnate in reality at the time of the fathers of the Old Testament, yet the Incarnation itself was already in God’s ordination, and in their faith, in virtue of which they were justified. For the times have changed, but the faith has not, as Augustine says.Augustine, *Enarrationes in Psalmos*, Ps 50, para. 17 (CCSL 38; PL 36:596). But nevertheless there was not as great an influence before the Incarnation as there is now, since then the obstacle had not yet been removed, nor had the sacraments of grace been offered, as they are now.  

Reply Sed Contra 5: Christ made satisfaction for the whole human race sufficiently, yet not effectively, since not all men become participants in this satisfaction. This is due to their defiance, not due to an insufficiency in the satisfaction itself.  

Reply Sed Contra 6: The resurrection to natural life will be common to all men, but not the resurrection to the life of glory. For evil men will have a conformity with the members of the Church only in natural life, but not in the life of glory. Whence, on account of this it does not follow that they are members, properly speaking.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 3  

To the third question, it should be said that Christ is the head of men with respect to their souls and bodies, but principally of their souls, and secondarily of their bodies. This is by reason of the conformity to his members, which is through the assumption of human nature, since he assumed the body by the mediation of the soul. It is also so by reason of the spiritual influence, which reaches the body by the mediation of the soul, insofar as the body is the instrument of a soul acting according to grace. And due to this, an order is left within the body toward a glorious resurrection.  

Reply Obj. 1: Our bodies in a certain way do have a continuity with the body of Christ—not a continuity according to quantity, or one according to natural perfection, but insofar as the Holy Spirit dwells within us, who was most fully in Christ: *do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you?* (1 Cor 6:19).  

Reply Obj. 2: Although the body does not immediately receive the spiritual influence, still it receives it by the mediation of the soul, as was said.  

Reply Obj. 3: We differ from the beasts even in virtue of our bodies, insofar as our bodies are perfected by the rational soul, unlike the bodies of the beasts.  

Question 3  

***The grace of the union***

***Next, we ask about the grace of union, and regarding this two things are asked:***

first, whether the grace of union is a certain created grace;  

second, how is this grace related to Christ’s other graces.  

Article 1  

***Whether the grace of union is created***

To the first we proceed as follows.Parallels: above, d. 2, q. 2, a. 2; d. 5, q. 1, a. 1, resp. 1; d. 7, q. 2, a. 1; *ST* III.2.7. It appears that the grace of union was not created.  

Obj. 1: For created grace is a habitual grace. But habit is ordered to activity. Therefore since the union in the person was not according to an activity, but according to existence, it appears that the grace of union is not a created grace.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, everyone of us is united to God through a created grace. Therefore if the grace of union in Christ were a created grace, then we would not be united to God in a different way than he is. And thus, the error of Nestorius would follow.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, the union of his humanity to the divinity is according to his body and soul. But the body is not receptive of a created grace. Therefore the grace of union is not some created grace.  

Obj. 4: In things that happen through a miracle, no disposition on the side of the thing done is required—rather, the whole is due to the infinite power of the agent, in the way that God was able to make wine from a stone as much as from water. But the union of the humanity with the divinity was the most miraculous of all. Therefore no created grace is required that would dispose toward this union.  

Obj. 5: Furthermore, no mediating accident falls between the person and the nature, just as none falls between form and matter; for substantial existence, to which there is no prior accidental existence, is in virtue of substantial form and matter. But the union of the human nature with God is as of a nature to a person. Therefore no medium falls there. But created grace is an accident. Therefore the grace of union is not a created grace.  

On the contrary (6), Augustine saysAugustine, *Enchiridion de fide, spe, et caritate*, ch. 11/35‒36 (CCSL 46; PL 40:249‒250). that whatever belongs to the Son of God by nature also belongs to the Son of man by grace. But to be God belongs to the Son of God by nature. Therefore it belongs to the Son of man by grace. And this is the grace of union, which was not always. Therefore it is a created grace.  

Furthermore (7), union in the person is greater than union through fruition. But the human nature can be exalted to the union of fruition only through a habitual grace. Therefore much less [is this needed] for union in the person.  

Furthermore (8), since [God] is in every created thing by his essence, presence, and power, and in holy souls by grace, he is in Christ’s soul either in a different way or not. If not, then [his soul] is no more assumed by the divine person than is Peter’s soul. But if in a different way, but God in and of himself is related to all things in the same way, whereas things are related to him in diverse ways, as is said in *On the Causes*,Proclus (pseudo-Aristotle) *De Causes*, prop. 20. and insofar as diverse things are related to him in diverse ways, so is he compared to diverse things in diverse ways—therefore it is necessary that in Christ’s soul there is some other disposition by which God is in him through the union. Therefore there must be some other created grace there.  

Furthermore (9), when the Holy Spirit is given to men, some new effect is understood to be in the created thing. Therefore likewise, insofar as the person of the Son is united to flesh, some new effect must be understood, and thus it appears that the grace of union is something created.  

I answer that, as the Master said above,*In II Sent.,* d. 26, prologue. “grace” is said in two ways. In one way, meaning the grace of the one giving gratuitously, which is God’s gratuitous will itself giving something without merit. In the other way, “grace” is said to be a certain gift given gratuitously.  

Therefore, according to the first way, the divine will itself uniting the human nature, without any [preceding] merits, in the person to the Son of God, is said to be the “grace of union.” And thus, the grace of union is an uncreated grace.  

However, if “grace” is being said as a gift given gratuituously, then the “grace of union” can be understood in two ways.  

In one way, it could name the union itself, which is something created, as was said above.Above*,* d. 5, q. 1, a. 1.  

In another way, the “grace of union” can be understood as some quality causing a disposition for the union. But something can dispose someone to a perfection in three ways.  

In one way, such that a medium falls between the subject and that perfection, as an instrument of that perfection, as transparency disposes something for light. And in this way, nothing can dispose a nature toward union, since the nature is united to the person immediately as to existence. In another way, a thing disposes something to a form as preparing the matter for the reception of the form, such that it pre-exists in the matter before the form does, in the order of coming to be, but not in the order of being, as heat disposes something to the form of fire, not because a medium falls between the form and the matter, but insofar as the matter is made appropriate to the form of fire through the presence of the heat. And neither in this way can something dispose a nature to a union in the person, since the human nature, according as it is this kind of nature, can be assumed by the divine person.  

In the third way something disposes something else to a perfection as contributing to the goodness and suitability of that perfection, as the ornamentation of a person contributes to the regal dignity. In virtue of this it is said that *the form of Priam is worthy of command.*Porphyry, *Isagoge*, ch. 2, para. 613, although the Greek more literally says, “the form is first worthy of command”; the expression is attributed to Euripides. And in this way everything that is suitable to the human nature united to God—whether it is on the part of the body or on the part of the soul—can be called the “grace of union.” And in this way the grace of union is also something created.  

Nevertheless, the saints who speak about the “grace of union” appear to understand it in the first way, insofar as the divine will giving even without merit is called a “grace.” And in this way we must respond to each of the objections on both sides.  

Reply Obj. 1: A created grace disposes to an activity as a principle of the activity, but to the union not as a principle of the union, but as making the union fitting  

Reply Obj. 2: Our union with God is through a created habitual grace as through a cause, and as through that wherein the union exists. For the soul is conformed to and united with God in the very likeness of grace. But if the “grace of union” is being said as something created, it is not that wherein the union exists; for the union is in the person, and not merely in some likeness. Nor does it even cause the unity, but it follows on the unity in the person, insofar as the union itself is being called the “grace of union.” Or it is what befits the united nature, if the habitual grace is being called the “grace of union.” And therefore the grace of Christ and that of us are not alike.  

Reply Obj. 3: That habitual grace in a certain manner even overflows to the body, insofar as it is an instrument of the soul, as was said above.Above, q. 2, a. 2, resp. 3. Whence, just as in the body perfected by the rational soul there remains after death an order to resurrection, and beyond that to glory if the soul that was its perfection had grace, so too Christ’s body, insofar it is perfected for such a soul, has a certain order to the union.  

Reply Obj. 4: That argument follows for a disposition according to the intermediate mode.  

Reply Obj. 5: That argument follows according to the first mode of dispostion, as is manifest through itself.  

Reply Sed Contra 6: When it is said that to be God belongs to the Son of man through the grace of union, either the “grace of union” is understood to mean the very union made gratuitously—which did not always exist, and it was created—or it is understood as the gratuitous will of God itself, which indeed always existed in itself, but its effect did not always exist.  

Reply Sed Contra 7: Union through fruition is a union through activity, and since human nature is capable of a perfect activity only through some mediating habit, therefore there must be some habitual grace that is the principle of this union. But union in the person is a union to existence. Now, between the human nature and the existence that it has in the person there can fall no intermediate that would be a principle of its existence. Therefore there cannot be some other grace there that is the principle of that union, either as perfective or as dispositive of it, except in the way said in the body of the article.  

Reply Sed Contra 8: The fact that God is in Christ’s soul, or in the assumed nature in a way other than he is in the other created things, is not through some added disposition, but through the divine person’s very existence, which is communicated to the human nature.  

Reply Sed Contra 9: The Holy Spirit is said to be given anew not in virtue of his own change, but in virtue of a change in the created thing, which occurs in the reception of his gift. And so too the Son of God is said to be united to the human nature not through a change in the Son of God, but through a change in the human nature—namely, its exaltation, not to some created gift, but to the divine person’s uncreated existence itself.  

Article 2  

***Whether the grace of union is the same as the grace of the head***

***Quaestiuncula 1***

To the second we proceed as follows.Parallels: *ST* III.8.5; *De veritate,* q. 29. a. 5. It appears that the grace of union is the same as the grace of the head.  

Obj. 1: For Christ is called the “head of the Church” insofar as f*rom his fullness we have all received*. But his fullness is in virtue of the fact that he is the only begotten of the Father, as is said in John 1. Therefore, since he is the only begotten of the Father through the grace of union, it appears that the grace of union is the same as the grace of the head.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, a middle communicates with its extremes. But Christ, insofar as he is the head of the Church, is a middle between God and men. For Christ is the head of man, and God is the head of Christ.1 Cor 11:3. Therefore he is the head insofar as he communicates with both. But he has this through the grace of union, through which he is God and man. Therefore the grace of union is the grace of the head.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, Christ is the head of the Church insofar as he exceeds men. But he exceeds human nature through the grace of union. Therefore he is the head through the grace of union.  

On the contrary, the grace of the head belongs to Christ in connection with others. But the grace of union belongs to him in himself alone. Therefore one union is not the other.  

Furthermore, the grace of the head was established from the creation of the world, from which men began to be his members. But the grace of union was not. Therefore the one is not the other.  

Quaestiuncula 2  

Obj. 1: Moreover, it appears that the grace of the head is the same as the grace of the individual himself.Parallels: *ST* III.8.5; *De veritate*, q. 29, a. 5. For that by which someone is a knower and a teacher of others is the same knowledge. And according to the Philosopher,Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* 5.2, 1130b30‒1131a5. the virtue by which someone is perfected in himself and the legal justice by which he is perfected in the order to the common good are the same virtue. Therefore by the same habit Christ was perfected in himself and poured his perfection into others.  

Obj. 2: Furthermore, grace is the life of the soul. But of one subject there is but one life. Therefore so too one grace. And thus the same thing follows as before.  

Obj. 3: Furthermore, just as a head is compared to its members, so are the other members to the head. But one does not distinguish the grace of Christ within the members, through which they are perfected in themselves, from the grace in virtue of which they are members. Therefore neither within Christ should one distinguish the grace through which he is the head from his individual grace in virtue of which he is perfected in himself.  

On the contrary (4), just as through Adam sin entered the world, so through Christ grace and justice.See Rom 5. But in Adam his actual sin, which is an individual’s sin, is other than original sin, which is passed on to his posterity. Therefore in Christ the two graces should be distinguished.  

Furthermore (5), diverse habits are ordered to diverse acts. But the act of doing what is right is other than the act of exerting influence on others. Therefore the habit of grace is also other. And so the same as before.  

Quaestiuncula 3  

Obj. 1: Moreover, it appears that the grace of the individual person precedes the grace of union.Parallels: *ST* III.6.6; 7.13; *Compend. theol*., ch. 214. For the grace of the individual person contributes to the union’s congruity. But the soul contributes to the congruity of the body's union, and it is understood as united to the body prior to the united soul and body being understood as united to the divine person. Therefore likewise also the grace of the individual in the human nature is prior in understanding to the grace of union.  

Obj. 2: Again, it appears that it is prior to the grace of the head. For something perfect in itself is considered to be prior to its bestowing perfection on another. But the grace of the individual is that by which he is perfect in himself, whereas the grace of the head is that in virtue of which he bestows perfection on another. Therefore the grace of the individual is prior to the grace of the head.  

Obj. 3: Again, it appears that the grace of union is prior to the grace of the head. For he is the head from the fact that he justifies. But he justifies through the faith in the Incarnation, as was said above.source? Therefore the Incarnation precedes the grace of the head.  

On the contrary (4), the grace of the head was established before the Incarnation, but the grace of union was not, and neither was the grace of the individual person. Therefore the grace of the head is prior to the other two.  

Furthermore (5), it appears that the grace of the union is prior to the grace of the individual. For he was full of grace and truth from the fact that he was the only begotten of the Father. But he was the only begotten of the Father through the grace of the union. Therefore he had the grace of the individual person through the grace of union. And thus the same as before follows.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 1  

To the first question, I answer that if the habitual grace in a certain way disposing to the union is called the “grace of union,” then the grace of union, the grace of the head, and the grace of this individual man are the same in essence, differing only in notion. For insofar as it perfected Christ’s soul with respect to meritorious acts, it is said to be the “grace of this individual man”; insofar as the fullness of this grace was so great that it could overflow to others, it is called the “grace of the head”; and insofar as so full a grace was fittingly present in the assumed nature, it can in a certain way be called the “grace of union.” But if “grace” is being used to mean God’s grauitous will, then it is again certain that it is one. However, taking the “grace of union” in the other ways, it is certain that it differs from the others by its essence—except insofar as the head is understood as being God.  

Reply Obj. 1: Although from the fact that he is the only begotten he has the fullness of the head, still the union itself is not that fullness.  

Reply Obj. 2: God is said to be Christ’s head and Christ the head of men according to different notions, as was said above.Above, q. 2, a. 1, ad 4. And further, insofar as he is a man he is an intermediate and partakes of the nature of both extremes, insofar as he is more like God than other men in virtue of the greater grace that he has.  

Reply Obj. 3: Not only does he exceed other men insofar as he is God, but insofar as he has a fuller created grace than all other men.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 2  

***To the second question the solution is clear from things said.***

Reply Obj. 1–2: And similarly for the first two objections, which proceed for a unity in essence.  

Reply Obj. 3: Although the grace of a member has diverse relations, still from the fact that he is a member the fullness is not required more abundantly. And therefore the grace of the member is not distinguished from the grace of the individual.  

Reply Sed Contra 4: The actual sin of Adam was the cause of the original sin in himself and others, and again, those two sins are not in reference to the same thing, but one belongs to the nature through itself, and the other to the person through himself, as was said in Book II.*In II Sent.*, d. 31, q. 1, a. 1. However, this is not the way it is in the proposed matter, since neither grace is the cause of the other, and both are in reference to the person of Christ by the mediation of his human nature. And therefore it is unnecessary that they differ by essence.  

Reply Sed Contra 5: In virtue of those acts, he merited for everyone, and exerted influence on others, since in his meriting he made satisfaction for others, and merited grace for others, in virtue of which he is called our head.  

Response to Quaestiuncula 3  

To the third question, it should be said that those three graces are attributed to Christ according to different considerations about him. For he can be considered in himself or in comparison to others. If he is considered in himself, then this is either insofar as he is God—and then the grace of union is attributed to him—or insofar as he is a man—and thus the grace of the individual is attributed to him. But if he is considered in comparison to others, then the grace of the head is attributed to him. And because someone is considered in himself prior to him in comparison to others, therefore the grace in virtue of which he is the head follows upon the other two graces. And because every grace and perfection of humanity is owed to him in virtue of that man being united to God in the person, therefore the grace of union precedes, according to the order of nature and understanding, the individual grace, even though these three graces are together within the same time.  

Reply Obj. 1: The soul completes the notion of human nature, and therefore the union of the soul with the body is understood before the union of the human nature with the Godhead. For considering something in itself is prior to considering it as united to another. But grace contributes nothing to the notion of human nature, since as such it can be assumed. Therefore it is not similar.  

Reply Obj. 2–3: These are clear.  

Reply Sed Contra 4: Even among the ancient patriarchs the grace of the head was active only insofar as the grace of the union was presupposed in their faith. For they were justified through their faith in the Incarnation.  

Reply Sed Contra 5: This is clear.  

Exposition of the text  

*But in the saints there is, as it were, touch alone*.*Sent.* III, 13.1(38).2. This appears to be false. For Origen, commenting on Leviticus,Origen, *Super Leviticum*, homil. 3, n. 7 (PG 12:432). distinguishes five spiritual senses, saying that vision is spiritual insofar as we see God, hearing insofar as we hear what he says, smell through which we smell the good scent of Christ, taste insofar as we taste his sweetness, and touch insofar as we feel, with John, the Word of life. Now, all of these are in all the saints. Therefore they do not have touch alone. To this one should say that the spiritual senses can be distinguished through a likeness to the bodily senses, and in this way they are in all the saints, as Origen says, or through a likeness to certain properties of the senses, insofar as touch is necessary but the others are not. And in this latter way, since in the other saints are all things that are necessary for salvation, whereas in Christ are all things that pertain simply to the perfection of grace, therefore in Christ are said to be present all the senses, but in the others touch alone.  

*To them was given the spirit according to measure*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).2. Against this, James 1:5 says God *gives to all men generously*, and a Gloss says, *he gives all things without measure, since his gifts are not according to measure*.source? And to this it should be said that “to be given with measure” can be taken either from the largess of the giver—and thus he gives to no one with measure, because he gives to all from his infinite largess—or from the capacity of the recipient—and thus he gives to each with measure, because he gives to no one beyond what has the capacity for—or according to the notion of the thing given—and thus he does not give to Christ with measure, since his grace, according to the notion of grace, is not limited, but he gives to others with measure, as is clear from things said.  

*Not according to essence, but according to likeness*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).2. Against this: for the same reason Peter can be called a head, since every saint is like another in grace. To this it should be said that here “likeness” is being taken actively, namely insofar as Christ likens others to himself in grace, as was said, which does not belong to another.  

*Christ may be said to have made progress in the sight of men and in what he revealed of his own senses*.*Sent*. III, 13.1(38).9. It appears that this was not Ambrose’s intention, since nothing is proved through this. For in this way even the wisdom of God progresses, insofar as it reveals itself more and more. To this it should be said that, although God’s wisdom shows itself more than it did before in certain effects, still it itself is always believed to be equal, whereas this was not so of Christ’s wisdom, who appeared at one time to be more wise than he was before. And therefore he says two things: *in the sight of men and in what he revealed of his senses*.
