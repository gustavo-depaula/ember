## The Heart as Symbol

In Sacred Scripture, the heart is far more than a physical organ. It is the seat of the person — the hidden center where thought, will, memory, and love converge. When God tells Samuel that He "looks at the heart" (1 Sam 16:7), He reveals that the heart is where the truth of a person is found.

The Sacred Heart of Jesus, then, is not merely a devotion to a physical heart. It is devotion to the whole interior life of Christ — His thoughts, His desires, His sorrows, His love. The physical heart is the *symbol* that makes this invisible reality accessible to us.

The Church has always understood this. The heart of flesh, pierced on the cross and from which flowed blood and water, is the sign of a love that is both divine and human, both infinite and intimately personal. To honor the Sacred Heart is to honor the love that moved God to become man, to suffer, and to remain with us in the Eucharist.

Three elements of the traditional Sacred Heart image carry this symbolism:

**The flames** represent the burning charity of divine love — the love that, as Jesus told St. Margaret Mary, "can no longer contain itself" and must pour itself out upon humanity.

**The crown of thorns** encircling the heart signifies the sins of mankind that wound this love. It is a reminder that devotion to the Sacred Heart is inseparable from reparation — the desire to console Christ for the ingratitude He receives.

**The cross** surmounting the heart points to the sacrifice of Calvary, where Christ's love was most fully revealed. The heart was pierced so that we might enter into it.
