A litany is meant to be prayed, not merely read. Its power lies in the rhythm — the steady alternation of invocation and response that draws the mind into focused attention and the heart into deepening trust.

**Praying alone.** When praying a litany by yourself, take both parts: read the invocation aloud or silently, then speak the response. Do not rush. Let each title or petition settle in your mind before moving to the next. The repetition of the response — "Pray for us," "Have mercy on us" — is not idle; it is the heart's refrain, renewed with each new facet of the mystery.

**Praying with others.** The litany comes most fully alive in community. One person serves as leader, reading the invocations, while the others respond together. This call-and-response pattern creates a shared act of prayer that is both simple enough for anyone to join and deep enough to sustain contemplation. In families, the litany is an excellent way to pray together — children can participate from a young age simply by learning the response.

**Posture.** Traditionally, litanies are prayed kneeling, especially the Litany of the Saints at the Easter Vigil and ordinations. In private devotion, kneel if you are able; otherwise, any reverent posture suffices. What matters is the disposition of the heart.

**Pace.** Pray slowly enough that each invocation can be received, but steadily enough to maintain the litany's characteristic momentum. The rhythm itself becomes a form of meditation.
