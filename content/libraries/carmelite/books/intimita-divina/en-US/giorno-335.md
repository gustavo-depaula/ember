## 335 — OUR DUTIES
*XXII Sunday after Pentecost*

### Presence of God

Teach me, Lord, to fulfill all my duties in homage to your sovereign Majesty.

### Meditation

Today’s liturgy may be summarized in the well-known phrase of Jesus that we read in the Gospel (Mt 22:15-21): «Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar’s, and to God the things that are God’s.» In other words: fulfill exactly your duties toward God and toward neighbor, giving each his due.

The Epistle (Phil 1:6-11) presents St. Paul as a model of charity toward those whom God has entrusted to our care. «I hold you in my heart,» the Apostle writes to the Philippians, «you who share in grace with me, both in my chains and in the defense and confirmation of the Gospel.»

Paul feels deeply his spiritual fatherhood toward the souls he has begotten in Christ. Even from afar he feels responsible for their perseverance in good; he supports them with paternal affection and wise counsel: «I am confident that He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion unto the day of Christ Jesus.»

He does not want them discouraged by his absence: he is only a poor instrument; God alone is the true guide of souls, and God will continue the work begun. As for him, they can be certain he never ceases to love them: «God is my witness, how I long for you all with the affection of Christ Jesus.»

St. John Chrysostom says that Paul’s heart is Christ’s heart, because his great love for souls makes him so like his Master. So it should be with every apostle’s heart. When God places a soul on our path and asks us to care for it, we can no longer be indifferent; that soul is now bound to ours, and we must feel responsible for helping it to the end.

After speaking of concern for those entrusted to us, the Epistle also calls us to charity in general toward every neighbor: «that your charity may more and more abound in knowledge and in all discernment.» It is a charity ever more delicate in understanding others, ever finer in adapting to their mentality, needs, and defects; a charity that urges us, as St. Paul says, to «approve what is better,» so that we may be «sincere and blameless for the day of Christ.»

The Gospel also teaches the Christian’s duty toward civil authority. The insidious question—whether it is lawful to pay tribute to Caesar—gives Jesus occasion to resolve the relation between civic duties and religious duties. He asks for a coin and says: «Whose image and inscription is this?» «Caesar’s,» they answer. And He replies: «Render therefore to Caesar what is Caesar’s and to God what is God’s.»

There is no opposition between the rights of political authority and the rights of God, since «there would be no authority unless it had been given from above» (cf. Jn 19:11). Legitimate political authority comes from God and must be respected as a reflection of divine authority.

Therefore the Christian must fulfill all duties of a good citizen and obey political authority, except when it commands things contrary to God’s law; then it no longer represents divine authority, and as St. Peter says, «We must obey God rather than men» (Acts 5:29).

We must not think that because we are dedicated to apostolate or consecrated works we are exempt from civic duties. On the contrary, Catholics should excel here as well. Emperors, kings, statesmen, and soldiers honored by the Church as saints remind us that holiness is possible everywhere and can be achieved even in service of the State, because here too one serves God in creatures.

By saying to give Caesar what is Caesar’s, Jesus teaches us to give the State all that belongs to its sphere, namely all that concerns temporal public order and common good. But Jesus does not stop there: «and to God what is God’s.»

If money that bears Caesar’s image must be returned to Caesar, how much more must our soul, which bears God’s image, be returned to God. To say that we must give God our soul is to say we must give Him everything, for everything we have received from Him.

In this sense, fulfilling our duties toward neighbor—toward equals or inferiors, toward ecclesiastical or civil superiors—is fulfilling our duties toward God: returning what He has given us, subjecting our freedom to His and placing our will at the service of His will.

### Colloquy

«O my God, since I am Yours for so many reasons and have so many obligations to serve You, never again permit fault, Satan, or the world to usurp, even in the least, what is wholly Yours. But take, if it pleases You, full and absolute possession of my being and my life. O my God, here I give myself entirely to You, promising to exist only for You and to will, think, say, do, and suffer nothing except for love of You, today, tomorrow, and always» (St. John Eudes).

«O my Lord Jesus, You gave me Yourself, and from me You ask only my heart. But, my Lord, what is this heart of mine while You are so great? If I had a heart that by itself were worth more than all the hearts of men together and all the affections of angels, and were so vast that it could contain at once many spiritual and bodily things—yes, more than the highest heaven—I should still owe to consecrate it wholly and entirely to You; and even then it would be but a small gift to so great a Lord, almost nothing.

«What then shall I give You but this little spark of heart that I find in myself? Yet for me it is a great thing that You deign to accept my heart. Would I not be foolish if from now on I consecrated it to any creature, when my God wants it for Himself? I no longer want it to remain in me; I want it to rest entirely in You, who created it that it might praise You.

«It is better that my heart remain in eternal joy, in divine majesty, in infinite goodness, than in my fragility; in your holiness rather than in my iniquity» (St. Bonaventure).
