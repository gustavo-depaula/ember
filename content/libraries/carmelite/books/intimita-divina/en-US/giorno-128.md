## 128 — SUFFERING AND ABANDONMENT

### Presence of God

— O Lord, teach me to suffer with simplicity, without useless returns upon myself, but by abandoning myself to Your divine will.

### Meditation

1 — The secret of learning to suffer virtuously
lies largely in forgetting oneself and abandoning oneself; forgetting oneself and one's own suffering and abandoning oneself to God.

Whoever folds in upon his own sufferings, concentrating
his attention on them, makes himself incapable of bearing them with serenity and courage. "Sufficient for each
day is its own trouble" (Mt. 6, 34), Jesus said;
let us therefore strive to bear in peace, day by day,
moment by moment those pains and sorrows that God places on
our path, without thinking about how much we suffered
yesterday, without worrying about how much we shall suffer tomorrow. Even when suffering is intense, let us not overvalue it,
let us not give it too much importance, let us not allow ourselves to be taken by that somewhat morbid tendency to caress our
suffering, to linger over contemplating it, analyzing it,
and weighing it in all its aspects. In this way we would paralyze our spirit of sacrifice, our
capacity for acceptance and action, often rendering ourselves useless to ourselves and to others. Whoever is too sensitive and too occupied
with his own sufferings often becomes insensitive and indifferent to others' sufferings.

To react against these tendencies of selfishness,
rightly called "the wormwood of Christian affliction" (Fr. Faber), one must forget oneself, one must go out of
oneself and out of the circle of one's own sufferings, and attend instead to those of others, striving to alleviate them. This
is a most effective means for recovering, in moments
of discouragement, the strength to carry one's own crosses.

One must always think - as is true - that we are
never alone in suffering, that if our sufferings are great,
there is never lacking someone who has greater ones; one must think that
our pains are a drop compared to the sea of
sorrows in which humanity sails, and are almost nothing
compared to Jesus' Passion.

Whoever is too preoccupied with what he suffers ends up
exasperating his own pain, drowning in it,
stifling in himself every generous impulse. Whoever, on the contrary, knows how to forget himself, maintains balance, always capable of thinking more of others than of himself, always open to charity and generosity toward God and neighbor. The simple soul, forgetful of self, is the one that knows how to suffer with greater courage, drawing from it greater profit for its own sanctification.


2 — Despite all efforts to throw oneself beyond
one's own pain, to forget one's own sorrows,
there may come moments of such profound anguish,
of such dense darkness that the poor soul does not know how to come out of it, all the more when the horizon, instead of clearing,
becomes ever more gloomy and threatening. In these cases there remains only to make a leap into the dark,
abandoning oneself totally into God's hands. We are so poor and weak that
we always need a point of support; if the soul forgets and disengages itself from itself, it still needs
someone to sustain and think of it. This Someone
is God, who never forgets us, who knows deeply
our suffering, who knows our needs, who sees our
weakness and is always ready to help those who
take refuge in Him. We can, it is true, seek a little
comfort and help also from creatures, but let us not deceive ourselves: they will not always understand us, they will not always be
at our disposal. But if we turn to God,
we shall never be disappointed; even if He does not change our
conditions, if He does not remove our sorrows, yet interiorly - though in a hidden and silent way -
He will comfort our heart, giving us strength to continue
the journey.

"Cast your care upon God, and He will sustain you"
(Ps. 54, 23). Here is the attitude of abandonment we must have in moments of suffering, and which we must
strengthen the more suffering becomes deep.
Greater suffering must correspond to greater abandonment in God, and then we shall not be lost.

Many souls exaggerate their sufferings, dramatize them, because they do not know how to see in them God's fatherly hand,
because they do not believe enough in His divine
Providence and, consequently, do not know how to abandon themselves to
it with full confidence. If our life, with all its
circumstances, even the most painful, were not in God's
hands, we would have reason to fear; but since everything is
always in His hands, we must not be afraid, we
must not be dismayed. The soul that is sure of God and
abandons itself to Him knows how to remain calm even in the greatest sorrows,
knows how to take even tragic things with simplicity,
knows how to suffer with serenity and courage, because it is always sustained by God.

### Colloquy

"O Lord, grant that my soul may run continually
toward You without pause, always looking only to You. Consoled
or desolate, let it run without stopping at anything, run so quickly
as to have no time to look at or see the things
of earth, for the pace is most swift. Away then, for
love of You, enjoyment, rest, reliance on the judgment
of men, resting on their approval, apprehension
about bodily illness, about moral sadnesses, about prosperous or adverse success. Away, in short, with all that
is not God.

"My pains, I recognize it, have been permitted and
willed by You, my Lord, to teach me to have confidence
despite everything.

"O Lord, be my only support in apprehensions,
in weaknesses, in anguishes; be my confidant or, better,
my confidence. Divine Guest, who dwell in me,
on the throne of my heart, dwell there as master; to You belongs
the dominion, the love, the governance of all my being!

"Why should I still be troubled or afraid?
Everything is Yours, O God, and You Yourself take care of it and provide for my needs. You are infinite love and love the work of Your hands
more than it knows and can love itself. Who would dare doubt Your power, the provident and affectionate care You lavish upon Your creatures, the eternity and efficacy of Your love?

"I believe that You do everything and permit everything for my
good and my salvation, and I abandon myself to Your guidance
with confidence, with love, without anxieties,
without apprehensions, without calculations" (Bl. Mary Teresa de Soubiran).
