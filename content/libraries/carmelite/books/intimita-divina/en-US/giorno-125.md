## 125 — THE VALUE OF SUFFERING

### Presence of God

O crucified Jesus, teach me the science of the cross, teach me the value of suffering.

### Meditation

1 — Jesus' Passion teaches us concretely
that in Christian life one must be able to embrace
suffering for love of God. This lesson is hard and repugnant to our nature so inclined to enjoyment,
to joy; and yet this lesson comes from Jesus, Master
of truth and life, most loving Shepherd of our souls,
who desires only our true good. If, therefore, He teaches us to suffer, it means that in suffering
a great treasure is enclosed.
In itself suffering is an evil and cannot be lovable;
if Jesus willed to embrace it so fully, and if He
proposes it to us, inviting us to esteem and love it, it is solely in view of a higher good attainable only through it: the sublime good of the redemption and sanctification of our souls.

Although man, being a composite being, is by his
nature subject to suffering, God, through the preternatural gifts, had willed that our first parents be exempt from it.
But with sin such gifts were forever lost,
and thus suffering entered our life inevitably.
The immense sum of sorrows that afflicts humanity is
the consequence of the disorder produced by sin, and not only by original sin, but also by actual sins. And yet,
the Church sings: "felix culpa"! Why?
Because God's infinite love transforms all and knows how to draw from the twofold evil
of sin and suffering the great good of the redemption of the human race. Jesus, taking upon Himself men's sins,
also took upon Himself their consequences, that is,
suffering, and this suffering, embraced by Him throughout His life and especially in His Passion, became the means of our redemption. Suffering, consequence of
sin, becomes in Jesus and with Jesus a means to destroy
sin itself. This is why the Christian cannot
consider suffering only as an undesirable burden to
which he must necessarily submit, but - and much more -
as a means of redemption and sanctification.


2 — Suffering is an unpleasant feeling that
we experience in face of something - situation, circumstance,
etc. - that does not correspond to our tendencies, needs,
aspirations, that does not harmonize with them, that does not
favor them, but rather opposes them and places obstacles in their way. All
men are subject to suffering, but only the Christian
possesses the secret of allowing it to enter his life
without letting it destroy the harmony and happiness possible on this earth.
And this secret consists precisely in knowing how to harmonize every
kind of suffering with one's aspirations, which, for
a Christian, can never be limited to an ideal of earthly happiness. This harmonization is possible, because
what is opposition and inconvenience from one point of
view is often very fitting from another: as, for
example, physical suffering - hunger, cold, illness, etc. -
which, though inconvenient for the body, can be very
useful for attaining a moral or supernatural good, such as acquisition of virtue, progress in holiness, etc. If from a purely human standpoint certain sufferings are entirely inopportune and unusable,
from the supernatural standpoint they never are.
"All things work together for good for those who love God" (Rom. 8, 28), and even the greatest private or social calamities can become a precious and most effective means of elevation. Any suffering can therefore be harmonized with the Christian's supreme ideals: eternal salvation, holiness, God's glory, the good of souls. But this is not possible without love; indeed it will be possible only in proportion to love.
Only Jesus' love could transform the cross, a horrible instrument of torture, into a most effective instrument for God's
glory and humanity's salvation. So it will be also for
us: charity, love of God and of souls will make us
capable of embracing any suffering, harmonizing it with
our highest aspirations. In this way suffering
finds a place - and a very important place - in
our life, without however destroying harmony and serenity, without oppressing the spirit, but rather expanding it in
an ever wider breath, in an ever greater
love. Precisely thus, even while suffering, one can be happy.
This is how Jesus transformed suffering; this is the
value His Passion conferred on it.

### Colloquy

"O Lord, it costs You to give us to drink bitterness;
but You know that this is the only means to dispose us to know You
as You know Yourself, to dispose us to become like You.

"You know well, O Lord, that if You gave me only
a shadow of joy, I would cling to it with all my energy,
with all the strength of my heart; and this shadow You refuse me...
because You want my heart to be wholly for You.

"Life passes so quickly that truly it is better
to have a beautiful crown in heaven and a little suffering
down here, than to have an ordinary crown without suffering.
When I think that by one suffering borne with joy
I shall love You more for all eternity, I understand well that
if You gave me the whole universe with all its treasures,
that would not compare to the lightest suffering.
Every new suffering, every anguish of heart is like a
light breeze that carries to You, O Jesus, the fragrance of the soul
that loves You; then You smile with love and at once prepare
a new bitterness, filling the chalice to the brim, thinking
that the more the soul grows in love, the more it must
grow in suffering.

"What a privilege You grant me by giving me to suffer!
Ah, eternity will not be long enough to thank You,
for such predilection! It is a secret that You will reveal to us in the
homeland, on the day when You wipe away all our tears.

"It is You, O Lord, who come to ask me in alms
for this sadness, this trial... You need it for
souls, for my soul. O Jesus, since You made me
understand that You would give me souls through the
cross, the greater the crosses I meet, the more
the desire to suffer comes alive in me.

"I am happy not to be freed from the sufferings of
this life; suffering united to love is the only thing that seems
desirable to me in this valley of tears" (T.B.G.
Lt. 32, 50, 23, 40, 58, 224; St. 7).
