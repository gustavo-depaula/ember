## 11 — THE SPIRIT OF POVERTY

**Presence of God.** — *O Jesus, I contemplate You on the mountain while You instruct the crowds on the spirit of poverty. I too draw near, desirous of understanding more deeply the spirit of poverty.*

### Meditation

**1** — "And Jesus, opening His mouth, taught
them, saying: Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the
kingdom of heaven" (Mt. 5, 3).

Thus begins the Sermon on the Mount, and then repeatedly Jesus explains how poverty of spirit must be understood. "Do not lay up treasures on earth, where rust and moth consume and where thieves dig through
and steal; lay up instead treasures in heaven, where neither
rust nor moth consume and where thieves do not dig through nor steal... For where your treasure is, there
also is your heart" (ibid., 6, 19-21).

Consider that Jesus addressed these teachings
not only to the Apostles, but also to the disciples and to all
the crowds that followed Him. This shows that, even if not all
must make the vow of poverty - family life does not
allow it - all, however, must acquire and practice
"the spirit of poverty," that is, arrive at affective detachment
from earthly goods, so as not to place in them their
treasure, not to seek them with greed and spirit of covetousness.
Those who, like fathers and mothers of families, have the
duty to administer goods and to increase them through
honest work, must do so with order, that is, avoiding that business and material interests divert
them from attending to the interests of the soul and duties toward
God. "For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world,
if he then loses his soul?" (Mt. 16, 26). And for one who possesses
little and lives in material straits, the spirit of poverty
requires accepting serenely and with patience one's own


conditions, seeing in them an invitation to imitate more closely
the life of poor Jesus.
Moreover, reflect that the divine Master, in saying "sell
what you have and give it to the poor," joined the exercise
of poverty to that of charity. The spirit of poverty,
detaching your heart from earthly goods, must make you
generous toward the needy.


**2** — "Do not be over-anxious for your life, about
what you shall eat or drink, nor... about what you shall wear.
Is not life worth more than food and the body more
than clothing? Observe the birds of the air: they neither sow,
nor reap, nor gather into barns; yet your heavenly Father
feeds them... And why take so much trouble about clothing?
Consider how the lilies of the field grow... If then God clothes
thus the grass of the field, which today is and tomorrow is thrown into the oven, how much more will He clothe you,
O men of little faith?" (Mt. 6, 25-30).
Jesus does not forbid you to provide for your tomorrow; indeed,
through the Apostle He says to you: "If anyone is unwilling to work, neither should he eat" (II Thess. 3, 10). But
Jesus does not want that anxiety which swallows you up entirely in temporal
affairs and which shows not only immoderate attachment
to earthly things, but also little trust in divine
Providence. The divine Master points out to you that if God
has given you the greater, that is, life and body, He will also give you the
lesser, that is, the means to preserve life and body,
which are food and clothing.

The spirit of poverty will not be in you lasting and profound unless it is based on trust in divine Providence.
Only if you truly trust in God and in His word,
which will never fail forever, will you have the courage to set aside every excessive concern for temporal things.
And then Jesus' word will be fulfilled for you: "Seek
first the kingdom of God and His righteousness, and all the rest
shall be given you besides" (Mt. 6, 33).

In every state of life and in the exercise of any profession, the principal care of the Christian must be that


of serving God, of tending toward Him; all the rest is secondary.
St. Teresa of Jesus said to her daughters: "Having renounced fixed incomes, renounce also any excessive concern
for what is necessary to life, otherwise everything would be lost... Let us not fail God, and what is necessary
will never be lacking to us" (Cam. 2, 1 etc.).

### Colloquy

"O Lord, this I consider a great treasure: holy
poverty, where no thing is prepared by human
industry, but what there is is prepared by Your divine Providence... And therefore I want to pray that You make me
love with all my heart the treasure of holy poverty,
which is so noble that it has You, most high God, for servant"
(cf. St. Francis).

O Lord, heal me, I beg You, from my excessive
anxieties for life's necessities. In the silence of
my heart I seem to hear resounding Your gentle rebuke: "It is the Gentiles who seek all these things, while your

Father knows that you need all these things"
(Mt. 6, 32).

O Lord, yes, You know my necessities and are not
for me a stranger, but Father, and as such You have bound Yourself
to provide for my needs. O Lord, strengthen
my faith in Your word. Grant that my trust may be
like that of St. Francis of Assisi, who felt so secure in You
that he did not hesitate to return to his father not only
money but also clothing and to go through the world
stripped of all, happier and safer in his poverty than
the rich in their riches. O blessed poverty that has
God for servant! For You, O Lord, do not abandon
those who trust in You, but rather are pleased to be generous
and munificent with those who, leaving all for love of You,
have totally entrusted themselves to Your heavenly Providence.

O Jesus, if I cannot materially leave all
goods and earthly affairs, grant that at least I know how to leave every exaggerated solicitude and preoccupation for them.
Grant that my only preoccupation be that of loving You and serving You
with all my strength, of seeking Your friendship,
Your intimacy, union with You.

To St. Catherine of Siena one day You said: "You
think of Me and I will think of you." Deign to repeat this word also


to me and fix it in my mind and in my
heart, so that no care for material things may be capable
of distracting me from You.
