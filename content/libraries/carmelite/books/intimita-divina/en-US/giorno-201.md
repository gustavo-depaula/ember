## 201 — FEAST OF "CORPUS DOMINI"
*Thursday after the First Sunday after Pentecost*

**Presence of God.** — *"O living Bread that hides my Treasure, before You, prostrate, I adore You!" (St. John of the Cross, Poems).*

### Meditation

**1** — Stage by stage, through the liturgical year, from considering the mysteries of Jesus' life we have risen to contemplation of the Most Holy Trinity, whose feast we celebrated last Sunday. Jesus, our Mediator, our way, has taken us by the hand and brought us to the Trinity, and today it seems that the Trinity itself wishes to lead us back to Jesus, contemplated in his Eucharist. "No one comes to the Father except through me" (Jn. 14:6), Jesus said, and then added: "No one can come to me unless drawn by the Father" (Jn. 6:44). This is the itinerary of the Christian soul: from Jesus to the Father, to the Trinity; from the Trinity, from the Father, to Jesus. Jesus leads us to the Father; the Father draws us to Jesus. The Christian absolutely cannot do without Christ; He is, in the strictest sense of the word, our Pontiff, the One who made Himself bridge between God and us. Once the liturgical cycle commemorating the mysteries of the Savior is closed, the Church, as a good mother, knowing well that our spiritual life cannot subsist without Jesus, brings us to Him, living and true in the Most Blessed Sacrament of the altar. The solemnity of Corpus Domini is not the mere remembrance of an event that happened historically about two thousand years ago on the evening of the Last Supper, but the feast of a present fact, of a reality always present and always living in our midst, so that we can truly say Jesus has not "left us orphans," but has willed to remain stably with us in the integrity of his Person, with all his Humanity, with all his Divinity. In the consecrated Host we have our God (Roman Breviary). Yes, in the Eucharist there is permanently Emmanuel, God with us.

The Mass of Corpus Domini, though not treating it directly, is full of allusion to this mystery. The Introit alludes to it, recalling the honey with which God nourished his people in the desert miraculously and yet only in figure of the "living and life-giving Bread" (*Lauda Sion*) of the Eucharist. The Epistle speaks of it, reporting St. Paul's words on the institution of the Sacrament, when Jesus, "after giving thanks to God, broke and said: Take and eat; this is my Body." The Gradual sings it: "My Flesh is true food and my Blood is true drink." Then, taking up a phrase of the Sequence, we return to Jesus' own words, fittingly reported in today's Gospel: "He who eats my Flesh and drinks my Blood abides in me and I in him" (Jn. 6:57). Jesus gives Himself to us as food in order to assimilate us to Himself, to make us live in Him as He lives in the Father. The Eucharist is the sacrament of union and at the same time the clearest and most convincing proof that God calls us and urges us to intimate union with Him.

### Colloquy

"O God, O Creator, O Spirit of newness who fills your creatures with ever new spiritual gifts, You grant your elect the gift ever renewed: the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ!

"O Jesus, You instituted this Sacrament, moved neither by fear nor by desire for any profit that could come to You, but solely moved by a love that has no name except this: love without measure. You instituted this Sacrament because your love surpasses all words. Burning with love for us, You willed to give Yourself to us and placed Yourself there, in the consecrated Host, wholly and forever until the consummation of the ages. And You did this not only in memory of your death, which is our salvation, but also to remain wholly with us, always with us.

"My soul, if you wish to enter into the depths of this mystery, your sight must be sharpened by love! You must see and know! Consider the Last Supper; consider Jesus Christ aware that He must soon separate bodily from humanity; consider his love by which, wishing to remain united with us forever, He instituted this Sacrament so that He might be bodily and forever united to humanity. O inextinguishable love! Truly ardent love was this, O love of Christ, O love for men! O Jesus, You already had before your eyes the death awaiting You; the pains and atrocious torments of the Passion were already piercing your Heart, and nevertheless You willed to offer Yourself to your crucifiers and to make it so that, through this Sacrament, they could always have You as a gift of eternity, O You whose delights are to be with the children of men!

"O my soul, how will you not wish to plunge yourself wholly into the love of Him who never forgot us and desires to unite Himself to us forever?" (cf. St. Angela of Foligno).
