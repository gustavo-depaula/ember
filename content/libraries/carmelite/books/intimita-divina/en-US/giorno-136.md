## 136 — THE MYSTERY OF THE CROSS
*Good Friday*

### Presence of God

— O Jesus, grant me to enter with You into the depths of the mystery of the Cross.

### Meditation

1 — Good Friday is the day that more than any other
invites us to penetrate "into the density of the trials and sorrows
of the Son of God" (J.C.C.A. 35, 9), and not only with
the theoretical consideration of the mind, but even more with
the practical disposition of the will to embrace willingly
suffering, so as to associate and assimilate ourselves to the Crucified. By suffering with Him we shall better understand His sufferings,
we shall better understand His love for us, since "the
purest suffering carries with it a more intimate and pure understanding"
(J.C.C. 36, 12), and "no one feels more deeply in
the heart Christ's Passion than one who has suffered something similar" (Imit. II, 12, 4). With these dispositions
let us accompany the Lord through His last earthly day.

The atrocious martyrdom that in a few hours will tear His
Body has not yet begun, and yet Jesus' agony
in the garden of olives is one of the most painful moments
of His Passion and most revealing of His spirit's most bitter sufferings. His most holy Soul finds itself immersed in an inexpressible anguish: it is abandonment and extreme desolation, without the least comfort either from
God or from men. The Savior feels weighing upon Himself the enormous burden of all humanity's sins;
He, most innocent, sees Himself covered with the most execrable crimes, made almost enemy of God, made target
of His infinite justice that will punish in Him all
our wickedness. Certainly, as God, Jesus
never ceased, not even in the most painful moments of
His Passion, to be united to His Father, but as
man He felt as if rejected by Him, "stricken by God
and humbled" (Is. 53, 4). This explains the intimate drama
of His spirit - a drama far more painful than the terrible physical sufferings awaiting Him - it explains the cruel agony that made Him sweat blood,
it explains His lament: "My soul is sorrowful unto death" (Mt. 26,
38). And whereas before He had ardently desired the
Passion, now that His humanity stands before the harsh concreteness of the event, deprived of the sensible aid
of Divinity, which seems not only to withdraw but even
to be indignant against it, Jesus groans: "My Father, if it is
possible, let this chalice pass from me!" But the anguished cry
of human nature is immediately silenced in the cry
of Christ's perfect conformity to the Father's will: "Yet not as I will, but as
You will" (ibid., 39).


2 — The agony in the garden is followed by Judas's
traitorous kiss, the arrest, the night passed between the interrogations
of the priests and the insults of soldiers who strike
Jesus, spit in His face, blindfold His eyes,
while outside in the courtyard Peter denies Him. At dawn the
interrogations and accusations resume, then begin the various passages
from one tribunal to another: from Caiaphas to Pilate,
from Pilate to Herod, from Herod back again to Pilate.
Then follows the horrible scourging,
the crowning with thorns, and finally, dressed as a mock king, the Son of God is presented to the crowd that shouts:
"Away with this one, and release Barabbas," while for
Him, the Savior, the rabble cries aloud: "Crucify Him, crucify Him!" (Lk. 23, 18 and 21). Laden with the wood
of execution, Jesus is dragged to Calvary and then
crucified between two thieves. It is a sum of terrible physical and moral sufferings that reaches its maximum when the Lord, agonizing on the cross, gives the cry:
"My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?" (Mt.
27, 46).

Here we are again before the intimate drama that
tears Christ's Soul and now accompanies, with rapid
crescendo, the intense increase of His physical sufferings.
During the discourse of the Last Supper, Jesus, speaking of His
coming Passion, had told the Apostles: "The hour is coming
when you will be scattered... and you will leave me alone;
yet I am not alone, because the Father is with me" (Jn. 16, 32).
Union with the Father is everything for Jesus: it is His life and
His strength, His comfort and His joy; if men
abandon Him, the Father is always with Him and this is
enough for Him. From this we can better understand the intensity of
His suffering when, during the Passion, the Father
withdraws from Him. And yet, even in His agony and His
death on the cross, Jesus is always God and as such is always
indissolubly united to the Father. But He has taken upon Himself
the heavy burden of our sins, and these rise up
like a barrier of moral separation between Him and the Father.
His Humanity, though personally united to the Word,
finds itself, by a miracle, deprived of every divine comfort and support and instead feels weighing upon itself the curse due to sin: "Christ" - says St. Paul - "redeemed us from the curse... becoming a curse for us" (Gal. 3, 13). Here we touch the depths of Jesus' Passion, the most atrocious bitterness that
He embraced for our salvation. And yet,
even amid such cruel torments, Jesus' lament resolves into the expression of total abandonment:
"Father, into Your hands I commend my spirit"
(Lk. 23, 46). Thus Jesus, who willed to taste to the
full how bitter it is for man to suffer and die, teaches
us to overcome the disturbances and anguishes caused in us by pain and death with acts of full submission
to God's will and of trusting abandonment
into His hands.

### Colloquy

"O Christ, Son of God, contemplating the great sorrow
You endured for us on the cross, I hear You say to my
soul: 'I have not loved you in deceit!' These words
open my eyes and clearly I see how much You have done
for me as effect of this love. I see what
You endured in life and in death, O suffering God-Man,
because of this intimate and ineffable love. Yes, O Lord,
You have not loved me in deceit, but with most perfect and true love. And in me I see the complete opposite,
for I love You tepidly and without truth, and to know this is an unbearable pain to me.

"O Master, You loved me without deceit; I instead,
sinner, have never loved You except with deficient love.
And I have never wanted to feel those pains that You
willingly suffered on the cross, and thus I have served You
with negligence and without truth.

"Your love, my God, stirs in me a burning desire
to want to do nothing in which there is
offense against You, to want to embrace the suffering and contempt
borne by You, to keep continually fixed in mind
Your Passion and Death, in which is our true health and
life.

"O Lord, O Master and eternal Physician, Your Blood
is the medicine that, without any price, You offer us
for the healing of our souls and, while it cost You
the most bitter Passion and Death on the cross, for me it
costs nothing, except to dispose myself to receive it,
and You immediately give it to me and heal my infirmities.
My God, since You have disposed to free me and heal me,
provided I show You with tears and sorrow my evils and infirmities,
Lord, since my soul is infirm, behold I show You my sins
and my miseries. There can be no sin,
no sickness of soul and spirit for which You have not
given sufficient remedy and satisfied by Your death.

"All my health and all my joy is therefore in
You, O crucified Christ, so that, in whatever state I
find myself, I never want to remove my gaze from Your
cross" (Bl. Angela of Foligno).
