## 356 — THE PERFECT YES

### Presence of God

Lord, grant that I may give You the free and full consent of my will.

### Meditation

St. John of the Cross says that the mark of union of wills is the soul's "yes of consent" given entirely to God. The soul hands itself over in the total gift of its will and chooses no longer to want anything but God and His good pleasure. In every circumstance it repeats its yes, lovingly accepting all that He wills and does.

At the beginning of spiritual life a fervent soul already desires to say yes without reserve. Yet in practice, while still entangled in passions and attachments, its gift is often partial. Faced with concrete sacrifices, renunciation, and struggle, the ideal yes can become a practical no. In the state of union this changes: the soul is so surrendered to God's holy will that it does not withdraw its gift. Its yes becomes stable and effective, offering it to God as a bride to the Bridegroom; this is why the mystics call it "spiritual betrothal."

The intensity of union depends, on our side, on the perfection of this yes. It must be perfect in breadth and in depth. In breadth, because it should embrace not only what God commands, but also what most pleases Him. Love makes the soul attentive enough to discern what pleases God more in each circumstance, and generous enough to carry it out without delay. In depth, because the soul must adhere to the divine will not grudgingly, but with the full momentum of a will happy to give itself despite sacrifices.

This appears above all in the direction indicated by Jesus in the great commandment of charity. St. Teresa of Avila says clearly: for us, the will of God consists in two things, love of God and love of neighbor. Here all our efforts must converge. She insists especially on fraternal charity, because it is the surest sign of love for God and also a vulnerable point: one may accept severe sacrifices and yet fail in charity through subtle self-love, rash judgments, and small unkindnesses.

As long as we find in ourselves such defects, even light ones, our gift is not yet complete, our yes not yet perfect. God wants us to love every neighbor and love with perfection: "This is My commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you" (Jn 15:12). How can we claim union with God's will if we do not keep this commandment with great diligence?

St. Teresa assures us that, if we practice fraternal charity as we should, we will not fail to obtain union. In return for the love shown to our neighbor, the Lord Himself makes our love for Him grow by a thousand means.

### Colloquy

"O Infinite Good, I want to offer and consecrate myself to You without ceasing, on the altar of my heart. I offer You my soul, redeemed by Your most precious Blood, that it may become a dwelling for Your Majesty and be transformed in You.

"I offer You my intellect, eager to know, O divine Wisdom, You who alone can satisfy the thirst to understand Your greatness. Enlighten my darkness and grant me a gentle knowledge of You that may inflame my heart with love.

"And I offer You my will, which above all You seek. Let it love You with eternal ardor and remain united to You forever. May it detach itself from creatures, rise to You, and rest in the cave of Your Heart.

"But why, my God, do I offer You soul and powers, if I am already wholly Yours by creation and even more by redemption? This offering is not for Your gain, but for mine, for my happiness is to be wholly offered and given to You, O immortal Life." (cf. St. Mary Magdalene de' Pazzi)
