## 164 — THE DIVINE OFFICE

### Presence of God

O Jesus, unite me to your great prayer, to the great prayer of the Church.

### Meditation

1. — The liturgy surrounds Holy Mass with the recitation of the Divine Office which - as Mediator Dei teaches -

"is the prayer of Christ's Mystical Body, offered to God in the name of all Christians and for their benefit, being made by priests, by the other ministers of the Church, and by religious delegated for this purpose by the Church herself." The great dignity of the Divine Office lies precisely in this: in being not a private prayer, but the public, official prayer of Christ's Mystical Body, in which the members do not pray alone, but pray together with Christ their Head. "By assuming human nature, the Word of God introduced into this earthly exile the hymn that is sung in heaven for all eternity. He unites to Himself the whole human community and associates it with Himself in singing this hymn of praise" (ibid.). In the Divine Office "Jesus prays with us as our Priest; He prays in us as our Head... Let us therefore recognize," says St. Augustine, "both our voices in Him and his voice in us." What a great gift! Jesus, the Son of God, associates our poor, miserable prayers with his great and most precious prayer.

Although the Divine Office is obligatory only for priests and for religious charged by the Church, one can say that it is the prayer of all the Christian people, in the sense that it is raised to God "in their name and for their benefit." It is therefore highly praiseworthy that lay faithful too seek in some way to participate in it, for example by reciting Sunday Vespers, Prime, and Compline. Besides, at every hour of day and night they can offer God the Church's great prayer according to their own intentions and needs, thus supplying for the deficiencies and brevity of their personal prayers; moreover, even in the midst of daily occupations, they can unite themselves from time to time through pious aspirations to the "perpetual praise" that the Church raises to God in the name of all Christendom.

2. — The Divine Office is composed for the most part of texts drawn from Sacred Scripture and therefore inspired by the Holy Spirit; we cannot therefore find more beautiful vocal prayers, more suited to praising the divine Majesty; through them the Holy Spirit himself "prays in us with inexpressible groanings" (Rom. 8, 26). And on the other hand, these prayers being so rich in doctrine and unction, they also help nourish personal piety. All these reasons make us understand that "the lofty dignity of this prayer must correspond to the inner devotion of our soul" (Med. Dei), so that, as St. Augustine says, "our mind may agree with our voice." The Divine Office, being the prayer the Church raises to God together with Jesus her Head, and being inspired by the Holy Spirit, already has in itself great value; but it has no value for us - for nourishing our union with God, for drawing down divine blessings - it does not become our prayer if we do not accompany it with our own personal devotion. As society of the faithful, the Church prays with the heart of her children, she prays with our heart; and the more this heart is fervent and full of love, the more efficacious and living the prayer of the Church will be.

Though not having the obligation of reciting the Divine Office and limiting itself to a few brief prayers taken from the Breviary, every soul of interior life should seek to grasp the spirit of liturgical prayer and make it its own. Spirit of praise and adoration that wishes to render God perpetual worship in union with Christ and in the name of the whole Church; spirit of solidarity with Jesus our Head and with all the faithful our brothers; universal spirit that embraces the needs of the whole world and prays in the name of all Christendom. How the horizons and intentions of our prayers then broaden! And in praying we no longer feel alone, but little ones praying beside Jesus, the great One who prays!

### Colloquy

"O Lord, your ears are turned not to the mouth,

but to the heart; they are open not to the tongue, but to the life of the one who praises you.

"I sing with the voice to awaken piety in me;
I sing with the heart to please you... May not only my voice praise you, but my works also praise you. Grant that I may not cease to live well, so as to be able to praise you without interruption. If at times my tongue must be silent, may my life cry out; your ears will be turned to my voice, but You direct them to my heart...

"I do not want to stop at the voice alone; when I praise you, I want to praise you with my whole self; let the voice sing, let life sing, let actions sing. And if here below I still have to groan, to suffer tribulation, to be tempted, I hope all will pass and the day will come when my praise will never fail. Let my voice fail, but not my affection.

"It is better for me to spend my strength praising you than to take breath praising myself. But it is not possible to grow weary while praising you. To praise you is like taking food: the more I praise you, the more strength I gain, because all the more You communicate to me your sweetness, You who are the object of my praises.

"Help me then to praise you with voice and with mind and with good works, so that, as You exhort me in your Scriptures, I may sing to you the new song. To the old man, old song; to the new man, new song. If I love earthly things, my song is old; to sing the new song, I must love eternal things. Your love is in itself new and eternal; it is always new, precisely because it never grows old. Sin is what made me old; renew me You with your grace" (St. Augustine).

1. — Month of May, month of Mary. Spontaneously every Christian heart turns to the heavenly Mother with the desire to live in greater intimacy with her and to strengthen the sweet bonds that bind it to her. It is a great comfort to meet along our spiritual path - often laborious and strewn with difficulties - the gentle figure of a mother. One is so well near mother. With her everything becomes easier; the discouraged and weary heart, the heart shaken by storms, finds new strength, new hope, and takes up the way again with renewed vigor.

"If the winds of temptations rise" - sings St. Bernard - "if you strike the rocks of tribulations, look at the star, invoke Mary. In dangers, in anguishes, in perplexities, think of Mary: invoke Mary." At certain moments the harsh way of nothingness frightens our misery and then, more than ever, we need her support, a mother's support. Mary Most Holy, before us, trod the narrow and strait way that leads to holiness; before us she carried the cross; before us she knew the ascents of spirit through suffering. Sometimes perhaps we do not dare to fix our gaze on Jesus, the God-Man, whom by reason of his Divinity we feel too far above us; but beside Him is Mary, his Mother and our Mother, privileged creature, yes, but creature like us and therefore a model more accessible to our weakness.

In this month Mary comes to meet us, to take us by the hand, to introduce us into the secret of her interior life and thus become model and norm of ours.

2. — St. Therese of the Child Jesus, speaking of certain discourses about Mary Most Holy, said: "they show Our Lady as inaccessible; she should be proposed as imitable" (NV). It is true: Mary is inaccessible in the most high privileges that crown her divine Motherhood, and it is right to consider these privileges to admire, contemplate, praise the greatness of our Mother and therefore to fall ever more in love with her; but at the same time one must also consider Mary in the concrete setting of her earthly life, a humble and simple setting that does not at all go outside the frame of ordinary life common to every mother of a family; and under this aspect Mary is truly imitable. To contemplate Mary's greatness and urge ourselves to imitate her virtues will be the program of our month of May. And above all we want to consider Our Lady as model and ideal of souls of interior life. No one, like her, understood all the depth of Jesus' word: "One thing only is necessary" (Lk. 10, 42), and no one more than she lived by this word. From the first moments of her life Mary was wholly God's and lived only for Him; hence the years spent in the shadow of the Temple in silence and prayer, the months spent at Nazareth in recollection, while the eternal Word was incarnate in her womb, the thirty years spent in sweet intimacy with Jesus, her Son and her God, then participation in his apostolic life, in his Passion, and finally the last years spent with John, when through her prayer Mary was the support of the nascent Church. Although the background in which she moves and acts changes aspect, although the external circumstances in which she lives change, Mary's life remains unchangeable in its substance: in the interior search for the "One thing necessary," in adhesion to God alone. The succession of events and her very exterior activity do not prevent her from persevering in that attitude of continual prayer in which St. Luke presents her to us: "Mary kept all these things [the divine mysteries] in her heart and pondered them" (2, 19 and 51).

If, in imitation of Mary, our heart is firmly anchored in God, nothing will be able to draw it away from its interior occupation: seeking, loving the Lord, and living in his intimacy.

Colloquy.

"My soul, are you afraid to draw near to God? He has given you Jesus as mediator. What can such a Son not obtain for you from the Father? The Father who loves Him will hear Him for the love He bears Him. But are you afraid to approach even Him? Yet He has made Himself your brother, your companion, and wished to undergo all the humiliations of human nature, except sin, precisely to have compassion on your miseries; this brother Mary gave to you. But perhaps his divine majesty still frightens you, because, though man, Jesus does not cease to be God. Do you then want an advocate with Him? Have recourse to Mary. Mary is a pure creature, pure not only because exempt from sin, but also because by nature solely human. O Mary, I am certain, You will be heard for the deference You merit; the Son will certainly hear You because You are his Mother, and the Father will hear the Son. Here is my unshakable trust, here is all the reason for my hope! Blessed Virgin, the Angel said: "You have found grace before God" (Lk. 1, 30). Therefore You will always find grace, and only grace do I need and only grace do I ask of You" (cf. St. Bernard).

"Draw me after you, O Virgin Mary, that I may run after the fragrance of your perfumes. Draw me, for I am held back by the weight of my sins and by the malice of your enemies. As no one goes to your Son unless the Father draws him, so, in a certain way, I dare to say that no one goes to Him if You do not draw him by your holy prayers. You teach true wisdom, You obtain grace for sinners, You are their advocate, You promise glory to those who honor you because you are the treasurer of graces. You found grace before God, O sweetest Virgin, You who were preserved from original stain, filled with the Holy Spirit, and conceived the Son of God. You received all these graces, O most humble Mary, not only for yourself but also for us, that You might assist us in all our necessities"

(cf. Ven. Raymond Jordan).
