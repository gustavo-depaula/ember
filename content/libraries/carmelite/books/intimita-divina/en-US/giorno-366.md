## 366 — COMMEMORATION OF ALL THE FAITHFUL DEPARTED
*November 2*

### Presence of God

O Lord, grant eternal rest to the souls of the departed, and let the thought of death urge me to greater generosity.

### Meditation

1 — "The Church, our good Mother, after praising her children who already rejoice in heaven, wishes to help the souls still suffering in the place of purification, and intercedes with all her strength before Christ her Lord and Spouse, that they may soon reach the fellowship of the elect in heaven." So reads the Roman Martyrology.

Yesterday we contemplated the glory of the triumphant Church and invoked her intercession. Today we contemplate the purifying sufferings of the Church suffering and implore divine help for her: "Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord."

This is the dogma of the communion of saints in act: the Church triumphant intercedes for us, the Church militant, and we in turn hasten to aid the Church suffering.

Death has taken beloved persons from us, yet there can be no true separation from those who died in the kiss of the Lord. The bond of charity continues to unite us, embracing earth, heaven, and purgatory, so that from these three shores fraternal help circulates - fruit of love aimed at the triumph of love in the common glory of paradise.

Today's liturgy is tinged with sadness, yet not the sadness of those "who have no hope" (1 Thes 4:13), for over it shines faith in the resurrection and the eternal blessedness awaiting us.

The Gospel passages of the three Masses for the dead speak these consoling truths with Christ's own words: "This is the will of Him who sent Me... that I should lose nothing of what He has given Me, but should raise it up on the last day" (Jn 6:39).

What could be more consoling? Jesus appears again as the Good Shepherd who wills to lose none of His sheep and spares no means to bring all to salvation. The Church responds with gratitude: "To Your faithful, O Lord, life is changed, not ended; and when this earthly dwelling is dissolved, an eternal home is made ready in heaven."

More than an inexorable ending, death for the Christian is a door opened onto eternity.

2 — This is true not only for our departed loved ones but for us as well. Death is a punishment and naturally brings sorrow, fear, and dread. Even the saints experienced this; Jesus Himself willed to experience it. Yet the Church places before us words that encourage: "Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord... that they may rest from their labors, for their works follow them" (Rev 14:13).

The body's life dies, what is earthly passes, but the life of the spirit remains, and good works remain: the one treasure that follows the soul in the great passage and makes death precious. "Precious in the sight of the Lord is the death of His saints." Such death has rightly been called dies natalis, birthday into eternal life.

How we would wish this for our own death: a birth into the beatific vision, into the unfailing love of heaven.

Yet today, while inviting us to pray for the departed, the liturgy reminds us that between death and eternal beatitude there is purgatory. Because our works follow us and are not all good - or, though good, still imperfect - the soul must be purified of every stain before seeing God.

If we were perfectly faithful to grace, purgatory would not be necessary. Already on earth God purifies those who give themselves wholly to Him and let themselves be shaped at His pleasure.

Moreover, while in purgatory one suffers without increasing in love, purification on earth has the great advantage of being meritorious: it increases grace and charity and thus enables us to love God more for all eternity.

For this reason we should desire to be purified in this life. But we must not deceive ourselves: total purification on earth demands great suffering. If today we are not generous in accepting pure and naked suffering like Jesus on the Cross, our purification will necessarily be completed in purgatory.

May the thought of this place of expiation make us zealous in suffrages for the dead and, at the same time, courageous in embracing suffering for reparation of our sins.

### Colloquy

"Grant, O Lord, that at the death of my loved ones I may feel a reasonable grief, shedding resigned tears over our mortal condition, soon calmed by faith's consoling thought that the faithful, in dying, leave us only for a little while to be better off.

"Keep me far from grieving like pagans who have no hope. I may feel sorrow; but when afflicted, let hope console me. With such hope, it does not suit Your temple to remain in mourning, O God, for You dwell there, You who are the Consoler and never fail in Your promises." (St. Augustine)

"O Lord and Creator of the universe, Master of life and death, You sustain and fill our souls with benefits; You complete and transform all things through Your Word in the time fixed by Your wisdom. Receive today our departed brethren and grant them eternal rest.

"And receive us too, at the hour You choose, after guiding us in this life as long as is useful and salutary. Receive us prepared by holy fear, without turmoil or hesitation in the last day. Grant that we not leave earth's things with pain, as those too attached to world and flesh, but move resolutely and gladly toward the long and blessed life in Christ Jesus our Lord, to whom be glory forever. Amen." (St. Gregory Nazianzen)
