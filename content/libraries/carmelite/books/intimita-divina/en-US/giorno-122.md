## 122 — BLIND OBEDIENCE

### Presence of God

O Jesus, who for love of me willed to submit Yourself to every authority, teach me the secret of Your obedience.

### Meditation

1 — When one sees God in the superior, one obeys
without discussions, without useless reasonings, without delay:
"Christus jubet, sufficit," Christ commands, this is
enough for me. In fact, what else shall I still seek,
when I know that my superiors' orders are the orders of God
Himself? Even if what is commanded is for me difficult and
burdensome, knowing with certainty that it is truly the Lord
who wills it from me will give me strength to undertake it
promptly, without offering the least resistance.

There can certainly be cases in which there is
a reasonable motive for thinking that a given order has been
imposed without taking into account elements that, if neglected, could bring painful consequences for the superior
himself; then it is good, and can even be a duty, to point this out. Likewise there is no imperfection in asking for explanations when an order
does not seem clear to us or puts us in serious embarrassment; but this must be done with humility, without insistence, and always ready to submit to the superior's decision. As for the rest,
we must be firmly resolved not to reason,
not to discuss orders received, not to investigate the motives that may have moved the superior
to give a certain order. If we begin to reason
about obedience, we will create obstacles to obeying;
therefore one must know how to cut off at once every reasoning,
even interior, and make of it a beautiful sacrifice to the Lord.
Worse still would be to communicate our reasonings to
others, to criticize superiors' dispositions, etc.; in this
way we would create obstacles also to others' obedience.

If you wish to give your whole personality to the Lord,
you must totally renounce your own way of seeing, which,
however good it may be, will always be infinitely inferior to God's, and God makes you act according to His
views precisely by means of superiors' orders.


2 — To say that the superior manifests God's will to
us certainly does not mean that everything he thinks,
says, or wills is also what God thinks, says, and wills.
Certainly not. It means only that, when the superior, as
such, gives a legitimate order, that order is surely
the manifestation of God's will. Blind obedience is precisely that which, going beyond every personal judgment
or opinion, adheres to superiors' orders
solely because in them it recognizes the divine will. It is
blind because it blinds the intellect, not allowing it
to take account of its own judgment, to examine the superior's motives, or discuss the command received; it is blind
because it is founded solely on a motive of faith, since precisely by faith we know that God's will
comes to us through superiors. And since faith
is an "obscure" knowledge, one can say that obedience conditioned by it is "without natural light" and
therefore blind; in other words, it is an obedience that does not
base itself on the reasonableness of human motives, but on
this one motive of faith: whoever listens to superiors listens to God. "He who hears you, hears me" (Lk. 10, 16).
In cases where the subject's opinion is better than
that of the superior, blind obedience does not consist in
denying one's own judgment to the point of asserting the contrary
- which would not conform to truth - but simply in renouncing regulation according to one's own opinion and in judging that one must nevertheless obey,
because it is certain that God wants from us what the superior
has ordered, and not what seems to us, and perhaps even
is, better.

Whoever, under pretext of what is more perfect, leaves the way of obedience, leaves the safe way of God's will for the
dangerous and trap-filled way of his own will; and it is
certain that a consecrated soul can do nothing pleasing to God outside holy obedience. "The actions of the religious - says St. John of the Cross - are not his,
but obedience's, and if he withdraws them from it, he will be called to account for them
as for actions lost" (Ct. 11).

### Colloquy

"O Lord, how different are Your ways from our
coarse imaginations! How true it is that from a soul
already resolved to love You and wholly surrendered into Your hands, You
want nothing else but that it obey, desiring nothing other than
to seek what can contribute most to Your greater glory!
Since its will is Yours, it has no need to seek nor choose another road, because this care, Lord, You Yourself take up and guide the soul along the path most advantageous to it. Even if the superior did not
have our spiritual profit at heart... You, my
God, would provide for it all the same, disposing souls and
our occupations in such a way that later we would find ourselves, without knowing how, so advanced in spirit
and with such profit as to remain amazed" (T.G.
Fd. 5, 6).

"From how many anxieties one is freed, good
God, by pronouncing the vow of obedience!... Having no
compass but the will of superiors, I shall always be sure
to proceed on the right path without fearing to err,
even when to my eyes it certainly seems that superiors are mistaken. But as soon as I cease consulting this infallible
compass, my soul at once loses itself on the driest paths,
where soon the water of grace fails it. O Jesus,
obedience is the compass You have given me to lead me
safely to the immortal shore; and how sweet it is for me
to rest my gaze upon it and then fulfill
Your will!" (T.B.G. St. 9).

O Lord, I want to give myself to obedience with an unshakable confidence in Your divine governance, which sustains,
guides, and infallibly turns everything to the good of my soul. I want
to give myself to obedience without the least hesitation; by binding myself to it,
I bind myself to You, I bind myself to Your will.
