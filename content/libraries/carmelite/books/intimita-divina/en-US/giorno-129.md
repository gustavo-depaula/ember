## 129 — THE SEVEN SORROWS OF MARY
*Friday of Passion Week*

### Presence of God

Receive me, O Mary, near the Cross, and let me share with you in Jesus' Passion.

### Meditation

1 — The first explicit declaration of the part
that Mary Most Holy would have in Jesus' Passion is found in the prophecy of old Simeon: "and a sword
will pierce your own soul" (Lk. 2:35); a prophecy that finds its full realization on Calvary. "Yes, blessed Mother" - comments St. Bernard - "a sword truly pierced your soul. For only by passing through it could it
reach the flesh of your Son. And surely, after
your Jesus had breathed His last, the cruel lance,
opening His side, did not reach His soul, but it pierced your soul. In fact, His soul was no longer there,
but yours could not be detached from Him" (BR.). A beautiful interpretation that helps us understand how Mary, as Mother, was intimately associated with her Son's Passion.

The Gospel does not tell us that Mary was present in
the glorious moments of Jesus' life, but it does tell us
that she was present on Calvary: "Standing by the Cross of Jesus
were His Mother and... Mary of Cleophas and Mary Magdalene" (Jn. 19:25). No one could prevent her
from running there, where her Son was to be put to death;
and her love gives her the courage to "stand" there, upright,
at the foot of the Cross, to witness the most painful agony and
the death of Him whom she loved above all things, since
He was at once her Son and her God. As one day she had
accepted becoming His Mother, now she accepts seeing Him
torn from head to foot, accepts seeing Him snatched from her by a cruel death.

And not only does she accept: she offers. Jesus had gone
freely to the Passion, and Mary freely offers her beloved
Son for the glory of the Most Holy Trinity and for the
salvation of men. Jesus' sacrifice thus becomes Mary's
sacrifice, not only because Mary offers Him with Him and offers in Him the Son who belongs to her,
but also because in this offering she accomplishes the deepest holocaust of herself, since Jesus is the center of
her affections, of her whole life. God, who had given her that divine Treasure, on Calvary asks Him of her, and Mary offers Him with all her heart's affection, with most complete
adhesion to the divine will.


2 — The day's liturgy places moving words on
the lips of the sorrowful Madonna: "All you
who pass by the way, look and see if there is any sorrow
like my sorrow" (MR.). Yes, her sorrow is immeasurable,
but her love is greater still,
so great that it can contain that whole sea of sorrow;
of no creature as of Mary can it be said that her
love is stronger than death, and indeed it made her capable
of bearing Jesus' most bitter death.

"Who could remain unmoved while contemplating
the Mother of Christ suffering with her Son?" sings the Stabat
Mater, and immediately adds: "O Mother... let me feel
the force of your sorrow, so that I may weep with you.
Make me bear in my heart the wounds of Christ; make me share
His Passion; let me be intoxicated by the Cross and
Blood of your Son." Following the Church's invitation,
let us contemplate and compassionate Mary's sorrows;
let us ask her for the great grace to share with her in the Passion of
her Jesus. And let us remember that this participation must not remain in the sphere of feeling - even if
good and holy feelings - but must lead us to true com-
passion, that is, to suffer together with Jesus and with Mary.
The sufferings we encounter in our life are given to us
precisely for this.

The vision of Our Lady at the foot of the Cross makes
the lesson of the cross less hard and bitter for us;
her motherly example encourages us to suffer,
it makes the way of Calvary sweeter.
Let us go, then, with Mary to meet Jesus on Calvary;
let us go with her to meet the cross and, sustained by her,
let us embrace it willingly to offer it together with her Son's.

### Colloquy

"O Mary, holy Mother of crucified Jesus, tell me
something of His Passion, because among so many who
were present, you felt and saw it more than all,
having looked upon Him with the eyes of body and mind,
and having considered it with all attention, since you loved Him greatly" (Bl. Angela of Foligno).

"O Mary, let me stand with you beside the Cross;
let me contemplate with you the Passion of your Jesus;
let me share your sorrow, your weeping. O holy
Mother, imprint deeply in my heart the wounds
of the Crucified; let me suffer with Him,
associate me with His suffering and yours" (cf. Stabat Mater).

"O Queen of virgins, you are also Queen of martyrs.
But the sword-wound opens in your heart, because
in you everything happens within...

"Oh, how beautiful you are when I contemplate you during your
long martyrdom, so serene, clothed in a majesty that
radiates strength and sweetness together! You had learned from
the Word Himself how those chosen by the Father as victims
must suffer, those He decided to associate with the great
work of Redemption, those He has known and predestined
to be conformed to His Christ," crucified for love.

"You are there, O Mary, at the foot of the Cross, standing,
in strength and courage; and the Master says to me: 'Behold,
your Mother.' He gives you to me as mother! Now that He has returned to the Father, now that He has put me in His place on the cross,
that I may suffer in myself 'what is lacking in His Passion
for His body, which is the Church,' you, O Mary, are still
there to teach me how to suffer like Him, to repeat to me,
to make me understand the final song of His soul,
that song which you alone, the Mother, could understand" (E.T.
II, 15).

O sweetest Mother, that my desire to suffer
may not be in vain, help me to recognize in every
daily suffering the cross of your Jesus and to embrace it with
love.
