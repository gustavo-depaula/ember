## 124 — JESUS PERSECUTED
*Passion Sunday*

### Presence of God

O Jesus, introduce me into the mystery of Your Passion, associate me with it, so that I may then share in Your Resurrection.

### Meditation

1 — Today begins "Passiontide," a time
particularly consecrated to the remembrance and loving contemplation of Jesus' sufferings. The veiled cross and images,
the suspension of the Gloria at Mass and in the responsories of the Divine Office, the omission of the Psalm "Iudica me" at the beginning
of Mass, are all signs of mourning with which the Church
commemorates the Lord's Passion. In the lessons of the Divine Office, St. Leo the Pope exhorts us to share "in the
Cross of Christ, so that we too may do something
that unites us to what He has done for us, as the Apostle says: 'if we suffer with Him, we shall be glorified with
Him.'" It is therefore not only a matter of meditating on Jesus' sufferings,
but of taking part in them, of bearing His Passion
in our heart and in our body (cf. 2 Cor. 4, 10),
because only thus can we share in its fruits.
This is why, in the Office of the season, the Church repeats
with pious insistence the invitation: "if today you
hear the voice of the Lord, do not harden your hearts."
In these days the Lord's voice is heard not by
means of words, but through the eloquent witness
of facts, through the great fact of the Passion, which is
the most convincing mystery of His infinite love for
us. Let us therefore open our hearts to the sublime lessons
of the Passion: let us learn how much He has loved us and how much
we must love Him in return; let us learn that for us too, if
we wish to follow Him, it is necessary to suffer, it is necessary to carry the cross with Him and after Him. And at the same time
let us open our hearts to the liveliest hope, because in Jesus' Passion is our salvation. St. Paul, in today's Epistle
(Heb. 9, 11-15), presents to us the majestic figure
of Christ, High Priest who, "by means of His own
blood, entered once for all into the sanctuary
[that is, heaven], obtaining for us eternal redemption." Jesus'
Passion has redeemed us, has reopened to us the Father's
house; Jesus' Passion is the reason for our
hope.


2 — In today's Gospel (Jn. 8, 46-59) we have a
scene of the relentless hostilities of the Jews, a clear prelude to Jesus' Passion. Those hardened hearts in no
way want to admit the Savior's mission and strive in a thousand ways to refute His teachings,
to denigrate Him before the people, presenting Him
as a liar, one possessed; finally, their fury
reaches the point that they decide to stone Him: "then they took up stones to throw at Him." Jesus'
death is already decreed by the Jews, but, since the hour fixed by the Father had not yet come, "Jesus hid Himself and went out of the temple."

This Gospel passage allows us to consider
Jesus' conduct before His persecutors: meekness,
zeal for their souls, personal disinterest, and total abandonment in God. St. Gregory the Great writes: "Consider, dearest brethren, the Lord's meekness.
He who had come to remit sins said:
'Which of you will convict me of sin?' He who by virtue of
His divinity could justify sinners does not disdain
to show by reasoning that He is not a sinner" (BR.).
Then come the calumnies: "you are a Samaritan and have
a demon." The divine Master answers, but always meekly and only as much as is necessary to bear witness to
the truth: "I do not have a demon, but I honor my Father,
and you dishonor me." As for the rest, He entrusts
His reputation and His cause into God's hands: "Yet
I do not seek my own glory; there is One who seeks and judges."
And nevertheless, through the dispute, He does not cease to instruct and enlighten minds so as to draw them from error;
always forgetful of self, He thinks only of the good of souls. Thus, precisely in this harsh circumstance, Jesus gives us precious teachings: "He who is of God hears God's words. If anyone keeps my word, he will never see death." From the mouth of the persecuted Master let us gather these warnings and keep them in our hearts with jealous care.
Even today the world is full of His enemies, of those who fight His doctrine, of those who despise His Passion. At least let us believe in Him and be His faithful friends.

### Colloquy

"Praise be to You, most merciful God, who, while
we were miserable, banished, imprisoned, and condemned, willed to redeem and exalt us by the Passion,
the suffering, contempt, and poverty of Your Son. I therefore run to
Your Cross, O Christ, to suffering, contempt, and poverty,
and with all my strength I desire to be transformed into You, O suffering God-Man, who loved me so much as to will
to suffer a shameful and horrible death, and this for the sole
purpose of saving me and giving me example to suffer, for love of You,
adversities. It is perfection and a true sign of love to conform myself to You, Crucified, who for my sins willed to die
pitilessly, giving Yourself wholly prey to torments.
O my suffering God, only by reading in the book of Your life
and death will I be able to know You and penetrate Your
mystery. Grant me therefore a deep spirit of prayer,
a prayer devout, humble, attentive, made not only
with the lips, but with heart and mind, so as to
understand the lessons of Your Passion!

"In this book I see Your infinite goodness and mercy,
by which You preferred to take upon Yourself our condemnation,
our contempt, and suffering, rather than leave us in
so miserable a state. I see Your infinite goodness,
diligence, and care in saving us and leading us back to the homeland of heaven. I see the infinite wisdom with which, in an ineffable manner,
You redeemed, saved, and exalted us by mercy,
without injury to justice. And thus You knew how
to do this: while dying painfully, You gave life to all
things and destroyed common death.

"Moreover, in the book of the Cross I see Your infinitely
meekness, by which, being cursed, You did not
curse, nor take revenge; rather You forgave and won
heaven for Your very crucifiers" (cf. Bl. Angela of Foligno).
