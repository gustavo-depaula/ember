## Translation Decisions

### Preface
- The preface is by Chanoine (Canon) Jules Didiot, not by Montfort. It is the 1891 editor's introduction. Translated faithfully as historical context.
- Dropped all editor footnotes per translation guidelines.
- French ecclesiastical titles rendered into English equivalents (e.g., "Éminentissime Seigneur" → "Most Eminent Lord", "Chanoine" → "Canon").

### Introduction
- "Alma Mater" — Montfort interprets this as "hidden and secret Mother." The original Latin actually means "nourishing/loving Mother," but Montfort's (incorrect) etymology is part of his argument, so the translation preserves his interpretation while noting the standard meaning would differ.
- Maintained all Latin phrases with their translations, following the French pattern.

### Part 1, Chapter 1
- "mahométans" → "Muslims" (modernized from "Mohammedans")
- "prédestinés" kept as "predestinate" throughout — this is Montfort's characteristic theological term for the elect
- Long prophetic passage about the apostles of the end times preserved with its full rhetorical force
- *Exspectans exspectavi* rendered as "I have waited; I have waited!" to preserve the emphatic Latin doubling

### Part 1, Chapter 2
- Seven categories of false devotees translated consistently: critical, scrupulous, exterior, presumptuous, inconstant, hypocritical, self-interested
- Five marks of true devotion: interior, tender, holy, constant, disinterested
- The long Latin prayer of St. Augustine retained in Latin in the body text with a translator's footnote providing an English rendering (matching Montfort's own approach)
- "esclave d'amour" → "slave of love" / "loving slave" — preserved Montfort's terminology

### Part 2, Preliminary
- Detailed liturgical devotion list (Rosary mysteries, Office, psalter, etc.) translated with standard Catholic English terminology

### Part 2, Chapter 1
- Clear explanation of satisfactory/impetratory/meritorious value of good works preserved with theological precision
- Baptismal vows renewal theology carefully rendered

### Part 2, Chapter 2
- Eight motives translated with their numbered subheadings
- Historical section on the devotion's antiquity (St. Odilo, Peter Damian, Simon de Rojas, Cardinal de Berulle, etc.) translated with proper names standardized
- The five qualities of the way (easy, short, perfect, sure) preserved as a recurring motif
- Mother Agnes of Jesus story preserved in full

### Part 2, Chapter 3
- Extended Jacob/Rebecca/Esau typology translated with consistent biblical language
- Five parallels between predestinate and Jacob, five between reprobate and Esau
- Five charitable duties of Mary toward her servants (loves, provides, guides, defends, intercedes)

### Part 2, Chapter 4
- Seven marvelous effects numbered and translated
- The "mold" (moule) metaphor — Mary as "forma Dei" — rendered carefully with the sculptor/foundry comparison preserved

### Part 2, Chapter 5
- Seven exterior practices and four interior practices (through/with/in/for Mary)
- Three-week preparation period described in detail
- Chain-wearing practice translated faithfully as historical context
- "O Jesus, living in Mary" prayer given in full in translator's footnote

### Part 2, Chapter 6
- Before/during/after Communion method translated with all Latin prayers and their renderings

### Consecration
- The consecration formula translated as a solemn prayer, preserving its liturgical cadence and structure

## Expressions Requiring Special Handling

- "bêtes frémissantes" → "raging beasts" (Montfort's vivid metaphor for opponents)
- "escadron de braves" → "squadron of brave soldiers"
- "le siècle de Marie" → "the age of Mary" / "the century of Mary"
- "noix confites" → "candied nuts" (Montfort's metaphor for crosses sweetened by Mary)
- "pierre philosophale" → "philosopher's stone" (metaphor for Mary transforming souls)
- "vaisseau" → "vessel" (consistent biblical/spiritual usage)
- "forma Dei" → "mold of God" (St. Augustine's title for Mary, central to Montfort's argument)
- "chaînettes" → "little chains" (historical devotional practice)
- "confit" → "preserved/sweetened" (used for crosses made bearable by Mary's grace)

## General Approach

- All editor footnotes (by Chanoine Didiot, 1891) dropped per guidelines
- Latin phrases retained inline with English translations following, matching Montfort's own pattern
- Scripture references preserved as-is
- Montfort's passionate, rhetorical style preserved — long sentences, exclamations, direct addresses to Jesus and Mary kept faithful to the original register
- Theological terminology (hyperdulia, satisfactory/impetratory/meritorious value, etc.) kept precise
- Archaic French constructions rendered into clear modern English without losing Montfort's characteristic warmth and urgency
