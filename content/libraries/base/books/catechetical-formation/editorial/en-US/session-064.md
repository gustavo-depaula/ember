For a great many Catholics, **Confirmation is the sacrament they remember least and used most superficially.** It came at fourteen or fifteen, in a parish hall, after some classes nobody quite took seriously, and then was filed away as the day grandma cried and somebody handed you a card with money in it. The catechism above is asking you to look again.

The older name was *Confirmation* because the sacrament *confirms* — that is, *strengthens* — what was begun at Baptism. Pius X uses the more military image: *the soldier of Christ.* Modern readers can hear that as triumphalism; it is not. It names a real fact: at some point the Christian life stops being given to you (Baptism, the parents' faith, a child's wonder) and starts being defended *by* you. Confirmation is the sacrament of that transition. The Holy Spirit does not arrive at Confirmation for the first time — He arrived at Baptism — but He arrives in a new mode, a *sealing*, an empowerment for public witness.

St. Thomas teaches that Confirmation imprints an **indelible character** on the soul (a permanent mark, like Baptism and Holy Orders) and that this character *configures* you to Christ as His public witness. What the Apostles received at Pentecost, you received — really, sacramentally, no less — at Confirmation. The same Spirit. The same fire. The same gifts: wisdom, understanding, counsel, fortitude, knowledge, piety, fear of the Lord (Isaiah 11:2-3, Pius X's standard list).

If your Confirmation passed quietly into memory, do not panic. The grace of the sacrament does not expire. **Stir up the gift of God which is in thee** (2 Timothy 1:6) — the verse the Church has always read on the renewal of confirmational grace. Pray for the seven gifts again now, by name. Ask the Holy Spirit, *concretely*, for the one you most lack. He has been waiting to be asked.
