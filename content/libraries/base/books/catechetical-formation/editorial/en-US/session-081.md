You have walked through the Creed (whose we are), the Decalogue (how we are to live), the Precepts (the floor of practice), the Virtues (what to become), and the Sacraments (how grace reaches us). What remains, in the next nine sessions, is **prayer** — the daily breathing of the soul that holds all the rest together.

Aquinas above lifts up the **five qualities of true prayer** the Lord's Prayer itself models: confident, suitable, ordered, devout, humble. Each is worth holding briefly:

  * **Confident.** *Let us therefore go with confidence to the throne of grace* (Hebrews 4:16). The first failure of modern prayer is the half-doubt with which we begin. *Begin trusting.*
  * **Suitable.** Aquinas warns that we sometimes pray for what is not good for us (Mt 6:8 — *your Father knows what you need before you ask*). Christ taught us the Lord's Prayer because it asks *for what we ought to ask for*. When in doubt, pray it.
  * **Ordered.** The order matters: *seek first the kingdom of God* (Matthew 6:33). Prayer that begins with my own urgent want and ends, perhaps, with His glory has the order backwards. The Our Father's first three petitions are toward Him; the next four toward us. Same order in your own prayer when you can.
  * **Devout.** Not emotional intensity, but the *whole-soul attention* the body's posture supports. Kneel sometimes. Bow the head. The body is a teacher of the soul.
  * **Humble.** *God resists the proud, but gives grace to the humble* (James 4:6). The Pharisee in the temple prays his own résumé; the publican prays *be merciful to me, a sinner* and goes home justified. The publican's seven words are the model.

The catechism above also walks the **two kinds of prayer**: *mental* and *vocal*. Both are necessary; both are taught.

  * **Vocal prayer** — the rosary, the daily Office, the spoken Our Father, the Mass — *forms* the soul through repetition and incarnation. The body learns the prayer the soul will eventually mean.
  * **Mental prayer** — meditation on Scripture or a mystery of the faith, contemplation, the silent dwelling with God — *deepens* what the vocal has begun. Bellini's Christ in Gethsemane (today's image) is mental prayer at its most extreme: *He kneels alone, knowing what is coming, choosing the Father's will*.

Bellini's painting is the catechism in pigment. *Prayer is not a hobby. It is the only thing that adjusts the soul to the size of God. He prayed. So must we.*

The next nine sessions will walk you through the Lord's Prayer petition by petition. Treat them as a master class with the One Teacher. Pray slowly. Pray daily. Pray *with* the Church, who is praying with you whenever you pray.
