Yesterday's session named the saints; today's takes up the **physical objects** by which their honor — and Christ's — is concretely expressed: **icons, statues, crucifixes, relics, holy places**. The Protestant Reformation's iconoclasm took swords to these things; the Church has always defended them, and the catechism above explains why with care.

The doctrine rests on **two precise principles**:

  1. **The honor given to an image passes to the prototype.** This was the formula of the Second Council of Nicaea (787 AD), the seventh ecumenical council, which definitively settled the iconoclast controversy. When you bow before a crucifix, you are not bowing to the wood; you are bowing to *Christ whom the wood depicts*. When you kiss an icon of Mary, you are not kissing pigment; you are honoring *Mary whom the icon makes present*. The image is the *channel* of the act, not its object.

  2. **God Himself uses matter to convey grace.** This is, at root, the same principle as the Incarnation. In Christ, God *became matter*. After that, no Christian can despise matter as an enemy of the spiritual life. Aquinas: *the visible signifies the invisible*. The Eucharist itself is the supreme case — bread and wine become the Body and Blood of Christ. **Sacramental thinking is the natural Catholic mode**: God working through *things*. Relics participate in the same logic.

A relic is not a magical talisman. It is a *fragment of the body of someone whom God made holy* — and the body, as we saw in Session 033, is part of the saint's identity, will rise at the end of time, and is even now ordered toward glory. To honor a relic is to honor the *whole saint*, including the body God Himself raised up. Scripture itself attests to the practice: the touch of Elisha's bones raised a dead man (2 Kings 13:21); handkerchiefs touched to St. Paul's body healed the sick (Acts 19:11–12). The Catholic Church has never invented this. She has *preserved* it.

What this asks of you today:

  * **Use sacred images.** A crucifix on the wall. A small icon at your desk. The image is not for itself; it is for the *attention* it directs. *The eyes have a Catholic habit of looking up*; train them by giving them something good to look at.
  * **Reverence the body of saints when you can.** A pilgrimage, an altar relic at Mass, a saint's tomb. The relic is the physical hinge between heaven and your day.
  * **Catechize when asked.** *Why do you Catholics worship Mary?* — they do not. *Why bow before a statue?* — to the One the statue represents. The distinction is ancient, conciliar, and exact. Have it ready.
