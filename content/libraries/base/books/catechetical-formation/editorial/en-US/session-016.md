The catechism above takes up Christ's *public life* — the three years between His Baptism in the Jordan and the Last Supper. Scripture devotes more pages to these three years than to the previous thirty in Nazareth. The reason is theological: in this period, *the Word who had been born of the Virgin began publicly to be the Word who saves the world*.

Three things the catechism is asking the modern reader to recover.

**1. The miracles are not stunts; they are signatures.** When Christ heals the leper, multiplies loaves, walks on water, casts out demons, raises Lazarus — these are not magic shows. Each is a *sign* (the Gospel of John uses the word *semeia*, signs) that points beyond itself to the doctrine it confirms. *He multiplied loaves* — *He is the Bread of Life*. *He stilled the storm* — *He is Lord over creation*. *He raised the dead* — *He is the Resurrection and the Life*. Every miracle is a brief unveiling of the divinity that, normally, walked covered in the mud of the road.

**2. The teaching cannot be separated from the teacher.** Modern readers sometimes admire Christ's moral teaching while declining the dogmatic claim that He is God. The Gospels do not allow this. *No man hath spoken like this man* (John 7:46) — and *No man cometh to the Father but by Me* (John 14:6). C. S. Lewis's classical formulation, drawing on a long Catholic intuition: a man who said what Christ said *was either a liar, a lunatic, or the Lord*. The teaching makes no sense apart from the teacher's identity.

**3. The miracles continue.** The catechism does not stop at the Gospels. *Christ has not stopped being God*. The Church has *over the centuries* received reliably attested miracles — the saints, the Eucharistic miracles (Lanciano, Bolsena, Buenos Aires), the cures at Lourdes, the conversions sealed by visions. These are not necessary for the faith (the Resurrection is sufficient), but **the same Christ who multiplied loaves is the same Christ alive in the Church now**. Aquinas does not reduce miracles to a literary technique of the evangelists; he treats them as *real interventions* that the Church continues to record.

Practical takeaway: Christ's public life is not biography to be admired from a distance. It is the **pattern of action you are baptized into**. He fed the hungry; you feed the hungry. He healed the broken; you visit the sick. He drew the children near; you are kind to children. He cast out demons; you pray for the oppressed. He preached the kingdom; you tell people, in your own words, what you have come to know.

Today, choose **one** action of Christ from the Gospels and *imitate it once*. Most Christian holiness is no more complicated than that.
