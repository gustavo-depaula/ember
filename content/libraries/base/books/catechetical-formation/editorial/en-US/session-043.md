Yesterday's session named *what* the Third Commandment requires (Mass first, the rest after). Today's session goes deeper: ***why does the Christian rest at all?***

The Western world has largely forgotten that **rest is a religious act**. Modern *self-care* and *vacation* are degraded descendants of Sabbath rest, and they fail in the way the original would not, because they have lost the *direction*. *Sabbath is rest, but it is rest with a direction* — the day's ekphrasis is exactly right.

Aquinas walks four reasons the Christian is bound to rest on Sunday, each grounded in something deeper than mere weekly fatigue:

  * **Because creation rested.** *In six days the Lord made heaven and earth, and rested on the seventh* (Exodus 20:11). The seven-day rhythm of human life is not an arbitrary social convention. It is *built into the order of being itself*. To work seven days a week, week after week, is to lie about *what time is for*.

  * **Because rest is the *practice* of contemplation.** Aquinas: the active life prepares for the contemplative life, and the Sabbath is the contemplative life inserted into the week. To rest *toward God* — at Mass, in the long Sunday afternoon, in the slow meal with people you love, in the visit to a sick friend, in the nap — is the body learning, even briefly, what eternity will feel like.

  * **Because rest is *justice* to the worker.** The Sabbath was given as a labor protection long before any modern labor movement. The catechism cites the original injunction: *Six days shalt thou labour... but the seventh day is the Sabbath of the Lord thy God: thou shalt do no work on it, thou, nor thy son, nor thy daughter, nor thy manservant, nor thy maidservant... that thy manservant and thy maidservant may rest, even as thyself* (Deuteronomy 5:13–14). Modern application: if you employ anyone, **let them rest on Sunday**. Do not be the customer whose order forces a shift.

  * **Because rest is the *taste of heaven*.** Aquinas, following Augustine, calls the Sabbath *the figure of the Sabbath of the world to come*. Every well-kept Sunday is a small rehearsal of the eternity for which we were made.

Modern obstacles to keeping Sunday — and the Christian responses:

  * *I work in healthcare / hospitality / public service.* Necessary work is permitted; Christ Himself defended it. Find another fixed time for Mass and rest within the week.
  * *I have small children, there is no rest.* True, but Sunday with children at Mass *is* the rest, even when it does not feel like it. The grace of the day still applies.
  * *Sunday is when I catch up on errands.* This is the modern temptation. The remedy is small and concrete: do *one* errand fewer; *let* one thing wait until Monday.

Today, plan next Sunday before it arrives. Where will you be at Mass time? Whom will you call? What will you not do? **Plan the day, even briefly. Then let it teach you eternity.**
