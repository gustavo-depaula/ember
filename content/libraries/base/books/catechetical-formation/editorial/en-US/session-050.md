St. Thomas wrote about gossip in 1273, when a rumor traveled at walking speed and reputations were damaged one village at a time. Pius X wrote about it in 1912, before radio. Both teachings stand. But the **acceleration** in our own century has made the Eighth Commandment perhaps the most quietly violated of all the ten — by Christians who would never imagine themselves murderers or thieves.

The principle is simple. *A good name is better than great riches* (Aquinas, citing Proverbs 22:1). To strip someone of theirs unjustly — even by repeating what someone else said, even *truthfully*, when it serves no good purpose — is the medieval sin of **detraction**. To strip them by *false* report is **calumny**. To listen with pleasure when someone else does it to a third party is **co-detraction**. To leap to a conclusion about a person from a screenshot, a quoted sentence, a stranger's account, is **rash judgment**. The catechism above lists every species; we live in an age that has industrialized them.

Three modern corollaries follow.

  1. **Posting is speech.** A retweet is testimony. A like is co-detraction. The Commandment binds the same way it binds the spoken word in the village square. The medium does not soften the moral act.

  2. **Verify before you carry.** Pius X's eighth commandment orders us to *interpret in a good sense, when possible, the actions of our neighbor*. The ancient Catholic discipline is to assume the most charitable plausible interpretation, and to refuse to forward what we have not verified. Most of the world's quiet evil comes from the second sentence in the rumor, not the first.

  3. **Repair what you have torn.** Pius X is unflinching: *one who has harmed his neighbor in his good name must repair the harm caused, as far as he is able.* If you have shared something untrue or unkind about a person, the obligation is real. Take it down. Where the damage cannot be undone, name it in confession and pray for the person.

Practice silence today. *Hedge in thy ears with thorns* (Sirach 28:24, in Aquinas's quotation): when a conversation turns to gossip, **change the subject**. Truth is not only what you say. It is also what you refuse to repeat.
