A **vow** is the strongest form of speech a Christian has. To vow is to *call God to witness* a promise so binding that breaking it is a direct insult to His truth. The Second Commandment forbids using the divine name lightly; one of its corollaries is that **the vows we *do* make must be kept**. Pius X above is exact: *whoever swears to do evil, or perjures himself, gravely offends God, who is Holiness and Truth.*

Aquinas distinguishes between the lawful and the unlawful vow:

  * **Lawful vows** must meet three conditions: the matter must be *good* (you cannot vow what is sinful or impossible); the matter must be *better* than its alternative (vowing to skip Mass is not a vow, it is a lie); and the vow must be made *deliberately*, not in the heat of emotion or compulsion.

  * **Unlawful vows** include: vows to do something evil (no obligation to keep them — they are void); vows beyond one's authority (a child cannot vow what a parent has the right to forbid); vows made under duress.

The Christian vows you have likely already made:

  * **Baptismal promises** — *I renounce Satan, and all his works, and all his empty promises*. Made for you by your godparents if you were baptized as an infant; assumed by you the moment you reached the age of reason. The Church renews them at every Easter Vigil. They are still binding.
  * **Marital vows** — if you are married, you have made the gravest vow a layperson makes. *For better, for worse, until death.* This is not a contract you can renegotiate when the marriage becomes hard. The grace of the sacrament strengthens you to keep them; *ask for it.*
  * **Religious vows** — for those in consecrated life: poverty, chastity, obedience, taken publicly before God and the Church.

The Hannah of today's image (1 Samuel 1) made a private vow before knowing if it would be answered. *If thou wilt give thy servant a son, I will give him to the Lord all the days of his life.* God answered. She kept the vow — Samuel became Israel's prophet. The vow was costly. It was also the door through which God's plan entered the world.

Two cautions and one practice for living this commandment well:

  * **Do not multiply vows.** Aquinas's counsel, distilled: promise less, and keep what you promise. The casual *I swear I'll do this* by which we accent ordinary speech is the misuse the commandment forbids. Let your speech be *yes, yes; no, no* (Matthew 5:37).
  * **Honor the vows you have already made.** Especially baptismal and marital. The temptation in our age is to treat vows as expressions of *current feeling*; the Christian treats them as expressions of *received truth*.
  * **One private vow is sometimes very right.** A *temporary vow* — to abstain from alcohol for Lent, to pray a daily rosary for thirty days, to give a fixed sum for a specific need — can be a powerful spiritual practice. Make it deliberately, write it down, fulfill it, end it cleanly.
