The catechism above uses the older name *Extreme Unction* — the *last* anointing. Since the Second Vatican Council, the Church more commonly calls it the **Anointing of the Sick**, and the change is more than cosmetic. Pius X already knows what the renaming will later make explicit: this sacrament is for the *gravely ill*, not just the dying. It is administered "when the illness is dangerous" — Pius X's own phrase — and it can be received again with each new serious illness, and again as a chronic illness worsens. The widespread idea that Anointing must be reserved for the last hour is pastoral folklore, not Catholic doctrine.

The scriptural foundation is one verse, three commands:

> *Is any man sick among you? Let him bring in the priests of the Church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil in the name of the Lord. And the prayer of faith shall save the sick man. And the Lord shall raise him up; and if he be in sins, they shall be forgiven him.* (James 5:14–15)

Three things happen: prayer, anointing, forgiveness. Pius X's catechism details a fourth: bodily healing, *if it is good for the soul*. The sacrament is real medicine. Sometimes God uses it to restore health. Always He uses it to restore the soul.

What it does to the soul is striking. Even mortal sins are forgiven *if the sick person, being only attrite, cannot confess*. Attrition — sorrow for sin from the recognition of God's justice or from honest disgust at one's own sin — is enough where confession is impossible. This is the Church speaking gently to a dying person who can no longer speak.

If someone you love is gravely ill, **call the priest early**. Not at the last hour. Now. The Anointing strengthens the sick person to bear illness, resists the temptations that come at the end of strength, prepares the soul, and very often quietly heals the body. *Pray for the dying. Some have no one else.*
