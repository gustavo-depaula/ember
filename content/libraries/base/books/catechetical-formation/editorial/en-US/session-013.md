The catechism above takes up the *Promise* — the long arc of the Old Testament that culminates in the Annunciation. From the moment after the Fall, when God promised that *the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head* (Genesis 3:15 — the *Protoevangelium*, the *first gospel*), Israel waited. Patriarchs, prophets, kings, exiles — every one of them, in some way, was *waiting for Him*.

What did the Promise contain? Aquinas walks the principal Old Testament prophecies the catechism above presupposes:

  * **From Abraham**, through whom *all the nations of the earth shall be blessed* (Genesis 22:18) — the promise of a Redeemer of universal scope.
  * **From the tribe of Judah** (Genesis 49:10), the kingly line.
  * **From David**, whose throne would *endure forever* (2 Samuel 7:13–16).
  * **Born of a virgin** — *behold, a virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and his name shall be called Emmanuel* (Isaiah 7:14).
  * **In Bethlehem** (Micah 5:2), of all the towns of Judea.
  * **A Suffering Servant** — Isaiah 53, the most uncannily detailed prophecy of the Passion, written 700 years before.
  * **Beginning his public mission** at a specific historical moment (*the seventy weeks* of Daniel 9), under specific Roman governance (*the scepter shall not depart from Judah until Shiloh come*, Genesis 49:10 again).

When the Annunciation came (Luke 1:26–38), it gathered every thread of the long Promise into a single sentence — *the Holy that shall be born of thee shall be called the Son of God*. **Mary's *fiat* is the moment Israel's long waiting becomes Christendom's long preparation.**

Two doctrinal points the catechism is asking the reader to recover:

  * **The Old Testament is not a discarded preface.** The Church reads from it in the lectionary every day for a reason: it is the *grammar* of what the New Testament reveals. *Whatever was written for our learning was written* (Romans 15:4). To know Christ well, you must know the prophets He fulfilled.

  * **The Annunciation is the doctrinal hinge of history.** Aquinas: at the moment of Mary's *yes*, the eternal Son took a human nature in her body. *A body forms in her like dawn forming in fog.* Time itself is split by this moment: B.C. and A.D., *before Christ* and *the year of the Lord*. The Christian calendar is the catechism in dates.

A small practice: this week, **read Isaiah 53 slowly**, with the awareness that it was written seven centuries before Christ. Then read the Passion in the Gospel of John. Notice how the prophet has *already* described what only happened later. **The Promise was kept.** It was always going to be kept. He is keeping His promises to you, also, even when you cannot yet see how.
