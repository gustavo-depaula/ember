**You are a body lit by a soul that will not die.** The catechism above teaches the doctrine the modern world has done its best to forget — that the human person is *one being* in *two natures*, body and soul, indivisibly until death and reunited at the resurrection.

Catholic anthropology is **strict** on this point against several errors:

  * **Materialism** says you are *only* a body — neurons, chemistry, evolution producing the illusion of a self. The Church rejects this. Aquinas, working with Aristotle, argues from the very nature of *thought* that the soul cannot be merely material: thought reaches universal truths (mathematics, justice, the concept of being itself) that no purely physical thing can grasp. **What thinks must be more than matter.**

  * **Spiritualism / dualism** says the body is a costume the soul will one day shed. The Church rejects this too. The body is *part of who you are*. Christ rose bodily; you will rise bodily (Session 033). Despising the body is a Christian heresy, not a Christian humility.

  * **Modern reductionism** says the soul is just *the experience of brain activity* — when the brain dies, the soul ends. The Church rejects this most firmly: the soul is **directly created by God** for each human being and is **immortal**. Aquinas, again, argues from the soul's *immaterial operations* (thinking, willing freely): an immaterial thing cannot be destroyed by the dissolution of a material body.

The doctrine has profound consequences:

  * **Free will is real.** The catechism above teaches: *man can do evil also; but he must not do it, precisely because it is evil*. Materialism cannot ground freedom — chemistry has no *ought*. The Catholic claim is that you are *responsible* for your acts because you are *free* to do otherwise. This is uncomfortable; it is also the reason your life means anything.

  * **You can pray for the dying.** A soul does not switch off at the moment of biological death. The deathbed is a *real moment of decision*, sometimes lasting after appearances would suggest. The Christian who prays for the dying — known and unknown — is interceding for a real soul *still in time*.

  * **Suicide and euthanasia are theologically grave** — not because the body is sacred and the soul is irrelevant, but because *each is a person*, and we have no authority over the *whole person*'s timing. (See Session 045.)

The Sistine *Creation of Adam* (today's image) is the icon. *Two fingers nearly touch — God's, full of intention; Adam's, slack with new being.* The detail is theological: God reaches; the human is *given* the spark. **You did not generate your soul. You received it. It is the part of you that came directly from Him.**

Today, take five minutes alone — no phone, no music, no input — and notice that *something is reading these words*. That something is your soul. He made it. He is sustaining it now. Speak to Him from there.
