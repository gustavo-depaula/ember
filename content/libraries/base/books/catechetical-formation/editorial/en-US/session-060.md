Yesterday named *what* a sacrament is. Today's session asks the harder question: ***how does a sacrament give grace?*** The answer the Catholic Church holds — against the great counter-claim of the Reformation — is short, scandalous, and exact: **the sacraments give grace by their own action, *ex opere operato* — "from the work worked."**

The phrase deserves unpacking. It does *not* mean magic. It does *not* mean the recipient's disposition does not matter. It means: **the sacrament does what it does because Christ Himself acts in it.** Not because the priest is holy. Not because the recipient is feeling devout. Not because the community gathered is a good community. The Mass said by a sinful priest, on a flat day, with a distracted congregation, is **still the Mass** — the same sacrifice as Calvary, the same Body and Blood. The grace flows from Christ's action, not from anyone's worthiness.

This is the Catholic answer to a Protestant scrupulosity that has crept into Catholic minds: *did I receive that sacrament well? Was the priest a good man? Did I feel anything?* The doctrine relieves you. The sacraments are *objective*. **They are real because Christ said they are.**

Aquinas adds the corollary that prevents the doctrine from collapsing into magic. The sacraments produce their full effect only in a recipient who is *not placing an obstacle*. Receiving Communion in mortal sin does not bestow grace; it adds the sin of sacrilege to the sin already there. Confessing without contrition (sorrow + purpose of amendment) does not absolve. The sacrament's *power* is from Christ; the *fruit* depends, in part, on us.

From where, then, does the power come? Fra Angelico's image (today's ekphrasis) is theologically exact: *from the side of the crucified Christ, blood and water flow.* The Fathers, beginning with St. Augustine, read this as the birth of the sacraments: **water for Baptism, blood for the Eucharist**, and by extension all seven flowing from the same wound. Every absolution given, every Eucharist consecrated, every anointing, every marriage blessed — *all of it* is the application of Calvary to a particular soul, in a particular moment, two thousand years later.

The sacraments are not free in the sense that nothing was paid. **He paid.** Receive them, then, with the gratitude one shows toward an inheritance bought at infinite cost. Take none of them lightly. Take none of them anxiously, either. They are objective. They are sufficient. They are *for you*.
