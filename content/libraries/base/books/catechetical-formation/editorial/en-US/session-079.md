The catechism above unfolds the **nature** of marriage as the Catholic Church receives it: a covenant between one man and one woman, *for life*, *open to life*, raised by Christ to the dignity of a sacrament. The Giotto *Marriage at Cana* (today's image) is theologically exact in its choice of moment — Christ's first public miracle is at *a wedding*, and what He works there is the **transformation of water into wine** — the foundational sign that marriage is one of the places where ordinary things become extraordinary by His action.

Three things the catechism is asking the modern reader to recover.

**1. Marriage is older than the Church and older than the State.** The catechism teaches that the natural institution of marriage was *raised by Christ* to the dignity of a sacrament — *but the institution itself was given at creation* (Genesis 2:24, *the two shall become one flesh*; cited again by Christ Himself in Matthew 19:5). Neither civil legislation nor ecclesiastical practice invented marriage; both *recognize* it. When the State changes its civil definition, the natural and sacramental reality is unchanged. **The Catholic mind keeps the older boundary.**

**2. The ministers of the sacrament are the spouses themselves.** Unlike Baptism, Confirmation, the Eucharist, Penance, Anointing, and Holy Orders — all of which require a priest, deacon, or bishop as ordinary minister — the *ministers* of Matrimony are *the man and the woman exchanging their vows*. The priest *witnesses* the sacrament on behalf of the Church; he does not *confer* it. This is theologically striking. **Two laypeople, in vowing themselves to each other for life before God, *make* the sacrament that bonds them.**

**3. The three goods of marriage** (Aquinas, following Augustine, codified by the Council of Florence and Trent):

  * ***Bonum prolis***, the good of children. Marriage is *ordered to* the procreation and education of children. Not every marriage will be blessed with children — God's providence works in mysterious ways — but every marriage must be *open* to them. Deliberately excluding children is contrary to the sacrament's nature.
  * ***Bonum fidei***, the good of fidelity. The two are *each other's* exclusively, in body and in heart. Adultery wounds the sacrament; pornography wounds it; emotional affairs wound it. The fidelity asked is total.
  * ***Bonum sacramenti***, the good of the sacrament. The bond is *for life*; it is *indissoluble* once consummated. *What God hath joined together, let no man put asunder* (Mt 19:6). Modern Catholic life has not always known how to say this without sounding harsh; the catechism does not soften it.

For married readers: examine which of the three goods you have honored well, and which has weakened. Restore the weak one this week, by one small act.

For unmarried readers: the marriages around you — including, especially, your parents' — are upheld more by the prayers of those near them than they will ever know. Pray for one married couple by name today.
