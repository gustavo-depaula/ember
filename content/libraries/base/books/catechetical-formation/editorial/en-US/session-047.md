Two of the hardest moral demands in the Gospel sit together in today's catechism: **do not scandalize**, and **love your enemies**.

**Scandal**, in the Catholic sense, is not "being shocked." It is the older meaning: from the Greek *skandalon*, a stumbling block — *anything you do that becomes the occasion of sin in someone else's soul.* Pius X is unflinching: *God will demand an account of the evil that one causes others to commit by perfidious incitements and by bad example.* You will not be judged only on your own sins. You will also be judged on the sins others committed because they followed where you led.

The doctrine has unsettling reach in our age. The post that spread cynicism. The ridicule that taught a younger sibling to despise virtue. The drinking habit that the children watched and copied. The cruelty that an employee learned from their manager. *Anything you do that becomes the occasion of sin in another* — including indirectly, including unintentionally, including online. Aquinas would call us to a careful examination of conscience here, not for paralysis but for clarity. *What of mine has been imitated, and to what end?*

**Love of enemies** is the other half. Pius X's phrasing — *to wish well to all, even to our enemies* — is mild only if you have not actually tried it. We can usually manage *not retaliating*. We can sometimes manage *being civil*. To genuinely *will the good* of someone who has wronged you, including the eternal good of their soul, including their flourishing, including their joy — that is the work of grace, not nature. Christ on the cross is the catechism's image: He prays *for those killing Him*. Not for their punishment. For their forgiveness.

Two practical disciplines this week:

  * **Pray, by name, for one person you cannot stand.** Pray that they may know the love of God; that they may flourish; that they may, even now, become a saint. Persist for thirty days. The interior shift is real. The first to be changed is you.

  * **Audit one habit you might be teaching.** Children are watching. So are colleagues, siblings, the strangers you do not know are watching. *Woe to that man through whom scandal comes.* The verse is severe, but the remedy is simple: live so the imitation of you would lead someone toward Christ, not away.
