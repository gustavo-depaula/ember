Modern Catholic life often pretends the doctrine of the *first parents* is folklore, a children's story to be politely set aside in adulthood. The Church has never permitted this. The catechism above teaches what *Humani Generis* (Pius XII, 1950) ratified for the modern era: **monogenism — the doctrine that the human race descends from one original couple, who possessed real bodies, real souls, and a real condition of grace before they fell.**

The Church distinguishes carefully between **the literary mode of Genesis 2–3 and the historical truth it conveys**. We are not bound to read the seven days literally, or the talking serpent literally, or to insist that the Garden was at a particular latitude. We *are* bound to hold:

  * **The first parents were real.** Two real human beings, the father and mother of every human being who has lived since.
  * **They possessed *original justice*.** Body and soul integrated, the soul in friendship with God, the passions in harmony with reason, exempt from death and from suffering. *Naked, unashamed, the lion lying down with the lamb.* The painter (today's image) remembers what we forgot.
  * **They were given a real choice**, and they really chose. The fruit may or may not have been a literal apple; the choice was a real act of disobedience by which they refused the love and the life God had given.

What we *lost* in the Fall was *not* nature itself — we are still made in God's image, still capable of reason and love — but **original justice**: the soul's integration with God, the body's exemption from corruption, the harmony of passions with reason. *Concupiscence* (the disordered tug of desire) and *mortality* (the wage of sin) entered as a result.

The doctrine has therapeutic consequences. **You can recognize the disorder you feel in yourself for what it is.** That fight in your soul between what you should do and what you want to do is not a personal failure of resolve; it is *the human condition* after the Fall. Aquinas, with St. Paul, calls this *the law in my members* (Romans 7:23). It is real; it is universal; *it is not what was meant for you*.

The Christian response is twofold:

  * **Take seriously the disorder.** Do not pretend you are not wounded. Pray, fast, confess, receive the Eucharist — the sacraments are precisely the medicine of original sin's lingering effects.
  * **Take seriously the original goodness.** *We were happy once. We can be again.* Christ came not to lower His expectations of you but to **restore the original**. The grace He gives is not consolation for being broken; it is the energy by which the broken thing is made whole.

The painter remembers. The catechism remembers. *We were made for the Garden.* We are walking back toward it, by stations of grace, until we cross over.
