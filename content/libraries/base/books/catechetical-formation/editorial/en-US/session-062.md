**Most of us slept through our Baptism.** The ekphrasis is right. Baptism is the most decisive event in our biographies — and we have no memory of it. The catechism above is asking us to recover that day with our adult mind.

What happened? Three things. **First**, original sin was washed away — the inherited deprivation of grace that every human being inherits from Adam. The water did this not because water is magic but because Christ joined Himself to it; *Baptism is*, St. Thomas writes, *a configuration to the death of Christ* (Romans 6:3 again, the verse Pius X cites). The old self is buried; the new self rises. **Second**, sanctifying grace was poured in for the first time — the divine life sharing with you the inner life of the Trinity. **Third**, an indelible character was imprinted on the soul, marking you as Christ's, His own, irrevocably. The character cannot be lost. The grace can be lost (by mortal sin) and recovered (by confession), but the character abides. Even a baptized soul in hell is still a baptized soul.

This is why the Church baptizes infants. It is not a casual rite. It is the gift of the entire Christian dispensation, given freely, before the soul can resist or pretend it has earned anything. The sacrament's power *comes from Christ's passion*, not from the disposition of the recipient or the worthiness of the minister. *In case of necessity, anyone can baptize* — Pius X is unambiguous — provided the form is used: *I baptize thee in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost*. There are children alive in heaven now because a parent in a hospital corridor did exactly this.

The first grace is also the door to all the others. **Renew your baptismal promises** today — not as ritual recitation but as adult assent to what was done to you when you could not yet say *I do.* Do you reject Satan, and all his works, and all his empty promises? *I do.* Do you believe in God the Father almighty, Creator of heaven and earth? *I do.* Live the day from inside that yes.
