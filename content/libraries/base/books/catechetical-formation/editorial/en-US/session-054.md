Each remaining precept of the Church teaches the body something the soul needs to learn.

**Fasting** teaches the body that it is not the master. St. Thomas argues, with the Fathers, that fasting orders three things at once: it offers reparation for sin, it disciplines the appetite that pulls us toward dissipation, and it lifts the mind toward prayer. The body, fed too easily, makes the soul thick. Fasting is not contempt of food (which is good) but a deliberate small *no* by which the body learns to obey something greater than its hunger.

**Yearly confession and Easter Communion** are the absolute minimum, but St. Thomas notes the word *at least* in the precept: the Church names this floor only because we are weak, and she desires far more — frequent confession to keep the soul washed, frequent Communion to keep it fed. The yearly precept is the lowest threshold of being called a practicing Catholic; the Christian who lives only at the threshold is starving with bread on the table.

**Offering for the needs of the Church** answers a question the modern Catholic rarely asks: *who pays for this?* The Mass that consoles you, the priest who hears your confession, the building you walk into for a wedding or a funeral — these are sustained by the offerings of the baptized. To give is to participate in the work of the Body, not to fund a charity.

These are not commercial transactions. They are little surrenders, repeated, until the heart learns the rhythm of yes.
