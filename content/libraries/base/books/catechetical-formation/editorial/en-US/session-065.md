Yesterday's session named what Confirmation *is* (Session 064). Today's session asks what *prepares* a soul for it — and what should change *after* it.

The catechism above is mostly directed at parents and pastors of catechumens; the editorial is for the rest of us, **who were confirmed years ago and may have done little with the grace since**. Two questions for the readers in that camp:

  * **What did your Confirmation give you that you have not used?** The seven gifts of the Holy Spirit (wisdom, understanding, counsel, fortitude, knowledge, piety, fear of the Lord) were *infused* in your soul on the day. They are still there. They are *dormant* in proportion as you have not asked the Spirit to activate them. The remedy is small: **name one gift, daily for a week, and ask for it specifically.** *Holy Spirit, give me counsel today. I do not know what to do; tell me.* The gift is given. Use it.

  * **Whom can you confirm?** Not sacramentally — that is the bishop's role. But the *confirmational disposition* — being a soldier of Christ — includes *strengthening other Christians* who are wavering. Pius X is exact: the sacrament makes us *perfect Christians and soldiers of Christ Jesus*. The soldier's first duty after enlistment is to *cover the flank* of those near him. Pray for the Confirmation candidates in your parish this year, by name if you can find the names. Befriend a young Catholic who is struggling. The grace flows through you to them.

For those preparing now — or for parents preparing a child:

  * Choose a confirmation name carefully. The patron saint will be your child's lifelong companion in heaven. Read the saint's life *before* choosing.
  * Take Confession well in advance, then again the day before. Confirmation infuses fully into a soul *in the state of grace*.
  * Approach the bishop with reverence; *kiss his ring* if it is offered. He stands as a successor of the Apostles in that moment, and the gesture is doctrinally significant — recognition of the apostolic succession by which the sacrament is what it is.
  * After the day, **practice the gifts**. Wisdom is exercised by sitting with Scripture daily. Counsel is exercised by asking advice from a wise person and following it. Fortitude is exercised by saying yes to one hard thing this week. The gifts grow with use.

Poussin's image (today's ekphrasis) — *the bishop laying his hand on a young head* — is theologically exact. *The first hand is gentle, but the work after is real.*
