You will say these words today, perhaps mechanically. The catechism above is asking you to slow down on each one.

***Our.*** Christ does not teach us to say *my* Father. He teaches us **our**. The first word of the Lord's Prayer is already a renunciation of the religion of self. Even when you pray it alone, in the dark, with the door closed, you are praying with the Church — past, present, future. Every Mass everywhere in the world is praying *with* you when you pray it. The Christian who prays *Our Father* is never alone.

***Father.*** Aquinas walks the three ways God is our Father — by special creation, by governance, and by adoption (whose fruit is inheritance: *if sons, heirs also*, Romans 8:17). The deepest is the third: through Baptism you have been *adopted* into the family of the Trinity. Christ is the eternal Son; you are an adopted son or daughter, with real legal standing in the divine household. *Heir* is the word St. Paul will not back away from. This is the Christian claim.

It is also the Christian *crisis* for many readers. If your earthly father was absent, harsh, cold, or violent, the word *Father* may carry a wound, not a gift. The teaching of the Church here is gentle and exact: God is not the projection of your father; **He is the correction of every human fatherhood**. He is what your father was meant to be and was not. Christ, in revealing the Father, is healing the human imagination of the Father. If the word is hard for you, do not abandon it. Sit with it. The healing happens precisely there.

***Who art in heaven.*** Aquinas insists that *in heaven* does not mean *far away*. It means *the throne from which He sees and rules*. He is high not as a distance but as a sovereignty. *He is nearer to us*, Aquinas writes, *than we are to ourselves.* The transcendence is the source of the intimacy: a god small enough to be your psychological projection cannot save anyone; the God whose throne is the heavens, who sees you in the closet — *He* can.

Today, when you pray the Our Father, **slow down on the first six words**. Six words are enough for an entire prayer.
