Modern ears will catch on Trent's language about *subjection*. The 1566 pastoral world spoke this way; it was naming an order it had received but had not yet articulated as fully as St. Paul actually had. Read the verse the Apostle himself put one line *before* his words to husbands and wives:

> *Be subject one to another, in the fear of Christ.* (Ephesians 5:21)

That is the Christian frame. In a marriage, both spouses are bound to give themselves over for the good of the other. The headship of the husband, on this reading, is the first servant of the household, not the first ruler. Christ is head of the Church the way He is head of the Last Supper — washing feet.

What St. Thomas, the Roman Catechism, and Pius X all see is that married love is *sacramental*: it actually transmits grace. The vows the spouses speak on the wedding day are the matter of the sacrament; their faithful living of those vows is its form, day by day, until death. The ministers of the sacrament are not the priest or the deacon — they are each spouse to the other. Through their *yes* — given in the kitchen, in the hospital corridor, at three in the morning with a feverish child, at the family Mass when nobody else came — God is becoming visible in the world.

Pius X names three concrete duties: *to live together in holiness, to help one another with constant affection in their spiritual and temporal needs, and to raise their children well — caring for their soul no less than for their body.* Each is a small sacrament within the great sacrament. Holiness is not what spouses achieve in spite of marriage. It is what the marriage is **for**.

If you are not yet married, today's catechism is also for you. The chastity that prepares a soul for marriage is the same chastity that prepares a soul for celibacy or singleness. The marriages around you — including your parents' — are held up by your prayers far more than they will ever know.
