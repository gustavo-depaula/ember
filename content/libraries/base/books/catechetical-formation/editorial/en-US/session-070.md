If you have understood transubstantiation (Session 067) — that the host on the altar *is* Jesus Christ, entirely — then **Eucharistic adoration is not a pious add-on; it is the only intellectually consistent response**. If He is there, you should be on your knees.

The early Church reserved the Eucharist primarily for the sick — *that they might receive at the hour of death*. Over the centuries, as Catholic devotion deepened, the practice of *visiting* the Reserved Eucharist developed. By the medieval period, the *exposition* of the Blessed Sacrament — the Host placed visibly in a *monstrance*, a sun-shaped vessel of gold and glass — became one of the great public devotions of the Church. The *Forty Hours' Devotion*, the *Holy Hour* before the First Friday Mass, the *Perpetual Adoration* chapels in many parishes — all are different forms of one thing: **time spent before Christ really present.**

Some Catholics treat adoration as advanced or optional. It is neither. The catechism above is clear: the Eucharist is *preserved in churches so that the faithful may adore it*. Adoration is what the Tabernacle is *for*.

What happens during a Holy Hour? **Almost nothing.** That is its glory.

  * You enter the chapel. You genuflect. You sit, kneel, or stand quietly.
  * You may pray vocal prayers (a rosary; a litany; the Office). You may simply *be* with Him without words.
  * Your mind will wander. Bring it back. Wander again. Bring it back. **The wandering is not a failure**; the *return* is what adoration is.
  * Sometimes you will feel His presence. Often you will feel nothing. *Sense fails*, as Aquinas warned. Faith reports what sense cannot.

The Catholic saints who have written on this — Francis de Sales, John Vianney, Margaret Mary Alacoque, Faustina, Mother Teresa — agree on the central testimony: **a soul is changed slowly by sitting before Christ**. The Curé of Ars said of his peasant who would visit the church daily: *I look at Him, and He looks at me.* That is enough.

Three practical disciplines:

  1. **One Holy Hour a week, at least.** Most parishes have a regular adoration time. Pick one. Make it permanent.
  2. **The visit on the way past.** Catholics of older generations would slip into a church for two minutes whenever they passed one. Recover the practice. Two minutes a day before the Tabernacle changes a year.
  3. **Spiritual Communion** when sacramental Communion is impossible. *I cannot receive You sacramentally now; come spiritually into my heart and remain there.* The classical formula. Christ honors it.

The Mass of St. Gregory (today's image) is the legend of a saint who saw the Lord on the altar at the words of consecration. The doctrine says **the Lord is on every altar**, even when no saint sees Him. Go anyway. Sit before Him. *There is nowhere better for a soul to spend an hour.*
