The cardinal virtues are called *cardinal* from the Latin *cardo* — a hinge. The doors of the moral life swing on them. Take any virtue you can name — humility, generosity, courage in suffering, gentleness with a difficult parent — and you will find one of the four cardinals holding it open: **prudence**, **justice**, **fortitude**, **temperance**.

St. Thomas explains them as the virtues that perfect the four powers in us that need ordering. Prudence perfects the practical reason. Justice perfects the will in its dealings with others. Fortitude steadies the *irascible* appetite — the part of us that fights for what is hard. Temperance moderates the *concupiscible* appetite — the part that wants pleasure. These are not abstractions:

- **Prudence** asks: *given what is real, what should I actually do now?*
- **Justice** asks: *what does this person, this commitment, this God deserve from me?*
- **Fortitude** asks: *what is hard, and how do I bear it without becoming bitter?*
- **Temperance** asks: *how much is enough?*

Christ Himself displays them all. He is prudent in the wilderness with the devil; just at the temple cleansing; brave at Gethsemane; temperate at every meal He shares with sinners. The virtues are not someone else's. They are His. And through grace, they become ours.

The evangelical counsels — voluntary poverty, perpetual chastity, perfect obedience — are an invitation, not a command. Some Christians are called to live them in the consecrated state, where the soul renounces three goods (possession, marriage, autonomy) so that nothing competes for the love of God. But every Christian is called to live their *spirit*: to hold what we own with open hands, to keep the heart undivided, to surrender pride to those Christ has placed over us. Cardinal virtues lived from this spirit are how the lay life becomes holy.

You do not have to wait until you feel courageous to act courageously. The catechism says it plainly: virtue is a *habit*, acquired by repeating good acts. Today, act once where you would normally hesitate. The hinge will hold.
