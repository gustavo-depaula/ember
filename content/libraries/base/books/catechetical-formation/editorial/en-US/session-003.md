You are about to walk for thirty-two days through the Apostles' Creed.

The temptation will be to treat the Creed as a quiz — a list of items you assent to, the way you assent to the terms-of-service on a phone. But that is not what the Creed is for. The Creed is not your *opinion* of God. It is the **deposit of faith** — what the Apostles received from Christ, what the Church has carried, intact, across twenty centuries, what is *handed to you*.

St. Thomas distinguishes carefully between what we *know* and what we *believe*. We know what reason can reach. We believe what God has revealed. Some truths sit on the boundary — the existence of one God, for example, can be reached by careful reasoning — but the full picture (one God in three Persons, the Father a Father, the Son begotten, the Spirit proceeding) only emerges because God Himself spoke. Faith is not lower than reason. Faith reaches what reason cannot. *Without faith it is impossible to please God* (Hebrews 11:6).

A *mystery*, as Pius X teaches, is not an unsolvable puzzle. It is *a truth above, but not contrary to, reason* — something larger than your mind, but not aimed against it. Twelve mysteries form the Creed; two are central (the Trinity and the Incarnation, with His Passion, Death, and Resurrection). The next thirty days will lead you into them.

Read with this expectation: you will not master the Creed. The Creed will master you. That is the entire point. The Christian life begins not when *you* decide what is true, but when you let *what is true* decide *you*.
