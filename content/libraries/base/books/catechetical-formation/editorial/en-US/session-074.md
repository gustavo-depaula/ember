**Contrition is the soul of penance.** Without it, the sacrament does not absolve — even if the priest pronounces the formula, even if you confess every sin in detail. The catechism above and the El Greco *Tears of Saint Peter* (today's image) are insisting on something often missed: **the necessary thing is not the count of sins; it is the *grief* with which they are brought**.

Aquinas, refined by the Council of Trent, distinguishes two kinds of contrition:

  * **Perfect contrition** — sorrow for sin *because sin offends God, who is all-good and worthy of all our love*. The motive is love itself. Perfect contrition, when joined to the *intention* to confess as soon as possible, **forgives mortal sin even before sacramental confession**. It cannot leave a soul unreconciled.

  * **Attrition** (or imperfect contrition) — sorrow for sin from a *lesser* but still genuine motive: fear of hell, recognition of the moral disorder of sin, embarrassment at one's own pattern, the awareness of how one has hurt others. Attrition is sufficient *for the sacrament*: combined with absolution, it forgives mortal sin. It is *not* sufficient by itself, without the sacrament, to forgive mortal sin — God provides for that case through perfect contrition or, if perfect contrition is impossible, through the sacrament's grace.

This distinction matters pastorally. The Christian who feels only attrition is *not unforgiven*. He is *invited to the sacrament*, where the priest's absolution will *complete* what his attrition has begun. The Christian who has perfect contrition has already begun to be reconciled, and the sacrament *seals* the reconciliation.

Three movements that lead the soul toward contrition:

  1. **Place yourself before the crucifix.** El Greco's Peter is not weeping in the abstract. He is weeping at having denied *the One whose face he has just seen*. The Christian does the same. Look at Him crucified; recall what your sin cost; feel what should be felt.

  2. **Pray the *act of contrition* slowly.** *O my God, I am heartily sorry for having offended Thee...* The classic formula spans both attrition (*because I dread the loss of heaven and the pains of hell*) and perfect contrition (*but most of all because they offend Thee, my God, who art all-good and deserving of all my love*). Both motives are voiced; the soul is given language.

  3. **Resolve specifically.** Contrition without *firm purpose of amendment* is incomplete. Name the *next concrete act* by which you will turn from this sin. The resolution does not promise that you will never fall; it promises that you will *try*, with grace, *not* to.

*Contrition is not embarrassment. It is the heart's recoil at having wounded the One it should have shielded.* Ask for it. The Holy Spirit gives it to whoever asks.
