Murillo's *Return of the Prodigal Son* (today's image) is the Catholic doctrine of **satisfaction and indulgences** rendered in pigment. The Father runs. He embraces. He calls for the best robe, the ring, the sandals, the fatted calf. The son's prepared speech of unworthiness is interrupted by the Father's *celebration*. **The mercy is already there.** The son walks into it.

But notice what the Father does *next*. He does not say *forget what happened*; He restores the son to the household with all its responsibilities. The fatted calf is killed; the elder brother is invited; the family meal is held *in the open*, where the village will see. **The son's restoration is real, public, and bodily.** That is what *satisfaction* is.

For Aquinas, satisfaction is what a son does, freely, after he has been received — not a payment to a creditor, but the *natural movement* of a healed soul toward repairing what it has damaged. Three layers of satisfaction the Christian tradition has always held:

  * **The penance the priest gives.** *Three Hail Marys*, or *one rosary*, or *go and reconcile with that person* — whatever the confessor assigns is **part of the sacrament itself**. Do it within twenty-four hours if humanly possible. The penance is not the price of forgiveness; the forgiveness has already happened. The penance is the *first act of the new life*.

  * **The ordinary satisfactions of the Christian life.** *Prayer, fasting, almsgiving* (Mt 6) — the three classical works of the soul that has been forgiven. These are not for show; they are the *body of contrition* lived out over time. Each Mass attended in piety, each act of mercy, each small fast, each spontaneous prayer for an enemy — all of it joins to Christ's satisfaction on the Cross and is received as *real reparation*.

  * **Indulgences.** Long misunderstood. The Church, by her own authority and drawing on the *inexhaustible treasury of merits* of Christ, Mary, and the saints, can apply that treasury to a soul to *remit the temporal punishment* still owed for sin already forgiven. Plenary indulgences (full remission, under specified conditions: confession, Communion, prayer for the Pope, total detachment from sin) and partial indulgences (less than full) are real spiritual gifts the Church makes available — for ourselves and for the dead. *The mercy is already there; we walk in.*

A small concrete weekly practice: each Friday, take five minutes to make a *general satisfaction* — *Lord, for what I have done this week, here is one small thing offered for it: this fast, this prayer, this dollar to the poor*. The accumulating effect over years is real reparation made through and with Christ.

The catechism above does not soften the doctrine, but neither does it lose the joy of it. *Indulgences open the same household stores wider.* Open them.
