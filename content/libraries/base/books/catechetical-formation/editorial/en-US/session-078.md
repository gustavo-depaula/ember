**The priesthood is not a profession.** That is the first thing the catechism above is asking the modern reader to recover. The priest is not a religious manager. Not a holy lecturer. Not a moral consultant. He is **the very hands of Christ when he says the words**. *In persona Christi capitis* — in the Person of Christ the Head — is the technical formula for what happens at the altar, in the confessional, at the bedside of the dying.

Aquinas walks the doctrine carefully. The priest possesses, by ordination, an *indelible character* (like Baptism and Confirmation, never lost). This character configures him to Christ in such a way that **certain acts performed by the priest are not the priest's acts at all; they are Christ's, working through him**. *I absolve you from your sins* is not the priest's pronouncement; it is Christ's, voiced through the priest's mouth, with the priest's authority *as Christ's*. The same is true of *This is My Body*. The bread becomes Christ because Christ Himself, working through this man, makes it so.

Two doctrinal corollaries:

  * **The unworthiness of an individual priest does not nullify his sacraments.** Augustine settled this against the Donatists in the early fifth century, and Aquinas systematized the teaching: the priest's holiness *helps* the people; his lack of it *grieves* God; **but his ordained acts remain valid**. The Mass said by a sinful priest is still the Mass; the absolution given by a struggling priest still absolves. The grace flows from Christ's action, not the priest's worthiness.

  * **There is one priesthood of Christ, with two participations.** Every baptized Christian shares in the *common priesthood of the faithful* — they offer the spiritual sacrifice of their lives. The ordained priest shares in the *ministerial priesthood* — he stands *in the Person of Christ* to consecrate the Eucharist, forgive sins sacramentally, and shepherd the people. Both priesthoods are real; they are not in competition; they are both ordered to the same Christ.

What the doctrine asks of the laity:

  * **Pray for priests.** *Daily, by name if you can.* The priest carries the souls of his parish on his back; he is attacked by the enemy more than most laity realize. *Lord, sanctify Your priests; protect them; give them rest.* This is one of the most needed prayers of our generation.

  * **Honor the office, not just the man.** A priest's tone, his theological inclinations, his quirks — these are real and may not always edify. But the *office* he carries is Christ's. Even when you cannot admire the man, you can honor what he does *in persona Christi*.

  * **Encourage vocations.** Most priests trace their first sense of call to **someone who said it out loud** — a parent, a friend, a teacher. *Have you ever thought about being a priest?* If you know a young man whose life seems to bend that direction, ask the question. The Church needs holy priests in our generation, and she will not have them unless families and parishes ask the question aloud.

The Easter image (today's ekphrasis) — *an old painting of an ordination, hands on the head, the bishop bending over a young man* — is the moment Christ in His Church reaches across two thousand years and continues to give priests to the people. Pray for priests today.
