> *The Samaritan tends a wounded stranger by lamplight. Hope dares; charity stoops. To love God is to refuse to set Him aside; to love your neighbor is to refuse to step over him. Both are happening in the painting.*

## Pius X asks

**238.** What is hope?

*Hope is that supernatural virtue by which we trust in God and expect from Him eternal life and the graces necessary to merit it here below by good works.*

**239.** For what reason do we hope from God for eternal life and the graces necessary to merit it?

*We hope from God for eternal life and the graces necessary to merit it because He, infinitely good and faithful, has promised them to us through the merits of Jesus Christ; therefore one who distrusts or despairs offends Him gravely.*

**240.** What is charity?

*Charity is that supernatural virtue by which we love God for Himself above all things, and our neighbor as ourselves for the love of God.*

**241.** Why must we love God?

*We must love God for Himself, as the supreme Good, the source of all our good; and therefore we must also love Him above all things "with all our heart, with all our soul, with all our mind, and with all our strength" (Mark XII. 30).*

**242.** Why must we love our neighbor?

*We must love our neighbor for the love of God, who commands it, and because every man is created in the image of God, as we are, and is our brother.*

**243.** Are we bound to love even our enemies?

*We are bound to love even our enemies, forgiving offenses, because they too are our neighbor, and because Jesus Christ has expressly commanded it.*

**244.** When must we make acts of faith, hope, and charity?

*We must make acts of faith, hope, and charity many times in life, and in particular when we have temptations to overcome or important Christian duties to fulfill, and in the dangers of death.*

**245.** Is it good to make acts of faith, hope, and charity often?

*It is good to make acts of faith, hope, and charity often, in order to preserve, increase, and strengthen virtues so necessary, which are like the vital parts of the "spiritual man."*

**246.** How must we make acts of faith, hope, and charity?

*We must make acts of faith, hope, and charity with the heart, with the mouth, and by works, giving proof of them in our conduct.*

**247.** How does one give proof of faith?

*One gives proof of faith by professing and defending it, when needed, without fear and without human respect, and by living according to its maxims: "faith without works is dead" (James II. 26).*

**248.** How does one give proof of hope?

*One gives proof of hope by not being troubled by the miseries and contrarieties of life, nor even by persecutions, but by living resigned, sure of the promises of God.*

**249.** How does one give proof of charity?

*One gives proof of charity by observing the commandments and exercising the works of mercy, and, if God calls, by following the evangelical counsels.*

*249 a. The seven corporal works of mercy: 1. To feed the hungry; 2. To give drink to the thirsty; 3. To clothe the naked; 4. To shelter the homeless; 5. To visit the sick; 6. To visit the imprisoned; 7. To bury the dead.*

*249 b. The seven spiritual works of mercy: 1. To counsel the doubtful; 2. To instruct the ignorant; 3. To admonish sinners; 4. To comfort the afflicted; 5. To forgive offences; 6. To bear wrongs patiently; 7. To pray for the living and the dead.*

## A pastoral reading

Hope is not optimism. Optimism is a temperament; hope is a virtue. Optimism predicts the weather; hope leans on a Person. St. Thomas teaches that hope reaches toward two things together — *eternal beatitude* as its end, and *the divine help* that makes that end possible. We hope **for** God, but only **with** God. The promise is His; the strength to walk to it is His; the courage to keep going after we have failed today is His.

That courage is what hope tastes like in ordinary life. The Christian who has fallen many times and gets up the seventy-seventh — that is hope. The Christian who confesses again the same sin she has confessed since adolescence — that is hope. The Christian who refuses to believe his own despair when it whispers *you are too far gone* — that is hope.

Charity, St. Thomas writes, is **friendship with God**. Not metaphorically. He grounds it on the words of Christ:

> *I will not now call you servants ... but I have called you friends.* (John 15:15)

Friendship requires shared life and shared affection, and grace makes both possible. From this friendship with God, every other love is reordered. We love God for His own sake. We love ourselves rightly because He loves us. We love our neighbor because they are made for Him too — and yes, even our enemies, because Christ named them as the testing ground of charity.

Charity is also what St. Thomas calls the **form** of the other virtues. Without it, faith and hope can exist (a person can believe and hope without yet loving), but the virtues do not yet *save* — they are like organs in a body where the heart is not beating. Today, then, attend to charity. Refuse despair. Refuse to step over the neighbor. Let one act of charity — small, deliberate, unseen — be the heartbeat that makes the other virtues alive.

> **Scripture.** *If any man say, I love God, and hateth his brother; he is a liar.* — 1 John 4:20

> *Lord, send me one neighbor today I cannot step over. Make me stoop.*
