## Practical Shape

**When.** Evening, before sleep, is the classical time — a final accounting of the day before it closes. Ignatius himself also practiced a midday examen, shorter, to reset the day. Pick one that you can keep. Begin with evening.

**How long.** Ten to fifteen minutes. Less, and you skim; more, and you turn it into meditation (different exercise). A timer helps at first.

**Keep a journal — lightly.** A notebook near the place of prayer. One line per step, not essays. Over weeks, patterns emerge: the same desolation returns at the same hour, the same consolation marks the same kind of encounter. This is the Spirit teaching you to read your own heart.

**For the busy day.** If fifteen minutes is impossible, take three: one breath of gratitude, one glance over the day, one sentence of contrition and resolution. A short examen daily beats a long examen weekly. Ignatius was realistic about this.

**Before confession.** The examen naturally matures into an examination of conscience for confession — the resolutions of several weeks show you what to bring. The two practices feed each other.

**Distraction.** If the mind wanders in step 3, gently return. If nothing comes to mind, ask the Spirit again. If the day feels flat, give thanks for the flatness — even that is given.
