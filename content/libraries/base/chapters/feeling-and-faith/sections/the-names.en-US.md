Two words from the old tradition of discernment, much abused by the world, and worth giving back their weight: **consolation** and **desolation**.

Consolation, as St. Ignatius means it, is not a good mood. It is an interior movement in which the soul is drawn toward God: a quiet warmth at prayer, an unexpected tenderness for a neighbor who used to irritate you, a tear that comes while reading the Gospel, a peace that settles without a reason, an increase of faith or hope or charity that you did not produce. The mark of true consolation is *direction*: the soul is being drawn **upward and outward**, toward the Lord, toward love, toward what it knows is good.

Desolation is the opposite movement. Not a bad mood, not sadness over a real loss — those are ordinary human things. Desolation, in the technical sense, is a pull *away* from God: an interior darkness, a dullness to the things of faith, a temptation to despondency, a restlessness that wants to abandon what was begun in the light. The mark of true desolation is also direction: the soul is being drawn **downward and inward**, toward discouragement, toward isolation, toward the abandonment of the practice.

Neither of these is a feeling *about* something. They are feelings *with* a vector. A sad mood after a hard conversation is not desolation in this sense; it is grief. An excited mood after an espresso is not consolation; it is caffeine. The rules that follow are not rules for managing moods — they are rules for reading what the inner life is doing *with* the movements that pass through it.

This is why the saints are careful with feelings but not contemptuous of them. Feelings are data. They are not the prayer itself, and they are not the faith itself, but they are a language in which the Holy Spirit often speaks — and in which the enemy often speaks too. Learning to read them is part of growing up in the interior life.

A word for our time. Modern spirituality often collapses faith into feeling, so that "I feel God" becomes the whole of it. This chapter rejects that, not because feelings are bad, but because they are *not enough*. The deepest prayer of Christ was offered in anguish, not consolation, and it saved the world. Your prayer on the dry day saves more than you know.

*"My soul is sorrowful even unto death."* (*Matthew 26:38*)
