Not every interior movement asks for action. Most do not. The first temptation of a soul learning discernment is to act on every flicker; the second, a reaction against the first, is to act on none. The middle path is old and unglamorous: *notice the movement, hold it up to the light, and ask what it is asking for, if anything.*

A rough taxonomy, in the form of questions.

**Is the movement toward or away from the ordinary duties of your state of life?** Toward your spouse, children, employer, parish, director — or away from them? The Holy Spirit does not pull a husband away from his wife into a higher "vocation" at 2 a.m.; the enemy does this routinely, dressed as zeal. Toward is a good sign. Away is almost always suspicious, however holy it looks.

**Is it toward the cross or away from it?** This is the sharpest test St. Ignatius gives. A true movement of grace, even when it offers a consolation, carries the shape of Christ's life — which includes accepting difficulty, humility, and obscurity. A false movement, even when it wears religious clothes, finds ways around the cross: around humbling apology, around the hard conversation, around the small daily fidelity you do not want to renew. If the inspiration will cost you something the old self is resisting, that is *a good sign*, not a bad one.

**Does it survive prayer?** A true inspiration can be held before the Lord and is only strengthened by the holding. A false one evaporates under the gaze, or becomes defensive, or starts generating arguments to justify itself. Sit with it in silence for a few days. Lay it before the tabernacle. If it still presses after a week and does not shrink under scrutiny, take it to your director. If it fades, let it fade.

**Does it bear the test of the director?** This is the final guardrail. The saints were suspicious of interior movements that did not want to be told to someone. An inspiration that tells you *"keep this to yourself — your director would not understand"* is almost certainly not from God. The Holy Spirit does not work around the ordinary means of sanctification; He works through them.

After these tests, most movements quiet down and take their place as what they are: passing weather, not messages. A few will keep pressing — those are the ones that ask for action. And the action the Lord asks is almost always *small and specific*. The grand inspirations of youth give way, over time, to the humble clarities of the mature soul: *call your brother tomorrow. Go to confession this week. Stop checking your phone before night prayer.* These are the calls that transform a life. The rest is often the imagination borrowing the language of prayer.

One final word, spoken for souls who have suffered from scruples or obsessive religious thought. If you are paralyzed by second-guessing every interior movement — if every feeling becomes a case to be solved, every consolation suspect of vanity, every desolation proof of sin — you need **not** more discernment but *less*, and you need it from a confessor, not from a book. The rules above are for souls navigating in clear waters. Souls with scrupulosity are in fog and must follow the director's compass even when their own reading of the movements says otherwise. *Obedience is always the right answer for the scrupulous.*

The normal shape of the interior life is this: more silence than speech, more fidelity than fervor, more duty than drama. The feelings will come and go. The faith remains. Keep the appointment.
