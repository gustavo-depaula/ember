## The App's Method — The Classical Four Parts

Give it thirty minutes when you can. Twenty if you must; never less than fifteen. Same time each day — early morning is traditional, before the day has a chance to crowd it out. Same place. A chair before a crucifix, an icon, an open Bible.

The app's guided practice follows the classical shape of mental prayer as St. Alphonsus Liguori distilled it — **Preparation, Reading, Meditation, Conclusion** — with the technical names the tradition has used for centuries. These are a circle, not a line: when the heart runs dry, return to the reading and begin again.

### Preparation — three acts

Before you speak, place yourself before the One to whom you speak.

1. **Act of Faith in God's Presence.** *"My God, I believe You are here, and I adore You with all my heart."*
2. **Where I am today.** Before you open the book, tell Him honestly what is in your heart right now — a joy, a weight, a worry. St. Josemaría: *"Prayer is talking with God."*
3. **Petition for Light.** Ask the Holy Spirit, and if you wish Our Lady and your patron saints, to enlighten this meditation.

### Reading

Open your text — today's Gospel, a psalm, a paragraph from *Divine Intimacy,* a line from a spiritual book. Read slowly. When a word catches you, stop there. *"Imitate the bees,"* Francis de Sales wrote, *"who linger on a flower while they find honey, and fly to another when it is gone."*

You are not studying. You are listening for a word addressed to you.

### Meditation — the three fruits, plus silence

This is the body of prayer. St. Alphonsus Liguori names its three fruits with plain technical precision — *affections, petitions, resolutions* — and the Carmelite tradition adds the silent rest that crowns them. Here everything happens as one continuous conversation.

1. **Considerations.** Let the intellect dwell briefly on what you read. What is He showing you? One or two honest questions — the mind here is only the door. *"Reasoning is not the goal of mental prayer,"* Teresa said, *"it is the door to it."*

2. **Affections.** Let the heart respond. Acts of love, adoration, thanksgiving, wonder, sorrow, contrition — whatever rises, speak it. *"My God, I love You above all things. My God, I thank You. My God, I am sorry."* Do not manufacture them; receive and return. *"The important thing is not to think much but to love much."* (Teresa)

3. **Petitions.** Ask Him for what you need — a virtue, a grace, the healing of a wound. Ask for those He has entrusted to you: the Church, the Pope, your family, those who suffer, the souls in purgatory. Be bold. He is a Father.

4. **Resolutions.** Take one resolution — not general, but particular. Not a list, but one small fidelity for today: a fault to resist, a virtue to practice. *"A single, specific, small resolution is worth more than many good intentions."* (Liguori)

5. **Silent Rest.** When words give out, rest. Let Him look at you, and look at Him in return. This silent gaze is the summit of the hour.

> **A circle, not a line.** If your heart goes dry, return to your phrase and begin again — consider, respond, ask, rest. Prayer is not a staircase; it is a walk around the Lord.

> **And in dryness:** *"If nothing comes today — no thought, no feeling — one cry suffices: 'My Jesus, mercy. My God, I love You.' That is already excellent prayer."* (Liguori)

### Conclusion — three acts, plus the bouquet

Liguori closes the meditation with three short acts before the vocal prayers.

1. **Thanksgiving.** Thank Him for the lights and affections He gave you, and ask pardon for distractions and tepidity. *"Lord, I thank You for the lights and graces You gave me in this meditation, and I ask pardon for the faults I committed in it."*

2. **Offering of Resolutions.** Offer your resolution with the firm will to keep it. *"My God, I offer You the resolutions I have taken with Your grace, and I am determined to carry them out, cost what it may."*

3. **Final Petition.** Ask the Father, through Jesus and Mary, for the grace to keep what you have resolved. *"My God, through the merits of Jesus Christ and the intercession of Mary Most Holy, give me the strength to faithfully put into practice the resolutions I have made."*

4. **Spiritual Bouquet.** Following St. Francis de Sales, choose one word, image, or phrase from the meditation to carry with you — a flower from the garden, to smell again in the margins of the afternoon.

Close with the three prayers the Church has given us — the **Our Father, Hail Mary,** and **Apostles' Creed** — entrusting all through Our Lady.
