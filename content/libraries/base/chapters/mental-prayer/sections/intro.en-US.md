St. Teresa of Ávila said it plainly: prayer *"is nothing else than an intimate sharing between friends; it means taking time frequently to be alone with Him who we know loves us"* (CCC 2709).

That is mental prayer. Not a technique. Not a performance. Not a duty pulled off the shelf. A conversation with someone who already loves you, and who has been waiting to hear what is actually on your heart.

St. Josemaría Escrivá puts it another way: *"You don't know how to pray? Put yourself in the presence of God, and as soon as you have said, 'Lord, I don't know how to pray!' you can be sure you've already begun."* That is the whole secret. The method is there to serve the conversation — never to replace it.

The centuries have given us many methods. The Ignatian school with its imagination and precise fruits. The Sulpician school with its adoration and communion. The Carmelites with their loving gaze. Francis de Sales with the patient bee. Liguori with his three simple acts and his cry of *"My Jesus, mercy!"* Each of them is a road into the same house. Each teaches the same friendship from a different door.

This chapter is an introduction to mental prayer itself — what it is, why it matters — and then a walk through those different schools, so that you can find the method (or the combination of methods) that best fits the weather of your soul. The app's guided practice, *Mental Prayer,* distills this into four simple movements you can keep for fifteen minutes a day.

For the deeper history of *lectio divina* — the monastic root from which all these methods grew — see the chapter *Lectio Divina: Praying with Scripture.*
