## Dryness: The Most Important Doctrine

You will keep the half-hour, and often — most days, for seasons of life — you will feel nothing. No consolation. No sweetness. The mind wanders. The heart is stone. You leave the chair thinking the time was wasted.

**This is not failure. This is mental prayer.**

John of the Cross called this *the dark night of sense*. The beginner is weaned off spiritual candy so that he can learn to love God for God, not for the pleasure God gives. Teresa said: *"The Lord does not look so much at the greatness of our works as at the love with which they are done."* And the purest love — the love of a soul that keeps showing up when nothing is felt — is the love that forms saints.

**Liguori's cry.** St. Alphonsus Liguori knew dryness as a pastor of ordinary souls. His counsel is the gentlest in the tradition: *"If our soul is in great aridity, it is enough for us to say: 'My God, help me. Lord, have pity on me. My Jesus, mercy!' Even though we do nothing more, our prayer is excellent."* Four words — *My Jesus, mercy* — repeated slowly in a dry half-hour are the prayer of a saint.

**Francis de Sales' patience.** Francis told the Visitandines: when you go to prayer and find the well dry, *stay.* Do not run off to find entertainment. *"Remain in the presence of God with as much interior attention as you can, and with humility. If you cannot converse with Him, at least stay near Him like a loyal courtier who, unable to speak with his king, is content to be seen by him."*

**Teresa's determined determination.** Teresa asks from the beginner one thing: *"a very determined determination"* (*una determinada determinación*) not to give up prayer, whatever happens. Dryness is the test of that determination. If you pass it, everything else passes with it.

**The rules in dryness:**

- **Do not shorten the time.** If anything, keep it more exactly. The enemy is never more active than in the soul's first real aridity, whispering that this is all a waste.
- **Do not keep inventing new methods.** The urge to "find what works" is usually disguised escape. Stay with a simple structure.
- **Do not force emotion.** Manufactured sentiment is worse than dryness. Just sit. Repeat the subject. Say a short prayer — *"Jesus, I trust in You." "My Jesus, mercy."*
- **Do read a spiritual master.** A paragraph of Teresa, John of the Cross, Francis de Sales, or *Divine Intimacy* before you begin. It primes the well.
- **Do bring dryness itself into the colloquy.** *"Lord, I feel nothing. I come anyway. I am yours."* That is already an act of perfect love.
- **Do go to confession more frequently.** Aridity can have a natural cause (tiredness, sin, distraction), and sometimes a good confession is what the soul needs.

St. Thérèse of Lisieux prayed through years of dryness. On her deathbed she said the state had not changed — yet her prayer had become an act of pure faith and love. That is what this whole method is, finally, for.
