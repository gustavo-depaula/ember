Every Mass, whether celebrated in Latin or Portuguese or the language of the Philippines, has the same four movements:

**1. Introductory Rites.** We gather. We confess our unworthiness (the *Confiteor* or *Penitential Act*). We glorify God (the *Gloria*, except in Advent and Lent). We ask, in the *Collect*, that the prayer of the whole assembly be gathered up into one.

**2. Liturgy of the Word.** We *listen*. The Old Testament, a Psalm, the New Testament, and the Gospel — the last we stand to hear, because the Lord Himself is speaking. Then the homily, the Creed (we confess what we just heard), and the Universal Prayer (the intentions of the Church).

**3. Liturgy of the Eucharist.** The offerings of bread and wine are brought to the altar — and with them, symbolically, our own daily work. The **Eucharistic Prayer** begins: the Preface, the *Sanctus*, the *epiclesis* (calling down the Spirit), the *words of institution* ("This is my Body… this is my Blood"), the anamnesis and intercessions, and the great *Doxology*. You answer "Amen" — and that Amen, St. Augustine said, is your signature on the whole sacrifice. Then the Our Father, the sign of peace, the *Agnus Dei*, and Holy Communion.

**4. Concluding Rites.** A blessing. A dismissal: *"Ite, missa est."* The word *Mass* itself comes from this dismissal — "Go, you are sent." The Mass is not a retreat from the world; it is the fuel for returning to it.

This four-part shape has been essentially the same since the second century. Justin Martyr, writing around AD 150, describes a service that a Catholic today would recognize without difficulty — readings, homily, prayers of the faithful, bread and wine offered, consecrated, distributed, carried to the absent. The liturgical languages have varied; the structure has not.
