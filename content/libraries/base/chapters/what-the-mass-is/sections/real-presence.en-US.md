When the priest, standing *in persona Christi*, pronounces over the bread: *"This is my Body…"* — the substance of the bread **becomes** the Body of Christ. The appearances remain (the whiteness, the taste of wheat, the round shape), but what that bread *is* has changed. The Church calls this **transubstantiation**: a change in the deepest reality of a thing while its sensible appearances remain.

This is not metaphor. The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) defined it; the Council of Trent reaffirmed it; the Catechism teaches it (§§1373–1381). The earliest Christians held it plainly — St. Ignatius of Antioch wrote in the year 107 that the Eucharist *"is the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins"* (*Letter to the Smyrnaeans* 7). This is not a late or Roman invention; it is the apostolic deposit.

Christ himself was blunt about it in John 6. When his listeners grumbled — *"This is a hard saying; who can hear it?"* — He did not soften. He let them walk away rather than correct a misunderstanding (*John 6:61–67*). He meant what He said.

**What does this mean for you at Mass?** When you kneel after the consecration, you are kneeling before the same Jesus who walked to Lazarus's tomb, who wept over Jerusalem, who was scourged and crucified and raised. He is there — Body, Blood, Soul, Divinity — under the appearance of the Host. When you receive Communion (in a state of grace, having fasted an hour, and having confessed any mortal sin), **He comes into you**. Union with God is not a distant reward. It is offered to you every Sunday morning.

This alone is enough to structure a life around.
