A last word, and an important one. The tradition's wisdom about dryness presumes a soul whose flatness is a matter of the interior life. But **the body is part of the soul's situation**, and many things that look like spiritual dryness are also, or instead, **bodily**.

If your "dryness" is accompanied by any of the following, please also see a doctor:

- Persistent fatigue that rest does not relieve.
- Difficulty sleeping, or sleeping far more than usual.
- Loss of interest in things other than prayer — food, conversation, work, family.
- A weight of sadness that persists across weeks and does not respond to the ordinary remedies.
- Thoughts of harm to yourself.

These are not symptoms of failure in prayer. They are symptoms that the body is asking for attention. Depression, thyroid disorders, hormonal shifts, chronic inflammation, burnout — all can produce a flattening that masquerades as spiritual dryness and resolves only with proper medical help. **Grace works through nature.** The Lord who made the body and the soul together does not expect you to heal one while neglecting the other. Caring for your body *is* part of the interior life.

This is not a footnote; it is the same pastoral instinct as the other sections. St. Thomas was clear that sleep, food, baths, and conversation with friends are legitimate remedies for interior heaviness. A body that has not slept for a month cannot pray well, and correcting the sleep is not a distraction from prayer — it is the correcting of the thing that is blocking prayer.

If, after medical care and enough sleep, the dryness remains and begins to feel more like the terrain the earlier sections described, then the spiritual work of this chapter is yours. If the dryness lifts when the body is cared for, thank God, and continue forward in the life of grace without guilt.

---

A closing word. Souls in the long dryness often feel abandoned. The felt abandonment is real to the one feeling it; the actual abandonment is not real. Christ's own cry on the cross — *"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* — is the First Word of the dry phase. It is a prayer of abandonment given *from within* the greatest act of love ever offered. The soul that can pray those words in dryness is praying *with* Christ, even when she cannot tell that she is.

Keep the appointment. Trim what must be trimmed. Seek the director. Read the Psalms. Pray Compline. Wait.

*"The night shall be my light in my pleasures."* (*Psalm 138:11*)
