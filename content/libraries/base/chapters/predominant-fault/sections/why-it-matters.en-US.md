Without a named predominant fault, the spiritual life becomes a hundred tiny skirmishes you cannot win — because you are fighting symptoms. You notice you were impatient this morning, gossiped at lunch, envied a friend at dinner — three confessable actions, each addressed in isolation, each recurring next week.

But in many souls, all three would flow from **one** underlying passion — say, a deep need to feel superior. Name that, and a new pattern appears: the impatience was at someone slower; the gossip was at someone ahead; the envy was that your friend got recognition you wanted. One fault. Three surfaces.

Fénelon, Scupoli, and the Carmelite tradition all insist: *strike the root, not the leaves.* The predominant fault is the root. Mortifying it — slowly, with grace — has more effect on the whole spiritual life than twenty parallel resolutions against its branches.

This is also why the saints spoke of "one virtue at a time." St. Francis de Sales counseled cultivating one *counter-virtue* against the predominant fault. If the fault is pride, work on hidden charity. If it is anger, work on gentleness. If it is sloth, work on order. The virtue is not abstract — it is aimed at the specific enemy in the specific soul.

*"An army that aims everywhere hits nothing."* (attributed to St. Thomas, paraphrased)
