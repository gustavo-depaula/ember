Before anything else, some quiet honesty about what Marian devotion is *not*, because most of the unease people bring to this subject is unease about things the Church does not actually do.

**Mary is not a fourth person of the Trinity.** She is not divine. She did not redeem anyone. She does not answer prayer by her own power. She is a creature — the holiest one God ever made, but still a creature, wholly dependent on her Son for everything, including her own sinlessness. Nothing a Catholic says to her or about her contradicts any of this, and whenever Catholic language seems to, the teaching of the Church quietly stands behind it to say otherwise.

**Prayers to Mary are not worship.** The tradition has been careful about this distinction for a very long time. St. Thomas Aquinas, in the *Summa* (II-II.103.4), uses three different words for three different postures of the soul: *latria* is the worship due to God alone, *dulia* is the honor due to the saints, and *hyperdulia* is the highest form of that honor, reserved for Mary because she is the Mother of God. Latria goes up; it does not go sideways. What Mary receives is a kind of honor — the sort a healthy family gives its mother — not an adoration that competes with God. When a Catholic bows before a statue of Mary, he is not bowing to a god. He is bowing the way a son bows in his mother's house.

It helps to know what the word **pray** used to mean. In older English it meant simply *to ask*: *"I pray thee, sir, help me."* Courts still use it this way: a legal petition "prays for relief." When Catholics say they *pray to* Mary, they mean they *ask* Mary — to pray for them, to intercede for them, to carry their need to her Son. Asking a sister in heaven to pray for you is no stranger than asking a sister on earth to pray for you. It is only stranger if you have decided that death cuts the communion of the Church in half, and the creed says it does not.

One more pastoral thing, which is more important than any of the above: **you are not required to produce feelings of devotion**. Many good converts, and many good cradle Catholics, arrive at this subject convinced that if they do not feel warmth toward Mary immediately, they are failing. They are not failing. They are only beginning. Start with a fact that is easier to hold than a feeling: *Jesus loves his mother*. You can stand on that alone for years. Love for her will grow in you at his speed, not yours, and usually sideways, while you are looking at him.

*"Behold, from henceforth all generations shall call me blessed."* (*Luke 1:48*)
