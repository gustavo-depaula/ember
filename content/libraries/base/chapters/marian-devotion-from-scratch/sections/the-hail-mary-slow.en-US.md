The best place to begin is one Hail Mary, prayed slowly, by a soul who has never actually meant one. Not fifty. Not a decade. One.

Look at what you are saying. The prayer is made of three pieces, and two of them are not the Church's composition — they are the Gospel.

The first piece is the archangel Gabriel's greeting at the Annunciation: *"Hail, full of grace, the Lord is with thee."* That sentence was not written by a medieval monk. It was spoken to a young woman in Nazareth by a messenger of heaven, in a moment that would decide the history of the world. When you say those words, you are saying them *after* Gabriel; you are taking the first words ever spoken to Mary by heaven, and making them your own.

The second piece is Elizabeth's prophecy, six months later, in the hill country, when her child leaps in the womb and the Holy Spirit lifts her voice: *"Blessed art thou among women, and blessed is the fruit of thy womb."* This is Scripture. The addition of the name Jesus makes explicit what Elizabeth already meant. So the first half of the Hail Mary is a small anthology: the angel, the prophetess, and the Name. You are echoing the Gospel.

The third piece is the Church's part — short, and entirely practical: *"Holy Mary, Mother of God, pray for us sinners, now and at the hour of our death."* This is the ask. After all the praise, a request; after all the Gospel, a need. She is asked to pray *for us*, at the two times we most need it: *now*, and *at the hour of our death*. The two hours no one gets to skip.

Try this once, tonight. Say the whole Hail Mary, aloud if you can, slower than feels natural. Leave a breath between the clauses. Notice which phrase catches in your throat. For one person it is "full of grace"; for another it is "mother of God"; for another it is "pray for us sinners" because he has not prayed for himself in years; for another it is "at the hour of our death" because he has been avoiding that hour with all his strength. Wherever the prayer caught, that is where the work is. Mark the spot, and leave it for today.

You have just prayed a Hail Mary. Not a hundred. One. This is how devotion is actually built — one honest Hail Mary at a time, not one hundred unmeant ones. The soul that prays one Hail Mary with attention tonight has done more than the soul that rattles off the full Rosary as a task. Begin here. There is no hurry. She has waited patiently for you much longer than you have waited for her.

*"Hail, full of grace, the Lord is with thee."* (*Luke 1:28*)
