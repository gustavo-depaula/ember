Somewhere between month two and month five, the honeymoon ends. It does this quietly, without a single dramatic event, and usually in a cluster of small disappointments that arrive close enough together to feel like one large one.

The **parish you came into is not the parish of your imagination**. The hymns are dated and badly chosen. The cantor is off key. The homily, which last Easter seemed to be spoken directly to you, now strikes you as flat, or political, or simply tired. The parishioner you admired in RCIA — the one whose questions were sharper than yours, whose notes were neater — has begun to miss Mass, and when you asked him about it he shrugged. The building is smaller than you remembered it. The coffee after Mass is bad.

The **cradle Catholics around you do not seem to believe what you just risked everything to believe**. The cousin who served as your sponsor does not know the names of the four marks of the Church. The colleague who encouraged you to cross the Tiber has not been to confession in a decade. People speak casually of missing Sunday Mass for a football game. You came in expecting to meet a body of witnesses; you appear to have walked into a room half full of sleeping ones.

The **spiritual reading that set you on fire a year ago now bores you**. Those passages of Augustine that you underlined with three colors of pen — you can still see that they are true, but they no longer burn. The podcast that converted you now seems repetitive. The new book is thinner than you hoped.

And then, underneath all of this, the worst one: **you have started to notice that you yourself have not changed as much as you hoped**. The temper is still there. The old sin came back last Thursday. The prayer is drier than it was in Lent. You suspect, for the first time, that you may have over-sold your own conversion to yourself.

None of this, taken together, is a failure of your conversion. It is the ordinary condition of the faith as it enters the ordinary conditions of a life. The saints describe this exact passage as routine — John of the Cross calls a version of it the "night of sense," and notes that souls who do not know it is normal often mistake it for having lost God, when in fact God has simply stopped entertaining them and begun forming them. The warmth of the beginning is real and is also pedagogical; it is the milk given to infants so they will grow. It is not meant to last. What replaces it — a plainer, steadier, less emotional adherence — is not a step back. It is the faith becoming adult in you.

Do not interpret the flatness as evidence. Interpret it as weather.
