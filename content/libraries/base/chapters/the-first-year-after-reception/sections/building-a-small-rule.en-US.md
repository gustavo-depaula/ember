Here is the practical problem no one in the program warned you about: after the Easter Vigil, the scaffold disappears. For most of a year you had a weekly meeting, a reading schedule, a sponsor, a catechist, and the quiet pressure of a group walking in the same direction. On Monday after the Vigil, all of that is gone. Nobody calls. Nobody sends a reading. You are alone with a Catholic life and no frame around it.

The convert's **most common first-year failure** begins in exactly this vacuum and takes exactly this shape. Told to "build a rule of life," and freshly in love with the riches of the tradition, the new Catholic attempts everything at once: daily Rosary, daily Mass, the full Liturgy of the Hours, spiritual reading morning and night, a weekly holy hour, meat fasting on all Fridays plus Wednesdays, a First Friday devotion, a First Saturday devotion, a consecration to Our Lady beginning next month, and a novena recommended by a friend. For two, perhaps six, perhaps ten weeks, it all holds. Then one ordinary Tuesday the whole structure gives way at once. A second chapter in this library — *Pacing* — describes that collapse in detail and is worth reading beside this one.

The response is not to try harder next time. The response is to build, from the very beginning, a rule so small that a sick week does not break it.

For the convert in the first year, the recommended rule is this, and only this:

- **Sunday Mass, every Sunday.** Non-negotiable, the way eating is non-negotiable. If you miss through grave fault, confess it before the next Sunday and go.
- **One Friday penance each week.** The simplest and most traditional is abstinence from meat. If your state of life makes meat awkward, pick a single other sacrifice and keep it on Fridays without changing it. The point is not severity; the point is that Friday is different.
- **Confession every two months.** On a fixed schedule, not by mood. Mark it on a calendar like a dentist appointment.
- **Daily: one Our Father, one Hail Mary, one Glory Be.** Three minutes. On waking, or before sleep, or both. Said slowly, not rushed.

That is the whole rule. Nothing else. No Rosary yet; no daily Mass yet; no Liturgy of the Hours yet; no Carmelite meditation yet; no consecration yet. All of those are good. None of them belong in the first year.

If you keep this rule for twelve months without adding to it, you will have done something unusual: you will have finished a first year. You will also have acquired, without noticing, a spine — Sunday Mass anchored, a Friday rhythm, a confessor who knows your name, and a daily prayer that reaches you on every kind of day. A second thing can be added in year two, from a position of strength. Adding now, from a position of enthusiasm, is how first-year Catholics quietly stop being Catholics by the third year.

If anything here feels too small for the fervor you still have: that is the correct feeling. That is what you are supposed to feel. Hold the fervor; do not spend it.
