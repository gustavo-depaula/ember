A short catalogue, so you are not surprised by yourself.

You will probably be **distracted**. You will think about your phone, your work, the argument from yesterday, what to eat after. The Mass will not fix this in the first hour. Do not grade yourself by your attention span; the faith of the Church is praying through you even while your mind wanders.

You may be **bored**. The homily may be mediocre. The music may be bad. The parish may be smaller and plainer than your memory made it. None of this counts against what is happening on the altar.

You may feel **tears you did not expect** during a simple moment — the *Kyrie*, an ordinary hymn, the small bell at the consecration. Let them fall and do not analyze them. Or you may feel nothing at all, and go home flat, and that is also normal. God is not withholding; he is simply not in a hurry to convince you of anything by feeling.

You may feel **anger** — at the homily, at a phrase you remember disagreeing with, at yourself for being here, at the Church for something done years ago that still hurts. Anger at Mass is not scandalous. It is often the first sign that something real is being touched. Bring it to the altar; do not pretend it away.

You may feel a **sudden flash of peace** — and then at once the suspicion that you made it up. You did not make it up, but do not build a house on it either. One felt moment does not settle anything.

You may feel like **a stranger**. You are not. You were baptized; this is your house. It may not feel like your house for a while, and that is all right. Houses take time to recognize.

You may feel **guilty about not receiving** if you go forward for a blessing, or if you stay in your pew. Good. That guilt is honest; hold it lightly for a few minutes and read the next section. Not receiving today is not a failure; it is often the most reverent thing a returning soul can do.

You may feel, for a single unannounced instant, a **certainty that you are home**. If it comes, receive it quietly. If it does not, do not look for it.

And the deepest warning the saints give about all of this: **do not read any one feeling as the whole message**. A morning of tears is not automatic conversion; a morning of dryness is not rejection; a morning of rage is not apostasy. The Mass is larger than the feeling you have at it.

Flannery O'Connor, over dinner when a literary woman praised the Eucharist as a "symbol," answered in the sentence that has become a watchword: *"Well, if it's a symbol, to hell with it."* If it is really Christ on the altar — and it is — then what you feel about it on one Sunday is not the measure. He is, whether your senses catch it or not.

*"My flesh is meat indeed: and my blood is drink indeed."* (*John 6:56*)
