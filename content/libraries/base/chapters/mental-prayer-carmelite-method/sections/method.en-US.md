## The Method

Give it thirty minutes. Twenty if you must; never less than fifteen. Same time each day — early morning is traditional, before the day has a chance to crowd it out. Same place. A chair before a crucifix or an icon.

**1. Place yourself in the presence of God.** Before words, a moment of recognition. "You are here. You see me. You love me." Calm the body. Close the eyes or lower them. A single sign of the cross, slowly. Teresa warned against rushing past this step — half the fruit of meditation is lost when the soul begins speaking before it remembers *to whom* it speaks.

**2. Take a subject.** A scene from the Gospels is the Carmelite's classical starting point — *"I want my Lord Himself as my book,"* Teresa said. A paragraph from *Divine Intimacy* or the Gospel of the day. One paragraph is enough. The point is not to cover material.

**3. Consider — but briefly.** Read. Pause. What is the scene? What is God doing here? What is He revealing about Himself, about me? This is the intellect's work — five minutes, perhaps ten. Do not prolong it. Teresa's rule: *reasoning is not the goal of mental prayer, it is the door to it.*

**4. Move to affection.** This is the heart of the method — and what distinguishes Carmelite prayer from study. When a truth strikes, stop. Do not *analyze* the strike; *respond* to it. Wonder, gratitude, sorrow, desire, adoration, petition — whatever the Spirit stirs, rest in it. Let the affection lengthen. *"It is not much knowledge that nourishes the soul, but feeling and tasting things interiorly,"* Ignatius wrote, and Teresa agreed. Fifteen minutes of loving God in one line is prayer at its height.

**5. Close with a colloquy.** Speak directly to the Lord about what you have seen and felt. (The next section treats this in detail.)

**6. End with a resolution and thanksgiving.** One concrete thing the prayer asks of your day — *"Today I will speak less sharply," "Today I will keep one minute of silence before every meal."* A brief thanksgiving. The Sign of the Cross.
