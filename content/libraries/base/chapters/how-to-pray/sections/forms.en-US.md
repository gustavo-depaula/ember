## Three Forms

The tradition distinguishes three forms of prayer. They are not ranks — a saint uses all three — but they describe where the emphasis falls.

**Vocal prayer.** Words, spoken or thought: the Our Father, the Rosary, the Psalms. Christ himself taught us to pray this way. Vocal prayer engages the body and anchors distracted minds; it is also the common language of the Church at worship. Never outgrow it. *"When two or three gather in my name, there am I in the midst of them"* (Mt 18:20) is fulfilled above all in liturgy and shared vocal prayer.

**Meditative prayer** (*mental prayer*). You take a scene from Scripture, a mystery, a truth — and turn it over in the heart. You ask: what is God saying here? what does He ask of me? St. Teresa of Ávila called this "nothing else than an intimate sharing between friends; it means taking time frequently to be alone with him who we know loves us" (CCC 2709). Fifteen minutes daily, in quiet, is the traditional starting point.

**Contemplative prayer.** When meditation simplifies into a gaze — when words fall away and one is simply *with* God — that is contemplation. It is not an achievement but a gift. The traditional advice: keep showing up for meditation, and if contemplation is given, receive it quietly, without grasping. *"I look at him, and he looks at me"*, said the peasant of Ars to St. John Vianney.
