## The Battles of Prayer

The *Catechism* calls prayer a battle (CCC 2725). Three enemies keep returning.

**Distraction.** The mind wanders. This is not sin, only the human condition. St. Teresa taught: do not fight distractions by fighting them — return gently, again and again, to the subject of prayer. Every return is itself an act of love.

**Dryness.** You feel nothing. This is the normal middle of any serious prayer life — "the dark night" in its ordinary form. The traditional advice: stay at the appointed time, cut nothing, expect nothing, trust that God is working precisely *because* the consolation is withdrawn. Consolations feed the beginner; dryness forms the son.

**Acedia.** The noonday devil — a sourness toward the things of God, a conviction that prayer is useless, that some other thing is more urgent. Name it. Do not argue with it. Keep the appointment anyway.

*"Pray without ceasing"* (1 Thess 5:17) is not a command to feel constantly prayerful. It is a command to live with one's face turned, so that even in the middle of distraction, dryness, and accusation, a deeper layer of the soul keeps saying yes.
