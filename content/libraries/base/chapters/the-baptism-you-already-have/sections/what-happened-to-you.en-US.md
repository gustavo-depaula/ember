You may not know what to call yourself. You were baptized as a baby — a church you do not remember, a priest whose name you never learned, a white garment in a photograph somewhere. Maybe you made First Communion because the school required it. Maybe you were confirmed, maybe not. Since then, nothing. You are not sure whether you are really Catholic, and no one has told you plainly what happened on that day.

Something objective happened. Not a ceremony for the family. Not a folk custom. At your baptism, water was poured on your head while a minister said, *"I baptize you in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."* At that moment — regardless of who was watching, regardless of what your parents believed, regardless of whether the room was reverent or distracted — you were joined to the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ. St. Paul puts it flatly: *"we are buried together with him by baptism into death; that as Christ is risen from the dead… so we also may walk in newness of life"* (Romans 6:4). You were buried with him. You rose with him. This is not a metaphor the Church uses to dress up a ritual; it is what the ritual accomplished.

And something was *put in you*. The Fathers had words for it that sound large because the reality is large. St. Gregory of Nazianzus called baptism **"God's most beautiful and magnificent gift"** — and then he listed the names the early Church gave it: *gift, grace, anointing, illumination, the clothing of immortality, the bath of rebirth, the seal*. Tertullian, a century earlier, had already written of the Christian as **"a little fish born in the water."** These are not literary flourishes. They are descriptions of an event.

A character was sealed on your soul — a permanent mark, invisible, that configures you to Christ the priest, prophet, and king. You became an adopted child of God. You were incorporated into the Body of Christ, which is the Church. Original sin was taken away. The life of grace — what the Church calls sanctifying grace — was poured into you.

Your parents may have done it as custom. Your godparents may have forgotten the promises they made for you. The priest may have been tired. None of that touched the action of God, because the action of God does not depend on the people in the room. It depends on Christ, who keeps his promises.

*"Unless a man be born again of water and the Holy Ghost, he cannot enter into the kingdom of God."* (John 3:5)
