The first thing to settle: you cannot be baptized again. The Church does not permit it. This is not bureaucracy; it is doctrine, and the doctrine is good news for you.

When the water was poured and the words were said, **a permanent mark was sealed on your soul** — what the Church calls a *character*, a word the New Testament uses for the impression a signet ring leaves in wax. St. Thomas Aquinas describes this character as a spiritual imprint that configures the soul to Christ and cannot be lost, not by sin, not by apostasy, not by decades of forgetting, not by death (*Summa Theologiae* III.63.5; III.66.9). That is why the Catechism states plainly that **baptism "cannot be repeated"** (CCC 1272). You were marked once, for always.

Protestants of certain traditions, meeting an adult who seems to have no real relationship with Christ, will sometimes urge a second baptism, an adult one, so it will "count this time." The Catholic Church refuses. Not because she is rigid, but because she knows what happened to you the first time. The first one counted. A second one would not be a better baptism; it would be a pretended baptism layered on top of a real one — and sacraments are not pretended.

The one exception clarifies the rule. If there is a genuine, documented doubt that a baptism ever took place — no certificate, no witnesses, and no way to verify — a priest may perform what is called a *conditional baptism*, using the words *"If you are not already baptized, I baptize you…"* This happens once, under the bishop's guidance, and resolves the doubt. It is not a repeat. It is the Church's way of making sure the sacrament is never presumed and never multiplied.

For most readers this is simply reassurance: **the hardest thing is already behind you.** The irreversible beginning, the moment you could not have done for yourself, the doorway into the new creation — that was handled when you were too small to help or hinder. The rest of your life is the slow, gradual, very reachable work of living what was already given.

This is why the Church never tells a returning soul *"start over"*. She tells them *"come home"*. There is no starting over, because there is no erasing of that mark. There is only the return of the one who bears it.

*"There is one Lord, one faith, one baptism."* (Ephesians 4:5)
