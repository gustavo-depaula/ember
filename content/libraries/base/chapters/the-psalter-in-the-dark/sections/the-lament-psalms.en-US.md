The Psalter already contains your worst day. This is not a figure of speech. Before you were born, before the apostles, before the exile and the temple, **someone in Israel had already written the prayer you cannot now write**, and the Church kept it for you. What follows is a short map. Sit with one of these when it matches the weather of the soul.

**Psalm 6 — for overwhelming weariness.** *"I am weary with my groaning."* The psalm of the body and spirit at the end of themselves. The night-prayer of the exhausted.

**Psalm 13 — for God's apparent absence.** *"How long, O Lord, wilt thou forget me unto the end?"* Four times the psalmist says *how long*. The prayer for the soul that no longer feels God is there.

**Psalm 22 — for abandonment.** *"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* The Lord's own psalm on the cross. The prayer the Church lets you share when nothing else fits.

**Psalms 42 and 43 — for spiritual thirst and depression.** *"As the hart panteth after the fountains of water."* A dialogue of the soul with itself in a season of inner heaviness, and a refrain: *hope in God*.

**Psalm 51 — for sin-shame.** *"Have mercy on me, O God, according to thy great mercy."* David's prayer after his fall. The Church has prayed this daily for two thousand years in Lauds and in confession.

**Psalm 88 — for the pit that does not open.** *"I am counted among them that go down to the pit."* Almost uniquely in the Psalter, this one does not end in consolation. For the nights that do not resolve.

**Psalm 130 (*De Profundis*) — for the cry from the depths.** *"Out of the depths I have cried to thee, O Lord."* The traditional prayer for the dead, and for every soul that finds itself at the bottom of a well.

Notice what these psalms do not require of you. They do not require you to feel consoled. They do not require you to produce fine images. They do not require you to frame things tidily. They let you say *how long* and *why* and *out of the depths* and *have mercy* — and they put those words in a form the saints have already prayed. When you pray Psalm 88, Augustine prayed it with you, and Teresa, and the Curé of Ars, and every carthusian of the last thousand years. **You are not alone in that psalm**, even if everything in you feels alone.

Do not worry if you cannot make the whole psalm yours on a given day. One line is enough. Return to it; let it stay on the lips; let it do what it will.

*"Out of the depths I have cried to thee, O Lord: Lord, hear my voice."* (*Psalm 129:1–2*)
