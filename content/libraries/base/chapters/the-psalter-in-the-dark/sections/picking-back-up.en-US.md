What begins as an emergency measure should, over time, become an ordinary companion. The Psalter is not meant only for the worst hours. It is meant for the ordinary evenings, the commute, the half hour before sleep, the minute waiting for coffee. A few practical ways to make it a steady presence.

**Keep a Bible open to the Psalms.** Literally — on the nightstand, on the desk, on the kitchen counter. A closed Bible is easier to ignore than one already turned to Psalm 34. The physical openness of the book is a small help, and small helps are most of the interior life.

**Or a Breviary, or the Liturgy of the Hours.** The four-volume set is what parish priests and religious pray. The one-volume *Christian Prayer* is the abridged version. Either binds you to what the whole Church is praying that day, and on dry days that is a great grace — you are no longer praying alone with a book; you are praying *with the Church*, in her rhythm, whether or not you can feel it.

**Or simply the 30-day psalter cycle.** The old English Prayer Book pattern walks through all 150 psalms in one month, morning and evening, and returns to the beginning on the first of the next month. Ember's office is built on this cycle; the app will open to the psalms of the day if you want it to do the choosing for you. There is a quiet dignity to living a month in the full Psalter, praying both what fits your soul and what does not fit it at all — because the not-fitting teaches, too.

**When you do not know where to start, start at Psalm 1.** Read one or two a day, in order. Do not skip the difficult ones. Do not skip the imprecatory ones. (The Fathers have much to say about those, and it is worth reading them; for now, trust that the Church has prayed them for twenty centuries, and pray them as a soul interceding against one's own sins and the evils of the age rather than against a personal enemy.) In a hundred and fifty days — a little less than half a year — you will have walked the whole Psalter, and you will find that every season of the soul has been described along the way.

St. Athanasius, writing to a friend named Marcellinus about how to pray the Psalms, noticed something that anyone who prays them for a year eventually notices too. In most of the books of Scripture, he said, you read about what the patriarchs and prophets did, and you learn about God from them. But in the Psalms, you do not only read *about* the speaker. **You find yourself speaking his words as your own.** The psalm you open in the morning is already, somehow, the prayer you would have said if you knew how to say it.

That is the gift the Church has been keeping for you. Take it down from the shelf. Open it. Pray.
