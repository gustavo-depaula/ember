## The Monastic Art of Praying Scripture

*Lectio divina* — literally "divine reading" — is the ancient Christian practice of prayerfully reading Scripture so that the Word of God is not merely studied but allowed to speak, question, and form the soul.

It is older than its name. The Fathers read this way. The Desert monks read this way. But around 1150, a Carthusian prior named **Guigo II** put the practice into a clear map in a short treatise called *The Ladder of Monks*. He described four rungs — *lectio, meditatio, oratio, contemplatio* — *reading, meditation, prayer, contemplation* — rising from the page to God.

*"Reading seeks, meditation finds, prayer asks, contemplation tastes."* — **Guigo II**

Vatican II and *Dei Verbum* (§25) urged all the faithful to take up this practice: *"Let them remember that prayer should accompany the reading of Sacred Scripture, so that a dialogue takes place between God and man."* Benedict XVI in *Verbum Domini* (§87) called it *"capable of opening up to the faithful the treasures of God's word."*

You are not studying. You are listening. Scripture is not an object under your examination — it is a voice under which you stand.
