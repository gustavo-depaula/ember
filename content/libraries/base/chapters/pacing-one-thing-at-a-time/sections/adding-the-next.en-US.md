The year is over. You have kept Sunday Mass, or the evening decade, or the morning offering. Not perfectly — nobody keeps anything perfectly — but you kept *coming back*, and coming back became what you do, and somewhere between month seven and month ten you stopped thinking of the practice as a project and started thinking of it as part of who you are. That is the *habitus*. That is what a year buys you. Now you may add.

**One more.** Not three. Not the full plan you sketched twelve months ago and quietly shelved. One. Small. Chosen, if at all possible, in conversation with a priest or a spiritual director, not from a book you just finished reading at three in the morning. The book will always have seven recommendations; the director or pastor knows the shape of your actual life and will help you pick the one that fits next to what you already have.

Pick it alongside the first, not instead of. The first practice has become the load-bearing wall of your interior life; do not disturb it. The new one is a second wall, placed carefully, so that together they begin to make a room. If Sunday Mass was the year, weekly confession (say every three or four weeks) might be the next. If the evening decade was the year, ten minutes of mental prayer in the morning might be next. If the morning offering was the year, a nightly examen might be next. Small additions, each living at a different hour or a different day from the first, so they do not compete.

Then an honest warning about what happens next.

**Expect the second practice to destabilize the first for a month or two.** This is normal. The soul was balanced around one rhythm; adding the second throws the balance off briefly while a new balance is found. The evening decade that had become automatic will suddenly feel clumsy when mental prayer is added in the morning; the morning offering that was effortless will feel forced when the examen joins at night. Do not drop either. Hold both, unimpressively, through the awkward season. By the end of month two or three, they settle, and you now have a rule of life with two things in it, both kept, both forming.

And then — only then, after the second is solid — a third. After a year or two. And later a fourth.

**This is how the devout life is actually built.** Not by climbing a mountain but by laying a stone per season. In ten or fifteen years of this, without a single heroic month, a soul will find that it prays the Office, goes to confession regularly, keeps mental prayer, fasts on the ember days, and reads the Fathers — not because it ever resolved to do all of that at once, but because each of those practices was added in its hour to a life already able to hold it. From the outside it will look like a transformed life; from the inside it will feel like the same soul, simply the soul after a long time of small fidelities.

St. Francis de Sales, in the last lines of *Introduction to the Devout Life* I.3, says this quietly: the devout life is not one shape but many, *"according to the strength, the employment, and the duty of each particular soul."* The shape is yours. The pace is yours. The one thing is yours. Keep it, and the Lord will build the rest.

*"He that is faithful in that which is least, is faithful also in that which is greater."* (*Luke 16:10*)
