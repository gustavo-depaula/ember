So: one thing. Which one?

That depends on where your soul is actually standing right now, not where you wish it stood. The tradition is concrete about this; the saints who wrote for laymen — de Sales, Scupoli, Josemaría — always started by asking the obvious question that pious enthusiasm skips: *what is the first thing that is not yet in place?*

A rough taxonomy, not as a prescription but as a map.

**If you are a returning Catholic — away for years, just back —** your one thing is almost certainly **Sunday Mass, every Sunday, without exception**. Not daily Mass. Not a new devotion. Sunday Mass. If confession is needed to get you there, confession is part of the one thing. Everything else can wait a year. A Catholic who keeps Sunday for a year has rebuilt the spine of a Catholic life and is ready for a second thing. A Catholic who has not kept Sunday cannot hold a second thing, no matter how beautiful it is.

**If you are a practicing Catholic newly serious about the interior life —** your one thing is probably one of three: **a decade of the Rosary every evening**, or **ten minutes of mental prayer daily**, or **monthly confession on a fixed day** (not "when I feel like it"). Choose one. Not the one that most impresses the part of you that reads spiritual books; the one you will actually do on the worst Wednesday of November.

**If you are a busy parent of young children —** your one thing may be even smaller. The **morning offering, said before feet touch the floor**, and *nothing else added to the rule for a year*. Or a **nightly examen of two minutes** while the baby nurses. These sound like almost nothing. They are almost nothing. They are also the beginning of a soul that prays, and in ten years of that almost-nothing you will have raised children inside a house of prayer without ever having had the time to "do" prayer the way the books describe.

**If you are single, young, and have time and energy —** the temptation is to take this as permission to stack four practices. Resist. Pick one. Make it something that will still be livable the week you are sick, the week you travel, the week you are dumped. A young soul with time who commits to one thing for a year acquires something most of her married friends will envy a decade from now: a *habitus*. A young soul who committed to six things and kept none of them for more than three weeks acquires a low-grade shame about the faith that can last until her forties.

The criterion across every state of life is the same, and it is St. Teresa of Ávila's, who warned her nuns against making vows in the heat of fervor. Ask of the candidate practice one question:

**"Can I do this on a sick day?"**

If the answer is no, the practice is too big. Not wrong — too big for *now*. Make it smaller until the answer is yes. A decade of the Rosary instead of the full five. Five minutes of mental prayer instead of thirty. Confession every six weeks instead of every week. The shrunk version is not a defeat; it is the version the soul can actually hold, and the version the soul can hold is the version that forms her.

*"Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might."* (*Ecclesiastes 9:10*)
