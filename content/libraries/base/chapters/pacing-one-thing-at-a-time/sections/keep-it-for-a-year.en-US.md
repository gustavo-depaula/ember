A year, not a month. The length is not arbitrary, and it is not a suggestion.

A month of a practice teaches the soul what the practice feels like when the soul is still fresh on it — which is to say, it teaches almost nothing real. A month is still fervor. A month is still novelty. A month is the honeymoon of a practice, and what a practice has to teach the soul is not available during its honeymoon. The soul must outlast the fervor to meet the practice as it actually is.

Somewhere around the sixth week the fervor breaks. The Rosary that felt luminous in the first days becomes a mouthful of beads said dutifully before bed; the ten minutes of mental prayer that were "the best part of the day" become ten minutes of fighting the grocery list. This is not a sign that you picked the wrong practice. This is the sign that you picked the right one and that it has started its real work, which is to teach the soul how to be faithful when fervor has left. St. John of the Cross called this passage the first of the dark nights, *la noche de los sentidos* — the night of the senses, in which God withdraws the felt sweetness so that the soul may learn to seek Him rather than the sweetness. Almost every serious Catholic who abandons a practice abandons it here, at exactly the moment it was about to begin to form her.

Keep going. This is what Teresa of Ávila meant when she told her nuns that the devout life is less like building a house in a day and more like digging a well over seasons. A house raised fast is raised shallow; a well dug in a week is dry in a month. You are not constructing a project. You are opening a shaft in the soul, and the shaft is opened by the same small motion repeated through every kind of weather — the fervent morning, the bored Wednesday, the flu, the grief, the travel, the insomnia, the three-a.m. feeding.

Two rules for the year.

**A fall does not end the year.** You will miss a day. You will miss a week. Something will happen — a death, a breakdown, a stretch of exhaustion, a simple bad mood that lasts eleven days — and you will look up and realize you have not prayed the decade for a long time. This is ordinary. It is not a sign you are starting over. Pick up the practice at the next opportunity and go on. The tradition is firm on this: the devout life is not broken by the fall; it is broken by the *reaction* to the fall. Resume quietly.

**Starting over after a fall does not reset the year.** This is the same rule, said from the other side. A soul that counts days like a sobriety chip is a soul that will eventually miss day 87 and be crushed and quit. The year is not a chain of unbroken days; it is a season of fidelity. At the end of the twelve months, look back honestly — did you keep *appointing* yourself to the practice, even when you missed it? Did you come back? If yes, the year has done its work, regardless of how many actual days were clean. You are ready for the next thing.

A monk's prayer, useful here: *"Lord, I am not faithful. But I am not stopping."*

*"He that shall persevere to the end, he shall be saved."* (*Matthew 24:13*)
