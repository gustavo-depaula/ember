Near the end of his life, in December of 1273, St. Thomas Aquinas — the greatest systematic theologian the Church has produced — stopped writing. His *Summa Theologiae* was not finished; it would be completed after his death from his earlier work. Pressed by his secretary, Reginald, who begged him to resume, Thomas answered quietly: *"I can do no more. Such things have been revealed to me that all I have written seems to me so much straw."* He said no more, and he did not write again.

There is a category of soul in the Church that Thomas, at the end, had entered: the soul that *knows the faith well and has only just begun to pray it*. This chapter is for the soul that has not yet gotten there — the soul that knows a great deal *about* God and has not yet learned to sit in front of Him in silence.

Every age of the Church has produced these souls in its own shape. Ours produces them by the thousand. The modern Catholic who returns to the faith through apologetics, podcast catechesis, the good YouTube channels and the great online commentaries, can end up — often quite quickly — with a confident grasp of the Creed, a working command of the Catechism, firm positions on the Eucharist, Marian dogma, the four marks, the authority of the Magisterium — and almost no interior life. He can answer the Protestant neighbor in five minutes; he can scarcely sit still for five minutes with the Lord.

The tradition calls this the difference between **scientia** and **sapientia** — knowledge and wisdom. Scientia is the grasping of truths; sapientia is the tasting of them. A soul can have vast *scientia* about God and little *sapientia* of Him; this is not a failure of intelligence but a stage of growth. The mistake is to stop at the first and mistake it for the second. The seminaries used to warn students that a man can study theology for ten years and not know how to make a proper holy hour. They still say it, but fewer listen.

This is not an argument against study. *Fides quaerens intellectum*, faith seeking understanding, is a permanent vocation of the Christian mind, and the Church depends on souls who love doctrine rightly. The argument is narrower: **the study is not the prayer**, and a soul that has only studied has not yet begun the journey the study was supposed to prepare. St. Bernard's warning is good here: *"There are those who seek knowledge for the sake of knowledge — that is curiosity. There are those who seek knowledge to be known — that is vanity. There are those who seek knowledge to sell it — that is lucre. But there are those who seek knowledge to edify — that is love. And those who seek knowledge to be edified — that is prudence."*

If you recognize yourself — if you have more certainty about the faith than peace in prayer, more articles read than minutes kept in silence — this chapter is for you. Not to take away what you have, but to add what you lack.

*"Knowledge puffeth up; but charity edifieth."* (*1 Corinthians 8:1*)
