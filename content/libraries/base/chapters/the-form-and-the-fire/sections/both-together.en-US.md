The reconciliation, when it comes, is simpler than the soul expects. The tradition has never asked anyone to choose between the old rite and charity. It has always taught both, and the saints who loved the old rite most were almost always the saints who loved the neighbor most. This is not an accident of biography; it is the logic of the thing.

St. Francis de Sales was, by the standards of his age, a rigorous traditionalist. He catechized in a diocese torn by the Reformation; he defended Catholic doctrine against Calvinist critics with patience and precision; he loved the liturgy and would have been scandalized by half of what is permitted in liturgy now. And he was also the bishop who, after writing his catechism tracts by the thousand and pushing them under doors in the Chablais in winter, wept in his study over the souls who had not yet received them. His rigor was indistinguishable from his tenderness. They were the same act from two angles.

John Henry Newman loved the ancient liturgy. As an Anglican he knew it; as a Catholic he received it; as a priest he said it; as a cardinal he protected it. He also spent himself, for decade after decade, on the particular people in front of him — students at Oxford, poor converts in Birmingham, the children of the parish, the letters that came to his desk and were answered by hand, one by one, until he was old. He is remembered as a giant of doctrine and also as the pastor who stopped to know the names of everyone who came through the oratory door. The two were one vocation in him.

The saints combine what we are tempted to split: **precise love of form and wide love of people**. The precision disciplines the love; the love ensouls the precision. A traditionalist who is only precise has not yet met the tradition he claims. A Catholic who is only warm but indifferent to the form has not yet received what the tradition has to give. The two are not rivals; they are the two sides of a single fidelity.

So, practically. This week, bring into your rule of life one traditional devotion and one corporal work of mercy. Go to a sung Vespers, or pray a full rosary on your knees, or fast on Ember Days, or read a few pages of the old *Imitation*. And then, the same week: visit someone who is sick, bring a meal to a family with a new baby, write to a prisoner, pay for a stranger's groceries, sit with someone whose grief has gone on too long to still be fashionable. Let the two disciplines talk to each other. The form will keep the mercy from sentimentality; the mercy will keep the form from pride. Each will correct the other, week after week, until they are no longer two disciplines but one life.

The form, prayed in charity, leads the soul home. The form alone does not. Neither does a charity that refuses the discipline of form. The Church has always asked for both, together, and the old saint from Hippo, who knew both as well as anyone, left a single Latin phrase that holds the whole matter: *dilige, et quod vis fac* — **love, and do what you will**. He did not mean, *do whatever you feel like*. He meant, *if the love is there, really there, everything else you do will take its shape from that love, and the love will not lead you wrong.* Let the form be what your love does. Let the charity be what your form is for.

*"Dilige, et quod vis fac. — Love, and do what you will."* (*St. Augustine*)
