There is a particular journey that has become common among serious Catholics of our time. A soul, hungry for something the parish of its childhood did not give, stumbles into an old chapel on a Sunday morning and finds an unfamiliar Mass in progress. Latin. Incense thick in the air. A schola in the loft singing what sounds like it was written for angels. The priest with his back to the people, kneeling more than speaking. The Communion rail. The veils. The silence. Something in the soul that had been quietly starving begins to eat.

This is not a trivial experience, and no pastor worth his salt will mock it. The old rites carry a weight that the soul recognizes before the mind can account for. Kneeling for Communion returns the body to what the body is for. The chant settles the nerves of a generation raised on noise. The visible seriousness of the liturgy tells the soul, *this is not a pep rally; something is actually happening here.* A real grace has come through a real door, and the soul is right to walk through it.

But after a year or two, for some souls, a subtle shift can begin. The rite, which was a door, starts to become **the test**. Catholics at the ordinary form Mass down the street are no longer simply fellow Catholics — they become *less serious*, a lower tier, the sort of people who haven't yet figured it out. The veil, which began as a simple act of reverence, drifts toward a loyalty badge — worn not only before the Lord but, quietly, before the other women in the pew. Rubric disagreements stop being rubric disagreements and harden into moral verdicts. A priest who holds his hands the wrong way at the *Per ipsum* is no longer a man with a different formation; he is a man of bad faith.

Name this carefully, because the soul in it almost never sees it. This is not the gift of tradition. This is **the Pharisee's trap wearing a lace alb**. It is the oldest temptation of religious seriousness, the one Our Lord named most often and most sharply — the temptation to take real gifts from God and use them as stones to measure ourselves against our neighbor. The rites did not cause it; the rites are innocent. The trap was there before the rites and will be there after them. But the trap has learned to wear the clothes of fidelity, and in those clothes it becomes very hard to see.

A soul drawn to the old forms does well to love them. A soul that has begun to use them to feel superior to other Catholics has begun, without noticing, to trade the fire for the form.

*"Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites; because you tithe mint, and anise, and cumin, and have left the weightier things of the law: judgment, and mercy, and faith."* (*Matthew 23:23*)
