Into this precise difficulty — the serious soul who has begun to confuse fidelity with bookkeeping — the Church sends a small French Carmelite who died at twenty-four and never left her cloister. St. Thérèse of Lisieux is a Doctor of the Church for many reasons, but one of them is this: she saw, with unusual clarity, that the holiness most souls are called to is *not measurable*, and she gave that invisibility a name. She called it the *little way*.

Thérèse could not, she said frankly, do the great things. She could not preach in the missions, though she longed to. She could not undertake the heroic penances of the old Carmelite mothers; her body was too weak, her superiors too prudent, her life too short. She watched herself, in the early years of the convent, trying to grasp at greatness and always falling short of it, and she felt the temptation that haunts every serious soul: *if I cannot do the visible heroic thing, then perhaps I am not really a saint at all.*

Her answer was to invert the question. Perhaps the saint is not the one who does the visible heroic thing. Perhaps the saint is the one who *sanctifies the unremarkable*. The irritating sister in the choir stall whose rosary beads clack — receive her with a small, real smile instead of the cold look that came naturally. The tiresome laundry. The winter rising in the dark. The sister whose temperament cuts against yours — choose to serve her as if she were Christ, precisely because the natural self does not want to. None of these are countable. None of these would register on a dashboard. None of these were seen by the other sisters in the community, many of whom, after her death, were surprised to learn she had been a saint at all.

This is the point. *She was formed by what no one saw.*

In the manuscript that became *Story of a Soul*, she writes: *"Love proves itself by deeds, so how am I to show my love? Great deeds are forbidden me. The only way I can prove my love is by scattering flowers, and these flowers are every little sacrifice, every glance and word, and the doing of the least actions for love."* Scattering flowers — a phrase almost too soft to be the spine of a spirituality. And yet this is the thing. The interior disposition of love, offered in acts so small they have no name, is the substance of most holiness. The Church's canonizations record the visible exemplars; the unnamed multitude of the saved in heaven were formed the same way, on the same scale, and most of them would have had nothing at all to enter into a log.

The little way is not a lowering of the standard. It is a raising of the *kind* of thing that counts. It tells the committed soul: *you do not need a streak. You need a thousand unnoticed Yeses. And you already have the opportunity for most of them in your kitchen, your office, your commute, and the next conversation you do not want to have.*

*"To love is to give everything and to give oneself."* (*St. Thérèse of Lisieux*)
