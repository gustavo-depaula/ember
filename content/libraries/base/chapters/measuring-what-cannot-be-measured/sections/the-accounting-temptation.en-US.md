There is a temptation that waits for the faithful soul at the far end of a long obedience. Not the temptations of early fervor — distraction, dryness, boredom in the pew — but a later, quieter one that wears the robes of fidelity and is therefore harder to see.

The soul has been praying for years. Morning prayer most mornings. Mass on Sundays and often on weekdays. Confession on a rhythm. Lent kept. A rosary carried. Something like a rule of life. And because the soul is serious, it has begun, almost without noticing, to *keep score*.

Forty-seven days of morning prayer in a row. A streak. On day forty-eight the alarm is missed, or the child is sick, or the heart simply cannot rise, and the chain is broken — and a feeling arrives that is not quite grief and not quite repentance. It is *chagrin*. The chagrin of a record broken. The soul mourns the number more than the missed encounter with the Lord.

Or another version. This Lent, compared with last Lent. Last year the soul kept the fast cleanly; this year it has slipped twice. The comparison hardens into self-judgment — or, worse, into a small, secret pride: *I am better at Lent than I used to be.* The examen, which was meant to be an honest meeting of the soul and its Lord, has become a quality audit. Devotion tallied, weighed, benchmarked.

Name the thing plainly: **spiritual pride that wears the robes of fidelity**. It does not look like pride. It looks like diligence. It looks like taking the interior life seriously. That is precisely why it is dangerous — the enemy, who cannot pull the committed soul back into obvious sin, settles for pulling it into the subtler sin of admiring its own devotion. Aquinas saw this: the spiritual goods themselves can become objects of attachment, envy, and that strange sloth which mourns not the absence of God but the absence of one's own performance before God.

St. John of the Cross names it sharper still: **spiritual gluttony**. The soul develops an appetite not for God but for the *taste* of religious exercises — the satisfaction of having prayed well, the reassurance of a consistent record, the sweetness of feeling devout. He warned that beginners often cannot tell the difference between seeking the Lord and seeking the consolations of the Lord, between loving prayer and loving the self that prays.

The accounting temptation does not begin in malice. It begins in care. A soul that has been fighting for its prayer life for years has learned to *protect* its prayer life, and counting is one way to protect it. The danger is not in noticing; the danger is in letting the number become the thing.

*"Take heed that you do not your justice before men, to be seen by them."* (*Matthew 6:1*)
