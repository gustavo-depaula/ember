A word, then, about the thing in your hand. The app you are reading this in has streaks. It has counts. It has a record of what you have prayed and when, and it will, from time to time, congratulate you for consistency. These features exist for a reason, and the reason is good, but it is important to be honest about what they are and what they are not.

They are a **scaffold for the will**. The human will, even a serious one, forgets. It intends to pray at 6 a.m. and at 6:05 is reading the news. The count, the streak, the small notification, are ways the environment around the will helps the will remember the appointment it already chose to keep. This is not cheating. This is how adult human beings have always organized their lives: calendars, breviaries, the bells of monasteries, the mother's tap on the shoulder. An app that reminds you to pray is, at its best, a small bell.

They are **not a report card before God**. They are not a measurement of your standing with the Lord. They are not evidence for or against your soul. The streak does not make your prayer better when it grows, and does not make it worse when it breaks. It is simply the outer shape of a habit, and the outer shape of a habit is not the habit itself, let alone the love within the habit.

The correct relationship to these features is **instrumental**. When the streak helps you pray — when seeing it quietly nudges you into morning prayer on a hard day — use it, and thank God for the small help. When the streak begins to get in the way — when you find yourself refusing a longer examen because it would spoil the pattern, when you feel smug about the count, when a broken streak pushes you toward *not* beginning again — put it down. Ignore the number. Delete the badge. Silence the notification. The app exists to help you pray. If it is helping you do something else, turn that part off.

There is no shame in this. A rosary bead that has become a distraction should be set aside for a while. A spiritual book that has become vanity should be closed for a season. A tracker that has become an accountant should be quieted. None of these tools is the interior life; they serve it or they do not.

St. Francis de Sales, patron of gentleness with one's own soul, says it plainly: *"Do not be troubled with what you have done, but keep doing."* That sentence is a medicine against every version of the accounting temptation. You missed morning prayer. Good: begin again tonight. The streak is gone. Good: you never needed it. Your Lent is worse than last year's. Good: last year's Lent was not the one the Lord was asking of you; *this* one is. The soul that keeps doing, without brooding over the ledger, is the soul that is actually growing.

*"Do not be troubled with what you have done, but keep doing."* (*St. Francis de Sales*)
