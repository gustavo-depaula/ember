If not the streak, then what? The question is fair. A soul formed in a metric-saturated world will ask, reasonably, *what should I be looking at instead?* The Lord has not left the question unanswered. He has, in fact, given a list, and it is known, and it is almost entirely unquantifiable.

He gave it on a hillside. *Blessed are the poor in spirit. Blessed are the meek. Blessed are they that mourn. Blessed are they that hunger and thirst after justice. Blessed are the merciful. Blessed are the clean of heart. Blessed are the peacemakers. Blessed are they that suffer persecution for justice' sake.* These are the Beatitudes, and they are the Lord's measuring rod — which is to say, the shape of the soul He is forming. None of them are countable. You cannot tally poverty of spirit. You cannot graph meekness. You cannot chart how pure the heart has become. These qualities grow the way trees grow — slowly, from within, usually without the tree noticing — and they are the true measure of a life.

He gave it again in Matthew 25, the parable of the final judgment, where the verdict turns on what the soul did without even realizing. *I was hungry and you gave me to eat; I was thirsty and you gave me to drink; I was a stranger and you took me in; naked, and you covered me; sick, and you visited me; in prison, and you came to me.* The saved are astonished: *Lord, when did we see thee?* They did not know. They were not keeping records. They had simply, over a lifetime of small choices, become the kind of person who does these things, and the Lord was present in each of them, uncounted and unannounced.

Jean-Pierre de Caussade, writing to his nuns, gave this a name: **the sacrament of the present moment**. The whole of the will of God for you is hidden in the small reality in front of you — this conversation, this task, this inconvenience, this person. Not in the grand hypothetical future prayer you will someday pray, not in the spiritual heights of last year's retreat, not in the comparison between this Advent and that one. *Here. Now. The sick mother you are about to visit. The spouse in the next room. The work on the desk. The interior Yes or No that is about to be offered in this very next second.* This is the sacrament. Everything else is weather.

And then, at the end of the temple accounts, the Gospel of Mark leaves a small picture that should be kept close by every serious soul. *And there came a certain poor widow, and she cast in two mites, which make a farthing. And calling his disciples together, he saith to them: Amen I say to you, this poor widow hath cast in more than all they who have cast into the treasury. For all they did cast in of their abundance; but she of her want cast in all she had, even her whole living.* The Lord watched the treasury. He did not count what the rich gave. He pointed at the thing that registered on no ledger — a coin so small it would have been beneath anyone's attention — and named it the greatest offering of the day.

A soul the Lord is forming is a soul less and less preoccupied with measurement, more and more preoccupied with the person in front of her. Less and less concerned with how *this* Lent compares, more and more present to *this* afternoon. Less and less watchful of the count, more and more watchful of the next small act of charity the day is about to ask. The streaks will take care of themselves or they will not; it matters very little. What the Lord is weighing is elsewhere.
