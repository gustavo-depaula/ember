# Chapter LXXXV. That the Divine Will does not take away Contingency from things

**CHAPTER LXXXV**—*That the Divine Will does not take away Contingency from things*[^1]

HYPOTHETICAL necessity in the cause cannot lead to absolute necessity in the effect. But God’s will about a creature is not absolutely necessary, but hypothetically so (Chap. LXXXIII). Therefore the divine will is no argument of absolute necessity in creatures. But only this absolute necessity excludes contingency: for even a contingent fact may be extended either way into an hypothetical necessity: thus it is necessary that Socrates moves, if he runs. It does not therefore follow that a thing happens of necessity, if God wills it: all that holds is the necessary truth of this conditional: ‘If God wills anything, the thing will be’: but the ‘consequent’ (as distinguished from the ‘consequence’) need not be a necessary truth.[^2]

[^1]: A ‘contingent’ thing, be it remembered, is a thing that actually is, but absolutely might not be. Except God, all actuality is ultimately contingent, however it be often hypothetically necessary.

[^2]: Thus in the proposition: ‘If I find the money, I shall pay twenty shillings in the pound’: the truth of the ‘consequence’ is small comfort to my rueful creditors for the falsity of the ‘consequent’
