Many faithful souls, after years of steady prayer, find themselves in a prayer that has gone dark. The Rosary feels wooden. Scripture reads as words on a page. The tabernacle is a box. What used to warm the heart is now a room with the lights turned down, and the soul does not know what it is doing there.

This is ordinary. It has been ordinary for two thousand years. Every saint who wrote about the interior life has named it. The Fathers called it *acedia* when it came with a heaviness; the Carmelite doctors distinguished its shades more carefully; the English spiritual writers called it *dryness*. The names differ; the reality is recognizable: **a soul that has not stopped, that continues the practices, and that is no longer fed by them as before**.

Three things the tradition says early and clearly about it:

1. **It is not a sign that you have done something wrong.** Some dryness comes after fault; most does not. Souls in the state of grace and souls in the state of sin both experience dryness, and the two feel similar to the one experiencing them. The confessional is always available — but do not assume the dryness *proves* you need it.

2. **It is not a sign that God has withdrawn.** The feeling of distance is not the fact of distance. God is at work in the dryness, and often most when the soul is least aware of it. St. Teresa of Ávila called these the hardest years of her prayer life — and the years that made her.

3. **It is not a sign that you have arrived at something great.** Dryness is not, by itself, mystical. The traditional writers are firm about this. Most dryness is ordinary dryness. A small portion, identified with care and usually only by a director, is the purgative darkness the Carmelite doctors discuss. Most of us who say *"I must be in the Dark Night"* are not.

The soul that wants to know what to do in the dryness is asking the right question. The soul that wants to know *what kind of darkness this is* is usually asking the wrong one — or at least asking it of the wrong source. This chapter is for the first kind of asking.

*"My soul is troubled very much: but thou, O Lord, how long?"* (*Psalm 6:4*)
