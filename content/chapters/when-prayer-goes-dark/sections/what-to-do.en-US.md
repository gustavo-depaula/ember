Five things, in rough order of priority. None of them is new; all of them are what the tradition has done for centuries.

**1. Find a spiritual director.** If you already have one, tell him what is happening. If you do not, make this the week's task. A parish priest who knows you over time is the baseline; a religious with contemplative formation (Carmelite, Benedictine, Jesuit of the older school) is ideal. Emails and letters, if in-person is not possible, are better than nothing. The app is not a director and cannot pretend to be one. *"The way of a fool is right in his own eyes: but he that is wise hearkeneth unto counsels."* (*Proverbs 12:15*)

**2. Reduce, don't add.** Cut your prayer rhythm back to what is most essential. Sunday Mass is not negotiable. A decade of the Rosary, prayed slowly, is enough. Five minutes of silent evening prayer is enough. The point is **to do what you do with fidelity**, not to keep up with what you did when prayer was alive.

**3. Pray the Psalter.** When the interior well runs dry, pray someone else's words. The Psalms have been the scripture of the dry phase for three thousand years. Begin with Psalm 42 or 63; sit with a single verse for an entire prayer session if that is what the verse gives. The lament psalms especially — *"Why hast thou forsaken me?" (Ps 22), "Out of the depths I have cried" (Ps 130), "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* — are honest in a way dry prayer often cannot otherwise be.

**4. Pray Compline, or another hour of the Divine Office.** When personal prayer has gone dry, the liturgical prayer of the Church carries you. Compline is short, gentle, and ends with the *Nunc Dimittis* — "now thou dost dismiss thy servant in peace" — which is the right night-time word for a soul that is tired. If Compline is already your practice, add Lauds. If Lauds too, add Vespers. Build slowly; the Office does not require energy.

**5. Before silent mental prayer: open the Gospel, set the crucifix, and do not read.** The discursive meditation you used to do is no longer accessible; do not force it. Instead, **sit**. A single verse open on the page. A gaze on the crucifix. A single word repeated interiorly — "Jesus," "Father," "Behold." You are not producing anything. Production is not the point. You are keeping the appointment.

A note on Mass during dryness. You will feel nothing at Mass sometimes. The Mass does not require your feelings to work — transubstantiation is not a matter of sentiment. Go. Receive. Make a brief thanksgiving. Go home. The soul is being fed even when the palate does not taste.

*"Wait on the Lord; do manfully, and let thy heart take courage, and wait thou on the Lord."* (*Psalm 26:14*)
