The wrong moves are predictable and, once named, easier to refuse. Four to refuse:

**Do not add penance to compensate.** The instinct — "if prayer is dry, let me suffer more to make up for it" — is often the first impulse and is almost always wrong in the dry phase. The soul is already being worked on; self-imposed harshness on top of the hidden work risks burnout, proud self-mortification, or a subtle bargaining with God ("if I do more, the consolation will return"). Keep what the Church prescribes; add nothing new without a director. *"Better to obey than to sacrifice."* (*1 Samuel 15:22*)

**Do not diagnose yourself.** The more firmly you conclude you are in the Dark Night, the more likely you are not. The three signs of the previous section are the tradition's careful work; apply them as tests only under a director. Meanwhile, do not let the name *Dark Night* become a costume for ordinary dryness. The costume looks noble and fits badly.

**Do not parish-shop or teacher-shop for the feeling you miss.** Many who hit the long dryness begin visiting different parishes, changing spiritual directors, trying new devotions, reading new mystics — all seeking the *warmth* they remember. The warmth is not found by being sought; it is *given* or *withheld* on a schedule that is not ours. Staying put is most of the answer. Stay in the parish you were already attending, with the director you were already seeing, using the prayer you were already praying.

**Do not stop.** The one response that guarantees failure is to stop the rhythm entirely. The soul who stops Mass during dryness stops receiving the very grace that prepares the next stage. Reduce before you stop: ten minutes of silent prayer can become five, and five will keep the heart where it was formed; a full Rosary can become one decade; a full Office can become Compline. Fidelity in smaller measures outruns zeal in nothing.

One more refusal, subtler: *do not blame those around you*. The dryness is not your spouse's fault, your children's fault, your pastor's fault, or your parish's fault. The characteristic move — "if only my situation were different, prayer would be alive again" — is an escape valve for real interior work. The Lord has put you where you are; the dryness is part of where He has put you.

*"In the morning I will stand before thee, and will see: because thou art not a God that willest iniquity."* (*Psalm 5:5*)
