St. John of the Cross, who wrote more carefully about this than anyone, gave **three signs** that distinguish the purgative dryness he called the *Night of the Senses* from the ordinary dryness that accompanies fault, distraction, tepidity, or fatigue. The signs are in *Ascent of Mount Carmel* II.13 and *Dark Night* I.9. Here they are plainly:

**First.** The soul *finds no pleasure or consolation in the things of God, nor in any created thing.* Ordinary dryness leaves the soul still drawn to one creaturely consolation or another — conversation, food, travel, music, work. The purgative night turns the whole landscape grey. What once compensated for dry prayer no longer compensates.

**Second.** The soul's *memory centers on God with painful care and solicitude*, thinking it is not serving God but going backwards. Ordinary dryness produces indifference; the Night produces an anxious wanting, a sorrow at not being able to pray as before, a sharp fear that the soul is failing God. The more the soul labors, the more it feels it is failing.

**Third.** The soul *cannot meditate or make reflection, or form pictures in the imagination, as it used to* — even when it places itself deliberately before a Gospel scene or a mystery of the Rosary. The capacity has simply dried up. Not for lack of effort; effort no longer reaches.

St. John is firm: **all three signs must be present, and they must be sustained over time**. One or two of them, or a passing week of all three, is not yet the Night. Dry prayer alone is not it. Even painful dry prayer alone is not it. The three together, persistent, are the specific form he is naming.

And he adds a warning that has saved many souls: *the one who is in this state is usually the last to know it is anything more than her own failure*. Souls who announce themselves to be in the Dark Night almost never are. The name is too large; it is easy to claim and hard to wear.

What to do with this? Hold it as a map, not a diagnosis. If all three signs seem to be present for you, and have been for a long time, and you have a spiritual director, bring it to him — *he* is the one to classify. If you do not yet have a director, the recognition is not yet yours to claim; continue faithfully with the simple measures of the next sections and seek a director with some urgency.

*"He hath set me in dark places, as those that are dead of old."* (*Lamentations 3:6*)
