So the psalm is open on the page. The soul still feels nothing. What now?

**Read it slowly.** Not fast, not to finish. A psalm is not a task you complete; it is a space you walk through. If it takes ten minutes to pray six verses, it took ten minutes.

**Read it aloud if you can.** Even in a whisper, even into a pillow. There is an old principle of the Fathers: **the mouth works where the heart is numb**. The breath, the lips, the voice — these are parts of the person too, and they can pray when the interior feelings have gone silent. A monk chanting Matins in choir at three in the morning is not always moved; the mouth keeps the vigil the heart cannot yet.

**Pick one line. Stay there.** This is what the tradition called *ruminatio* — rumination, chewing on a word the way cattle chew grass. Read the psalm once, see which line rises slightly off the page, and then **stop moving**. Stay with the line. Repeat it softly. Let it do its work. You are not required to reach verse 12. One verse, held for twenty minutes, is already a session of prayer the saints would recognize.

John Cassian, in the tenth *Conference*, taught the Egyptian desert's method for the unceasing prayer of the monk: a single short verse — *"Deus in adiutorium meum intende, Domine ad adiuvandum me festina"*, "O God, come to my aid; Lord, make haste to help me" (Psalm 69:2) — repeated through every labor, every distraction, every temptation, every hour of the day and night. This verse, Cassian said, **contains within itself everything a human soul has to say to God**: the cry for help, the confession of weakness, the confidence that God hears. The Latin Church took this so seriously that it placed this verse at the opening of every Hour of the Divine Office to this day. When a priest opens his breviary at Lauds, his first words are Cassian's verse.

So if a whole psalm is too much, take one verse and make it the prayer of the hour. If one verse is too much, take four words. *"Lord, have mercy."* *"Have mercy on me."* *"Into thy hands."* *"How long, O Lord."* These are real prayers. These are prayed prayers.

Know this: **the psalm prays you**. Even when you feel nothing, the words are doing something. They are shaping the soul while the soul rests. A field that is lying fallow is still a field, still being prepared, still under the working of rain and sun. You are not wasting the hour. You are letting the Church's prayer carry you when your own cannot.

*"O Lord, thou wilt open my lips: and my mouth shall declare thy praise."* (*Psalm 50:17*)
