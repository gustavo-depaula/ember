## The Four Rungs

### 1. Lectio — Reading

Take a short passage. A few verses. Read it slowly, aloud if you can. Then read it again. And again. You are not aiming at volume or ground covered — you are aiming at presence. Let the words *enter*.

Monastic tradition called this *ruminatio* — "chewing." A cow does not rush its food. The mind moves across the text until a word or phrase *catches*: a verb that surprises, an image that lingers, a sentence that reads you.

### 2. Meditatio — Meditation

Hold the word that caught you. Turn it over. Ask: *What is God saying in this word? What does it mean in Scripture elsewhere? What does it mean in my life today?*

Meditation is not analysis — it is honest, prayerful attention. You are not writing a commentary; you are sitting with a Person who is speaking. Let the text question you before you question the text.

### 3. Oratio — Prayer

From meditation rises prayer. The text has spoken; now you speak. Thank, ask, repent, offer, adore — as the word suggests. If Scripture showed you a gift, give thanks. If it showed you a wound, ask healing. If it showed you a sin, repent. If it showed you Christ, love Him.

This is where the exchange happens. Monks called this the *heart's answer*.

### 4. Contemplatio — Contemplation

Sometimes — not always — the words fall silent. You simply rest in the presence of God. Nothing more is required. *"One thing is necessary,"* and Mary chose it (Lk 10:42).

Do not force contemplation. Do not grade yourself for not reaching it. It is a gift. When it is given, receive it. When it is not, the other three rungs have already done their work.
