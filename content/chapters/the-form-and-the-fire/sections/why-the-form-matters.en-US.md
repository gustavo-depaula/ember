Be honest about the other side. It is tempting, when correcting a Pharisaical drift, to swing hard in the other direction and pretend that form does not matter at all. That pretense is not Catholic, and this chapter will not make it.

The form **does** matter. It matters more than a generation of well-meaning catechesis has been willing to admit. Pope Benedict XVI, across decades of writing, kept returning to the phrase *ars celebrandi* — the *art* of celebrating. The Mass is not a generic container into which a community pours whatever it happens to feel; it is a specific act with a specific shape, and that shape teaches. The way the priest holds his hands, the direction he faces, the silence at the consecration, the words chosen and the words refused — all of these are catechesis, whether or not anyone is paying attention. A sloppy liturgy teaches a sloppy theology; a reverent liturgy teaches reverence. The body learns what the mouth repeats. The soul takes the shape of what it kneels through, week after week, for fifty years.

The old rite is a treasure. It is not a museum piece to be tolerated by a condescending pastor; it is a living inheritance. The Roman Canon was old when Charlemagne was young. The propers chanted in a good Tridentine Mass on a Sunday in Ordinary Time were chanted in substantially the same form to saints whose names you know and saints whose names only God knows, century after century, on every continent the faith has reached. To love this and to want to protect it is not a quirk of taste; it is a right response to a real thing. Souls who feel drawn to the ancient liturgy are not wrong to love it, and they are not wrong to be distressed when it is treated with the indifference so many parishes have treated it with.

The fault this chapter names is not in loving the form. The fault is in isolating the form from the charity it was meant to carry, and then using the form as a measuring stick against other souls. A beautiful Mass celebrated in a parish whose members cannot bear to speak kindly of their neighbors is not a triumph of tradition; it is a judgment on it. A soul who loves the old rite and cannot love the Catholic in the sanctuary next door has misunderstood what the old rite is for.

Our Lord came, He said, not to abolish but to fulfill. The old rites are fulfilled, not abolished, in the charity they exist to form. Defend them. Pass them on. Give your children the chant you were given. Do this *and* do the other — the other being mercy toward the priest whose formation differed from yours, patience with the novus ordo Catholic who loves Christ without loving Latin, gentleness toward the new convert who does not yet know why the veil matters to you. If the form you love has not begun to make you kinder, the form has not yet done its work in you.

*"Do not think that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets. I am not come to destroy, but to fulfill."* (*Matthew 5:17*)
