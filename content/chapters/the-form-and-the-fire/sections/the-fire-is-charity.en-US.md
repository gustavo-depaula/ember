If the form is the shape the Church has given to love, then the fire is the love itself. And the fire has a name. The tradition has always called it by that name, and refusing to call it by that name has always been the mark of a distortion. The fire is **charity** — the supernatural love of God, and, in God, of the neighbor.

St. Thomas Aquinas, in the *Summa* (II-II.23.7), calls charity the *forma virtutum* — the form of all the virtues. The phrase is technical and worth pausing over. Every other virtue — prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance, religion, humility, patience, the long list — is a real habit, a real perfection of the soul. But each of them, Thomas teaches, is *informed* by charity the way a body is informed by its soul: charity is what makes them alive. Strip the virtues of charity, and you do not have dead virtues — you have no virtues at all, only the outward shapes they used to wear. A chaste body without charity is not chaste in the way heaven counts chastity. A disciplined prayer life without charity is not a prayer life in the way the Father counts prayer. Every virtue without charity is a corpse in the clothes of a saint.

St. Francis de Sales, in the *Treatise on the Love of God*, draws the same line on the side of devotion. Devotion, he writes, is not a separate thing from charity — it is charity in action, charity made ready, charity alive and reaching. The devout soul is not a soul that performs more religious acts than others; it is a soul whose love has begun to do its work. A devotion without charity is, for de Sales, simply a counterfeit. The outward exercises may be flawless; if the love is not there, the exercises have no weight before God.

Test yourself. This is not meant to wound but to help. The traditional soul reading this chapter can ask itself a few concrete questions and learn something true about where it actually is.

*Can you love the Catholic in the guitar Mass next to you?* Not tolerate, not explain, not mourn — **love**. Can you see him as your brother in Christ, joined to you by the same Baptism, by the same Real Presence, by the same faith of the same Church? Can you ask the Lord to bless him, silently, while he sings what you would not sing?

*Can you speak kindly of your novus ordo pastor?* In front of your traditional friends, when the knowing laugh is expected. In front of your children, when it would be easy to pass on a contempt you will not be able to undo. Can you refrain, for the love of Christ, from a mockery your soul does not need?

*Can you forgive a rubric violation the way you would want your own faults forgiven?* The priest who held his hands wrong, the cantor who chose the wrong hymn, the bishop whose decisions wound you — can you take the slow, patient, merciful view of them that you hope the Lord will take of you on the last day?

If the answer to these is no, the form has become an idol. Not because the form is bad; the form is good. But the fire has gone out, and what is left is a shell that no longer carries what it was made to carry.

*"If I speak with the tongues of men, and of angels, and have not charity, I am become as sounding brass… I am nothing."* (*1 Corinthians 13:1–2*)
