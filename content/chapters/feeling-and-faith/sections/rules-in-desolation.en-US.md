The first rule in desolation is also the last, and if you only remember one thing from this chapter, remember this:

**Never change course in desolation.**

St. Ignatius gives this rule bluntly and repeats it, because he knew how the enemy works. In desolation, every decision is colored by the pull downward; the resolutions that looked luminous in the light now look naive, the director's counsel now looks fussy, the Rosary now looks mechanical, the prayer you chose now looks like the wrong one. *Do not trust any of it.* The resolutions you made in consolation were made with a clearer head. Keep them. The changes the desolate mind wants to make are almost always the changes that unmake you.

After that first rule, four more, each practical and old.

**1. Intensify quietly.** Not by adding new practices — the desolate mind will suggest grand replacements it cannot sustain — but by a gentle strengthening of what is already yours. Pray five minutes longer, not an hour longer. Add a brief examen before bed, not a new devotion at midday. Go to confession if it has been a while. Small increases of fidelity push back against the pull.

**2. Remember that it will pass.** The interior weather has seasons; desolation is not the permanent climate of your soul even when it feels like it. The saints who lived in long dryness were careful to *say* this to themselves, not because it lifted the weight, but because it oriented the will. "This is temporary; what I do in it is not." The second sentence is where the work happens.

**3. Examine, but gently.** Desolation sometimes has a cause you can find — a secret sin you have been excusing, a missed Mass, an uncharity you never named, a habit of entertainment that is eroding your interior life. Sometimes the darkness is not a mystical trial but a draft coming through a window you left open. If you find it, close the window with confession and amendment. But do not *search* desperately; if nothing surfaces after a calm look, accept that this one is not yours to explain, and keep the appointment.

**4. Read the Psalms of lament and the Passion.** Both Testaments have been preparing you for this. The Psalter gives the dry soul words it does not have. The Passion gives the dry soul companionship it cannot otherwise find. Christ in the Garden sweats blood and is not consoled; Christ on the Cross cries *"forsaken"* and is not answered; the soul in desolation who prays beside Him is not alone, however alone she feels.

A word on what *not* to do in desolation: do not take big decisions, do not change your director, do not abandon the devotions you committed to, do not announce your state to everyone you know, do not spend the day on your phone hoping distraction will substitute for grace. The pull of desolation is toward isolation and escape; both must be refused. Stay where the grace has been growing, even if today you cannot feel it growing.

And a gentle last note: **desolation is not failure**. It is a station through which every serious soul passes. The saints had more of it than they had consolation, and they are the saints. You are not being punished; you are being taught to love without the wages.

*"I have waited patiently for the Lord, and he was attentive to me."* (*Psalm 39:2*)
