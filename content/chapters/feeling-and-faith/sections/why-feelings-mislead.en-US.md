Three reasons the tradition is careful with feeling as a measure of prayer.

**First, feelings have many sources.** A given interior state — warmth, heaviness, calm, restlessness — can come from the Holy Spirit, from the enemy, from your own temperament, from your body, from the weather, from what you ate, from what you read before you sat down, from a conversation you are still replaying. St. Ignatius teaches that the *same feeling* can have different origins depending on the soul's current direction. Tears, for instance, can be a gift drawing you deeper; they can also be a sentimental indulgence that substitutes for repentance. The feeling is the same. The source is not.

**Second, the enemy imitates consolation.** This is the hinge of all the Ignatian rules and the point modern spirituality most often misses. A counterfeit consolation — a sweet enthusiasm for a devotion you have just begun, a lofty inspiration that lets you dispense with the ordinary work, a "peace" about a course of action that the saints would call imprudence — can feel exactly like the real thing while it is in your mouth. Only its *trail* reveals it: what did it leave behind? If what followed was restlessness, pride, avoidance of duty, contempt for the simple souls around you — it was not from God, however sweet its taste at the time.

**Third, God also sends dryness.** The absence of felt consolation is not always desolation. Sometimes it is simply the stripping of supports — the Lord weaning you off milk so that your faith is strengthened without the sensible help you were leaning on. A soul that demands consolation and reads its absence as abandonment has turned the gift into the requirement. The real work is done in both registers: the warm and the cold.

So what is faith, if feelings cannot decide it? Faith is the assent of the intellect to what God has revealed, made alive by the will's love, and sustained by grace. All three of those — *believing*, *willing*, *receiving grace* — are real whether warmth attends them or not. This is why you are asked at Mass to make an act of faith when you receive the Lord, not an act of *feeling* the Lord. The substance does not depend on the sentiment. *Ex opere operato* is not cold doctrine; it is consolation of a different kind — the guarantee that the sacrament is working even when you cannot feel it.

A practical test, inherited from the Carmelites and the Jesuits alike: **trust the direction, not the weight**. A dry prayer faithfully kept, a Mass attended in fatigue, a decade of the Rosary that felt like nothing — these are not failures. Their *direction* is toward God. The warm prayer that ends in self-congratulation, the Mass attended for the music, the devotion pursued because it makes you feel spiritual — their direction is toward the self. Weight tells you nothing; direction tells you everything.

*"Now faith is the substance of things to be hoped for, the evidence of things that appear not."* (*Hebrews 11:1*)
