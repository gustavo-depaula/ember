It is worth naming honestly why this particular imbalance is so common in our moment, because naming it is half the cure.

**Catechetical collapse made apologetics the first formation many converts received.** For a generation, catechesis in most parishes was thin; adults coming into the Church, or returning to it, found serious formation outside the parish — in books, blogs, podcasts, streaming videos, long-form interviews. This was, in its way, providential: an enormous amount of good Catholic content became available to anyone with a phone. But the genre of most of it is *argumentative*: it answers objections, defends doctrines, explains in intellectual terms what the faith teaches. Apologetics is a real art and a real need of the Church, but it is not a *formation in prayer*. A soul fed only on it will know *the case for* Catholicism far better than it knows *how to sit in front of the tabernacle*.

**Study gives a concrete sense of progress; prayer often does not.** After an hour of reading a good theologian, you can point to what you learned; after an hour of silent prayer, you often cannot. The mind of a studious convert, especially if he is competent and hungry, will naturally drift toward the activity where the returns are measurable. Prayer has no such returns on its visible side. It gives returns, but they are often invisible until they surface years later in how you handle your children, how you forgive an insult, how you die. A soul that needs to feel productive will prefer the book to the silence without noticing the preference.

**Doctrine is easier to love than God is.** Doctrine is orderly. It does not interrupt. It does not ask you to forgive your brother before tomorrow morning. It does not sit silently across from you in the chapel while you discover how restless your own interior is. A soul that is uncomfortable with the Lord's particular presence can hide, for a long time, in *thinking about* Him — and the thinking can even be devout and true and still be a hiding place. The devil is not disturbed by an orthodox mind that has built itself a small shelter of correct opinions and never comes out into the open ground where God operates.

**There is also a subtle pride in being the one who knows.** The online Catholic world rewards certainty, and a well-informed convert becomes a minor authority in his social circle quickly. It is very hard to pray honestly — that is, *as a beggar* — when you are also the person who explains the doctrine to everyone around you. The posture of the explainer and the posture of the supplicant are not the same, and over time the first tends to colonize the second until the prayer becomes an internal lecture, even when one tries to make it silence.

None of this is malice. Most souls in this pattern did not choose it; they were *formed* into it by the media they had and by the absence of better pathways. The first step of the cure is simply to see the pattern for what it is: **not a deficit of orthodoxy but a deficit of relation**. The Church does not need you to know less. She needs you to stop using what you know to keep the Lord at a readable distance.

*"I will destroy the wisdom of the wise, and the prudence of the prudent I will reject."* (*1 Corinthians 1:19*)
