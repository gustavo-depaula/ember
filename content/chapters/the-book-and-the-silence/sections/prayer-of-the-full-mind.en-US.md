The question for a well-informed soul is not *"how do I learn prayer"* — much of the learning is already available in this very app and in a thousand books. The question is *how to pray well when you already know a great deal*. Four concrete dispositions, and then the classical practice that integrates them.

**1. Come to prayer empty-handed.** This is the hardest for a well-formed mind and the most important. The temptation is to bring to prayer the book you just finished, the argument you are still thinking about, the theological puzzle that is turning over in your head. The correct movement is the opposite: **set all of it down at the chapel door**. Not permanently — the study is good — but for *this hour*. Prayer is not an extension of the study hour in a more reverent room. It is a different register of being with God altogether, and the full mind must become the empty mind, not of ignorance but of attention.

**2. Shorten the material.** A studious soul will naturally prepare for silent prayer with *too much* to pray about — a whole chapter of Isaiah, a section of St. Thomas on charity, a three-page passage from the *Imitation of Christ*. This is still the student's impulse, not the pray-er's. For silent prayer, **one verse is enough, often too much**. A single sentence from the Gospel, held slowly in the mind for twenty minutes without attempting to master it, is the food of this hour. If the mind escapes into analysis, gently return to the verse; do not chase the analysis and do not scold the mind for wandering — simply come back.

**3. Refuse the reflex to explain.** When a thought surfaces in prayer — "this connects to what the Fathers say about..." — *do not follow it*. Not because the thought is wrong, but because *following it is the return of the student*. Let the insight pass. If it is from the Holy Spirit, it will survive and come back after prayer, in a form you can write down or share with your director. If it evaporates when you do not chase it, it was your own mind entertaining itself with what it already knew. The pray-er is not there to receive insights; he is there to receive *God*.

**4. Accept that you will feel you are wasting time.** A soul used to intellectual productivity will find silent prayer excruciating at first, precisely because the intellect is not being fed in the way it is trained to be fed. You are not wasting time. You are learning, slowly, a mode of being that study cannot give you. The farmer does not resent the seed for the month it spends underground before anything rises.

The classical practice that integrates all of this is **lectio divina** — *divine reading* — a four-step method given to the Church by the monastic tradition and formalized by Guigo the Carthusian in the twelfth century. Briefly:

- *Lectio*: read the passage slowly, once, then again.
- *Meditatio*: let one phrase surface; repeat it interiorly; turn it gently.
- *Oratio*: speak to the Lord out of what the phrase has surfaced — a petition, a sorrow, a thanks.
- *Contemplatio*: stop speaking. Rest in His presence with the phrase in the background, not as an object of analysis but as a presence in the room.

Keep the four steps small. Fifteen to twenty minutes total is more than enough. When the mind wanders into commentary — "this phrase is actually better rendered as..." — return to *lectio*, read the verse again, and let it carry you past the commentary. Over months, this practice teaches the studious mind how to stop studying when the Lord is present, without abandoning the mind altogether. The mind is not discarded; it is **silenced in place**, so that something deeper than thought can begin to happen.

*"Be still, and see that I am God."* (*Psalm 45:11*)
