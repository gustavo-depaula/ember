A final word, the hardest and the most necessary.

There is a conversion the studious soul must eventually undergo, and prayer is what accomplishes it. The conversion is from **instruction to adoration** — from being the one who explains the faith to being the one who kneels in front of the mystery and says nothing, or says only what his father has taught him to say: *"My Lord and my God."* It is not that the explaining ever ends; the Church needs teachers and always will. But a teacher who cannot kneel is not, yet, a Catholic teacher; he is a lecturer on Catholic material. There is a difference.

What changes in the soul that undergoes this conversion?

**The mouth gets slower.** In conversation, the soul is less quick to correct. The Protestant friend's imprecise statement about Mary does not, this time, become an immediate teaching moment; the soul asks herself *what is the love that would help him see this* before she asks *what is the argument that would answer it*. Often the love opens what the argument would have closed. Sometimes the argument is then also given, but later, and differently.

**The reading shifts.** The soul begins to choose differently at the shelf. The apologetics shelf is still visited, but the devotional and ascetical shelves begin to pull — the *Confessions* of Augustine, the *Story of a Soul* of Thérèse, the *Introduction to the Devout Life* of de Sales, the *Spiritual Combat* of Scupoli, the *Imitation of Christ*. These books are not less intellectually serious than the apologetics; they are of a different order. They train the reader not to *know* more but to *become* different. A soul that did not touch them for years and suddenly finds them compelling is a soul being turned.

**The silence grows bearable.** At first, silent prayer is an agony for the studious mind; after a season, it becomes the hour the soul most wants. Not because it has become entertaining — it has not — but because the soul has started to taste what it could not taste before. The *sapientia* is beginning. Thomas, at the end, is the icon of this: a lifetime of unmatched intellectual labor, and then, in December of 1273, *"straw."* Not because the work was wrong. Because what he had begun to taste was larger than what the work could contain.

**The argumentative posture softens in every corner of life.** The spouse who is always a little bit in debate retreat; the friend who always knew the counter-position before the question was finished — this one gets quieter. The quiet is not indifference; it is the opposite of indifference. It is the quiet of a soul that has begun to suspect that every human being across from her is more mysterious than any sentence she could say about them, and that the same is true, incomparably, of God.

A small warning. Some souls, when they recognize themselves in this chapter, will swing to the other extreme: burn the apologetics, renounce study, perform a kind of anti-intellectual piety. Do not. The cure for an imbalance is not the opposite imbalance; it is the balance itself. Keep studying. Keep reading what makes you understand. **And carve out, inside the life of study, a hour of silence that is not study**. The hour will work on the study, and the study will work on the hour, and over years a different kind of Catholic mind will come into being — one that is wise as well as clever, prayerful as well as accurate, and finally good company for people who are broken and do not know what they believe.

That is what the Church needs from you. That is what prayer is for.

*"Magister adest, et vocat te."* — *"The Master is here, and calleth for thee."* (*John 11:28*)
