Stop, for a moment, and catalogue honestly what the numbers cannot see.

A Mass was attended on Tuesday. The dashboard registers it. What the dashboard does not register: that the Mass was offered for a colleague the soul still resents, that the resentment returned in the parking lot, that the soul rehearsed, all the way home, the cutting thing it might yet say. A Rosary was prayed on Wednesday — fifteen checked minutes in the log. What is not in the log: the rushing, the thought about the email that waited at the bottom of the decade, the thin attention given to the mysteries, the way the prayer was said *to the ceiling* rather than to the Mother of God. A Communion on Thursday. The count goes up. The heart received the Lord perfunctorily, out of habit, and forgot Him by the time the car reached the stoplight.

None of this is pretended piety. The Mass was real. The Rosary was real. The Communion was real. Grace works in ruins. But if the interior life is measured by the outer gesture alone, the measurement is flattering itself — because the outer gesture can be kept almost intact while the thing it was meant to carry quietly bleeds out.

Now turn the page over. Consider what the dashboard *also* cannot see.

A woman crosses the office at noon and thinks, almost without thinking, *Lord, have mercy on him*, about a man she does not like. That sigh, seven words long, is not in any log. A husband, who has been nursing a grievance against his wife for a week, chooses over coffee to say the small, costly sentence: *I'm sorry for how I spoke on Saturday.* Not in the log. A mother forgives, for the fortieth time, the sister whose name still makes her stomach tighten — forgives not with feeling but by an act of the will, there in the kitchen, between loads of laundry. Not in the log. A worker at 3 p.m., shoulders aching, silently offers the ache to the Lord for a nephew who has left the Church. Not in the log. The tongue held back when the sharp remark was already at the lips. The moment of charity toward the cashier having a bad day. The small, decisive turning away from the browser tab that was about to open. None of it counted, none of it tallied, and most of it, the Lord will say on the last day, was the greater part of what was done for Him.

St. John of the Cross teaches that the most profound work of grace happens below the level of the soul's own perception. The deepest Yes to God is often the one the soul does not even know it has said. The most powerful prayers are frequently the ones that felt like nothing.

The rule, then: what is visible to the app is often the least of what God counts; what is invisible to the app is often the most.

*"Thy Father who seeth in secret will repay thee."* (*Matthew 6:6*)
