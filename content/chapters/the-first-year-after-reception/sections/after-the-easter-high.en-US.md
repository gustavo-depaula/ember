If you came into the Church at the Easter Vigil, you will very likely remember that night for the rest of your life. The candles. The Exsultet sung into the dark. The water poured, the oil cold on the forehead, the host placed on your tongue for the first time. Many new Catholics, asked years later, still describe that Vigil as the most profound hour of their life so far. That is not an exaggeration and it is not fervor talking. Something really did happen. The sacraments worked.

And then, slowly or suddenly, the hour ended. The sponsors hugged you. You went home. You slept. A week later there was a Tuesday, and the Tuesday was ordinary.

The temptation at that point is to read the ordinary as a failure — as if the Vigil had promised a permanent altitude and something has already gone wrong because you are no longer there. It had promised no such thing. The Vigil is a **peak**, not a plateau. Peaks, by their nature, do not last; you visit them, and then you come down. The Spirit almost never leaves a soul on the mountaintop, because the mountaintop is not where the work of ordinary Catholic life is done. That work is done in the valley — in the parish that is not as beautiful as you imagined, on the morning you do not feel like praying, in the week when nothing particular is happening and you are still, quietly, a Christian.

Peter on the mountain of the Transfiguration is the type. He sees Christ in glory with Moses and Elijah and blurts out, *"Lord, it is good for us to be here. Let us make three tabernacles."* Build a shelter. Stay. Do not come down. And Mark notes, gently, that he did not know what he was saying. He wanted to freeze the peak. But the road from that mountain led down, and then to Jerusalem, and then to the cross, and only then to the empty tomb. The glory was not cancelled by the descent. The glory was the reason the descent could be borne.

So: expect descent. Not as failure, not as punishment, not as a sign that the Vigil was less than it seemed. Expect it the way a climber expects the walk back down — as the ordinary second half of the thing, the half in which the ascent is carried into the rest of the life.

The Vigil is still true on the Tuesday. The character is still sealed. The grace is still there. You have not lost anything. You are simply, now, a Catholic on a Tuesday, which is what the whole mountain was for.
