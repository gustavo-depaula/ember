This is the section that is the hardest to write honestly, and the one new Catholics need most. Some months into the first year, almost every convert runs into it: the quiet, unwanted realization that many of the cradle Catholics around you do not appear to believe what you just turned your whole life upside down to believe.

The friend who sponsored you at the Vigil barely goes to Mass now and laughs about it at brunch. The aunt who fought for your Catholic wedding cannot finish the Creed. The pastor who received you seems tired, distracted, short with the sacristan. A man who made a long speech at your reception about the joy of the faith has, it turns out, not been to confession in fifteen years. The examples accumulate. The shock is real, and because it is layered on top of your own still-fresh fervor, it stings in a way that is hard to put words to. You risked something to come in. They seem to be inside without risking anything. And you begin, without meaning to, to feel a small, superior disappointment in them — and, worse, a creeping contempt for the Church that lets them stay.

Three things have to be held together here, and if you hold them together, the scandal can be made to carry you deeper. If you do not, it will turn you sour within two years.

**First: the Church is not the Catholics in it.** The Church is Christ's body, and his body contains both saints and the half-asleep, and has contained both since the apostles themselves quarreled about who was greatest while he was walking to Jerusalem to die. The Church you believe in at the Creed is not the roster of names on the parish list. It is Christ with all his members, living and dead, awake and drowsy, and it is held together by him, not by the average fervor of the people in the pew next to you. No cradle Catholic's tepidity disproves the Creed, because the Creed was never a claim about the cradle Catholics. It is a claim about Christ.

**Second: you were once where they are.** Maybe not at the same place in the same way — your sleep may have been a different sleep, outside the Church rather than inside, or a different inside — but you were not better than them. You were earlier in the journey, or differently asleep, or simply not yet visited by the particular grace that visited you last Easter. The grace that woke you was a gift, not a diploma. If you had been handed the faith at six days old, fed it in badly-catechized classes, and surrounded by the noise that surrounded them, you would very likely be standing exactly where they are standing, making exactly the shrugs they make. Remember that honestly, because it is honest.

**Third: judging them is a step back into the Pharisee's coat you just took off.** You were washed at baptism or at the Vigil. The Pharisee's coat — *I thank you that I am not like other men* — does not belong on a newly baptized Christian. When you feel it settling on your shoulders as you watch a cradle Catholic scroll through his phone at the consecration, take it off. The moment you are proud of being more serious than other Catholics is the moment you have stopped being a Catholic and started being a critic, and the critic will not persevere.

What, then, to do. Pray for them. By name, quietly, on your own, without telling them. When you find yourself preparing to complain about a parishioner or the pastor, close the sentence before it is spoken; complaint about the Church is acid on a new soul, and converts who speak it fluently by year two are usually out the back door by year five. Let the disappointment, instead, drive you **further into your own fidelity** — deeper into Mass, deeper into confession, deeper into the small rule you have begun — rather than into the managerial posture of auditing everyone else's fidelity for them.

The cradle Catholics whose lukewarmness scandalizes you are, many of them, one honest conversation and one good confession away from their own Vigil. You will not be the one who gives it to them by despising them. You may be the one who gives it to them by being, without comment, the person who kept Sunday and kept Friday and kept the three-prayer rule for a year while they watched.

*"Why seest thou the mote that is in thy brother's eye, and seest not the beam that is in thy own eye?"* (*Matthew 7:3*)
