By month twelve, if you have kept the small rule, something has changed that you may not notice from inside. You are no longer new. The catechumens at this year's Vigil are the new ones; you are a year ahead of them. The question in front of you is no longer *how do I build the habit?* — the habit is built. The question is now *how do I deepen?*

The honest answer is that the second year looks very different from the first, and is quieter, and is slower. Four things are worth considering, one at a time, not all at once.

**A regular confessor.** Not a different priest every time, chosen by whoever has the shortest line. The same priest, ideally monthly, who begins to know the shape of your life and can tell when a sin you are confessing is an old pattern and when it is new territory. This matters more than any book you will read in year two. If your parish has a priest who is available, ask to see him on a regular schedule. If not, find a nearby parish or a religious house. Converts who acquire a confessor in year two are substantially the ones who are still praying in year ten.

**One spiritual classic, read slowly.** One. Not a shelf of them. A traditional first choice is Thomas à Kempis's *Imitation of Christ* — short chapters, plain language, read a page or two at a time with a pause afterwards. Other defensible first choices are St. Francis de Sales's *Introduction to the Devout Life* and St. Thérèse's *Story of a Soul*. Pick one; read it over six months; finish it. A book read at that pace enters the soul; a book read in three nights is entertainment.

**One corporal work of mercy, integrated into the week.** Not as a project; as a rhythm. Visit a specific elderly parishioner every Saturday for a half hour. Bring food to a specific shut-in once a month. Take one shift at the soup kitchen the first Tuesday of every month. The point of integration is that the work becomes boring, and still gets done. Mercy that survives boredom is how charity is actually acquired.

**The beginning of mental prayer — ten minutes daily.** Not twenty, not thirty. Ten, in a fixed place, at a fixed time, sitting still before God with a short piece of Scripture open. If ten feels too short, good — keep it at ten. If ten feels too long, keep it at ten and endure. Year two is the year to begin this; year one was too early. The chapter on mental prayer in this library (the Carmelite method) will give you a shape. Do not add a fifth thing in year two. These four, over a second year, are enough.

And the unromantic truth, since it will save you years of confusion if you accept it now: **sanctity is not built in the first year**. The first year is for surviving. The second year is for beginning to deepen. The third and fourth are for the first real acquaintance with your own predominant fault. The fifth through the tenth are for learning, slowly, that the Christian life is not a series of ever-higher peaks but a long and mostly hidden faithfulness. The decades after that are where the saints are made, and they are made almost entirely out of Tuesdays.

The Vigil was the door. The first year was the threshold. The second year is the first few steps of the house. The house itself is large, and you have the rest of your life in it.

*"But grow in grace, and in the knowledge of our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ."* (*2 Peter 3:18*)
