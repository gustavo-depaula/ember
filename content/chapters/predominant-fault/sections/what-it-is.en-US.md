Every soul carries, along with the general wreckage of original sin, **one particular passion that is more stubborn than the others** — one vice that keeps coming back, that colors the day, that you confess over and over in different dress. The ancient and classical spiritual writers called it the *defectus praevalens* — the **predominant fault**.

St. Francis de Sales, in the *Introduction to the Devout Life*, puts it plainly: *"We must know one thing: that there is in every man some one predominant passion which is more powerful in him than the rest, and which, as the lord of the house, rules the whole family of the heart."* (III, 21)

This is not a quirk. It is a *structural* feature of your fallen nature — a particular weak point where grace will have to win a particular victory. The saints were not free of inner enemies. They knew them by name. St. Teresa of Ávila could tell you her predominant fault. So could St. Ignatius. So could your grandmother, if she prayed seriously. Modern Catholic life has largely forgotten this — we confess *sins* (actions) and fail to name the *passion* underneath from which the sins keep springing.

Naming it is half the war.
