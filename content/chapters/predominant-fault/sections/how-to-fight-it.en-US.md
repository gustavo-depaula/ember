Naming the fault is not defeating it. The struggle is slow and specific. The classical writers give a practical four-part combat:

**1. The particular examen.** Twice a day — midday and night — spend two minutes checking *only* on this one fault. Not a general conscience review. *Today, how many times did pride move me? When? What did I do or fail to do?* St. Ignatius built this into the *Spiritual Exercises* for good reason: narrow attention creates traction.

**2. The counter-virtue.** For every predominant fault, choose a single opposed virtue and practice it in concrete, small acts. Against pride: do something hidden and unrewarded this week. Against anger: delay all sharp responses by thirty seconds. Against sloth: finish the boring task first.

**3. Frequent confession, naming the pattern.** Don't just list sins of the past week. Name the **fault** driving them: *"I confess the sins of pride this week — specifically X, Y, Z, which all come from the same root I am trying to uproot."* Confessors can accompany a fight they can see the shape of.

**4. Mental prayer that touches it.** Bring the fault into prayer directly. Ask Christ to heal the specific wound. *"Lord, you know my pride. You know where it began. You know what it costs me. Have mercy and root it out."* Not "help me be better" — specific, named prayer.

Expect the fight to last **years**. St. Teresa of Ávila fought hers long after her mystical experiences began. St. Francis de Sales said he spent twenty-two years mastering his temper. Discouragement at recurrence is part of the disease; perseverance through recurrence is already a victory. *"A thousand victories, and a thousand falls"* is not failure — it is the **life** of sanctification.
