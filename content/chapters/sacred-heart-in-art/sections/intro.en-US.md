The Sacred Heart has inspired some of the most beloved images in Christian art. From the earliest symbolic representations to the great oil paintings of the Baroque and Academic periods, artists have sought to make visible the invisible reality of Christ's burning love for humanity.

The heart as a symbol of the interior life was already present in medieval devotion, but it was only after the revelations to St. Margaret Mary in the seventeenth century that a distinctive Sacred Heart iconography emerged. The first images were simple: a heart crowned with thorns, surmounted by a cross, encircled by flames. These appeared on holy cards, vestments, and altar frontals in Visitandine convents across France.

The great turning point came with Pompeo Batoni's 1767 painting for the Church of the Gesù in Rome. Batoni depicted Christ Himself gesturing toward His visible heart — a composition that became the template for nearly every Sacred Heart image that followed. His work fused Baroque grandeur with devotional intimacy, and copies spread rapidly through the Catholic world.

In the nineteenth century, artists like William-Adolphe Bouguereau brought Academic realism to related devotional subjects — his *Pietà* captures the very moment when the lance pierced Christ's side, opening the Sacred Heart for all humanity. Meanwhile, popular devotional art developed its own rich tradition — the familiar holy cards and statues that adorned Catholic homes and churches around the world.
