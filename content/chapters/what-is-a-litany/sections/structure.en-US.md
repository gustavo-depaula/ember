Most litanies follow a five-part structure that has remained remarkably stable across the centuries:

**1. The Kyrie** — The prayer opens with the ancient cry *Kyrie eleison* ("Lord, have mercy"), *Christe eleison* ("Christ, have mercy"), followed by "Christ, hear us / Christ, graciously hear us." This places the entire prayer under the mercy of the Trinity.

**2. The Invocations** — The heart of the litany: a series of titles, attributes, or petitions addressed to God, Our Lady, or the saints. Each invocation is met with a fixed response — "Pray for us," "Have mercy on us," "Save us," or another phrase. The repetition is not vain; it is the steady rhythm of a heart crying out in trust.

**3. The Agnus Dei** — "Lamb of God, who takest away the sins of the world" is prayed three times with ascending petitions: "Spare us," "Graciously hear us," "Have mercy on us." This echoes the Mass itself and orients the prayer toward Christ's sacrifice.

**4. The Versicle and Response** — A brief scriptural or traditional verse and its response, proper to the particular litany. This transitions from supplication to the concluding prayer.

**5. The Collect** — A formal closing prayer that gathers the intentions of the litany into a single petition, usually ending "through Christ our Lord."

Not every litany follows this pattern exactly. The Litany of Humility, for instance, dispenses with the Kyrie and Agnus Dei entirely, using its own unique petition structure. But the standard framework has proven remarkably enduring — a vessel that holds nearly two millennia of Christian prayer.
