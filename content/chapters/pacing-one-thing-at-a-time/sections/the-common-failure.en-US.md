There is a shape this almost always takes, and it is worth describing plainly so that you can recognize it in yourself if you are in it, or see it coming if you are not.

A soul becomes newly serious about the faith. It may be a conversion, a return after years away, or a quiet deepening in someone who has gone to Mass his whole life. A book has been read, a retreat has been made, a grace has been given. The soul, rightly, wants to live differently now. And so, in the first two weeks, the soul takes on: daily Mass, weekly confession, the full Rosary every day, the Liturgy of the Hours (at least Lauds and Vespers, sometimes all seven), a daily examen, twenty minutes of mental prayer, spiritual reading, the First Friday and First Saturday devotions, a fast on Wednesdays and Fridays, a novena that was recommended, and a consecration to Our Lady that begins next week.

For about fourteen days, all of this is kept. The fervor is real. The soul feels, correctly, that something is happening.

Then there is a Tuesday. The Tuesday is ordinary; the soul is tired; a child is sick; work runs late; the Rosary is forgotten; Lauds is skipped; the examen does not happen; by Wednesday the soul has missed three things and is ashamed. Shame is the hinge. The soul that is ashamed does not usually *reduce* its practices; it *abandons* them. Within a month, nothing is kept. Within two months, the soul has concluded, quietly and privately, *I cannot do this. I am not the kind of person who can live this way. I tried and I failed.*

This is the shape. It is not rare and it is not a character defect. It is a **pacing error**. The soul mistook the height of its fervor for the height of its ordinary strength, loaded the ordinary strength with the weight only the fervor could carry, and then concluded, when the fervor passed, that it had no strength at all. It has plenty of strength. It simply mis-measured.

St. Philip Neri, who watched this happen in Roman laymen for decades, warned his penitents continually against the enthusiasm that will not last — *piccoli passi*, small steps, was almost a refrain of his direction. He would rather a man say one Hail Mary a day for a year than the entire Rosary for a month and then nothing. He was not lowering the bar. He was naming the bar as it actually is for the soul that stands in front of it: **not where you can reach in fervor, but where you can reach on the worst Tuesday of the year.**

*"Better is the end of a thing than the beginning thereof."* (*Ecclesiastes 7:8*)
