The reason for moving slowly is not pastoral softness; it is how the soul actually works.

Virtue, for St. Thomas, is a *habitus* — a stable, acquired disposition of the soul, formed by repeated acts of the same kind until the soul begins to do the act readily, almost without choosing it anew each time. A *habitus* is not a mood or a resolution; it is a change in what the soul *is*. And a *habitus* forms only one way: *by small acts, repeated, over time*. The size of the act matters less than the repetition. Ten thousand small acts form the soul. Ten grand acts followed by nothing do not, because the *habitus* has no chance to take.

This is why one practice kept faithfully for a year changes a soul more than ten practices kept badly for a month. The year of one practice is the soul acquiring a *habitus*. The month of ten practices is the soul acquiring a memory of having failed at ten things. The first is the beginning of the devout life. The second is the beginning of the belief that the devout life is not for people like you.

Consider the *Rule* of St. Benedict. In chapter 64, writing to the abbot about the government of his monks, Benedict gives a line that has guided Western monasticism for fifteen hundred years: *nihil asperum, nihil onerosum* — "nothing harsh, nothing burdensome." The abbot is to "arrange all things so that the strong may have something to strive after, and the weak may not be dismayed." He is to set the pace of the house by the weakest member of the house, not the strongest, because a pace that breaks the weakest breaks the common life, and a common life that is broken forms no one.

The same logic governs your own interior life. You are, on any given week, both the strongest and the weakest member of the little community that is your own soul. You are strong on the retreat, weak on the Tuesday; strong in the morning, weak at ten at night; strong this year, weak in the year of the diagnosis. The rule you write for yourself must be livable by the weakest version of you, not the strongest. If it is not, the weakest version of you will simply not keep it, and the strongest version of you will not be there to rescue her.

St. Francis de Sales says the same thing to laymen in the *Introduction to the Devout Life* I.3: devotion must take the shape of the state of life in which it is lived. "It would not be fitting that a bishop should want to live a Carthusian solitude, nor that the married should want to lay up no more goods than a Capuchin, nor that the workman should spend the whole day in church like a religious." The devout life is not a single height to be climbed; it is the Christian life lived *in the size and shape of the soul actually living it*. A practice that does not fit that shape will not last, and a practice that does not last does not form.

*"Take my yoke upon you, and learn of me, because I am meek and humble of heart: and you shall find rest to your souls. For my yoke is sweet and my burden light."* (*Matthew 11:29–30*)
