When you say *"Bless me, Father — it has been [twenty] years since my last confession,"* something small and good will happen in the priest's heart. He will slow down. He will listen more carefully. He may say, quietly, *"Welcome back."*

He will ask you a few gentle questions if you need help — not to pry, but to make sure the confession is complete. He is trained for this. He is not a judge; he is a physician for the soul.

If your examination has surfaced a mortal sin you are not ready to say out loud, **tell him that**: *"Father, there is something I am struggling to name."* A good confessor will help you through it — or, if genuinely needed, invite you back after more reflection. You will not be turned away.

If the priest you have chosen is unexpectedly harsh, you are still validly absolved. But do not let a bad encounter become a second ten-year gap — find another priest, and come back the following week. The sacrament is bigger than any one confessor.

The absolution itself is the heart of it. When the priest says: *"I absolve you from your sins, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit,"* **you are actually forgiven.** Not hopefully, not mostly — actually. Whatever years or weight you brought in are gone. This is not our promise. It is Christ's.
