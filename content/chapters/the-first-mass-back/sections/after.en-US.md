The Mass ends. The priest goes out. You stand a moment in the pew, unsure what to do with yourself.

Here is the counsel, and it runs against the grain of a returning soul's best instinct: **do not over-plan the next step**.

You will be tempted, on the way to the parking lot, to build a whole life. *"I will come every Sunday from now on. I will pray the Rosary daily. I will read the Catechism cover to cover. I will go to daily Mass on Wednesdays. I will volunteer. I will get rid of the things in my life that do not belong anymore, this week."* The list will be beautiful. It will also, most likely, collapse within two weeks, and the collapse will tell you — falsely — that the whole return was fake.

One Mass does not make a life. But **this one Mass is a hinge**. A door has moved. You felt it move. Do not mistake the size of the door for the size of the life behind it.

A small next step for the week. Only one. Pick one:

- **Go again next Sunday.** That is enough, by itself, to be the whole of this week's Christianity.
- **Go to confession**, if the previous section applies to you and you have not yet gone.
- **Open a Gospel and read one chapter.** Start with Mark. It is short and moves quickly.
- **Say a single Our Father before bed**, each night, for seven nights. Not the Rosary yet. One prayer.
- **Call the parish office** and ask when confessions are heard, or when the next daily Mass is, or whether there is someone to talk to. A five-minute phone call can settle a month of wondering.

Choose one. Do it. Let the rest of the life build itself back slowly, in the way real lives build — by small repeated acts, not by programs announced.

St. Francis de Sales, writing to souls who were resuming the devout life after a lapse, said something like this: begin again gently, and do not be astonished at your own unsteadiness. The devout life is not a performance you perfect; it is a path you walk, falling and rising, and **the rising is the proof of the life, not the not-falling**. He was gentler than most of his century, and right to be.

If you received this morning — thank God, very quietly, at home, not in words. If you did not — do not brood on it. God has been patient across the years you were away; he is patient now across the weeks until confession. What matters is that something has begun again that is real.

Your mother the Church is not in a hurry. She has had you on a list for a long time. She will be here next Sunday, and the Sunday after, and the Sunday after that. The Eucharist will be there. The priest will be there. The bell will ring at the consecration whether you are in the pew or not — but the pew is waiting for you.

Come back. Keep coming back. That is the whole of it.

*"This is the day which the Lord hath made: let us be glad and rejoice therein."* (*Psalm 117:24*)
