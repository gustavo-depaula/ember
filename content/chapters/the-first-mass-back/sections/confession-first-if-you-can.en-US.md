A pastoral note, placed here because it matters and because the tradition is clear.

If you have been away for years and there are grave sins on your soul that you have not confessed, **the ordinary path is confession before communion**. This is not a trick the Church invented to keep you out; it is the path of love. St. Paul's warning — that receiving unworthily is to eat and drink judgment on oneself — is old and serious. The Church does not want you to stand in the communion line with a wound that a single good confession would close. She wants you to come to the table clean and free.

That said: **do not let this stop you from going to Mass this Sunday**. Attend. Stay in your pew, or go forward with arms crossed for a blessing. Both are honorable. Nothing is lost by not receiving today. Everything can be gained by being there, listening, beginning again.

Then — this week or next — **go to confession**.

A few words for the walk to the confessional, because many returning souls freeze there.

**What to say when you go in.** The old formula is still the easiest: *"Bless me, Father, for I have sinned. It has been X years since my last confession."* You are allowed to say *"I do not remember how long."* You are allowed to say *"I have forgotten most of what to do."* The priest has heard every variation. He will help you. He is **glad** you are there — gladder than you can imagine. One returning soul is the whole day's work; it is what he was ordained for.

**What to confess.** The grave matters, as best you remember them. You do not owe him a chronological diary. You are not graded on eloquence. Say the things that are heavy, in plain words. If you cannot remember dates, give ranges. If you are unsure whether something counts, ask — briefly — and let him judge. He is there to lift a load, not to weigh one.

**What not to say.** Do not confess other people's sins. Do not justify yourself. Do not lecture the priest about the Church. Do not try to impress him with your theology or your honesty. Just tell the truth and let him absolve.

St. Monica wept for her son Augustine for seventeen years. He finally came home. If Augustine could come home, so can you. The Prodigal's father *"saw him while he was yet a great way off, and had compassion, and running to him, fell upon his neck, and kissed him"* — notice that the father runs. The confessional is where the running happens now.

John Paul II said in *Reconciliatio et Paenitentia* that the confessional is not a courtroom but **a place of mercy where the Father waits**. You do not have to be good to go. You have only to be honest, and to want, even weakly, to be changed.

*"I will arise, and will go to my father, and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven, and before thee."* (*Luke 15:18*)
