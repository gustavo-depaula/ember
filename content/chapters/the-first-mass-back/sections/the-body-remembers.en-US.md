Something you may not have been warned about: **your body remembers more than you do**.

You will walk past the holy water font and, before you decide anything, your fingers will lift. You will cross the threshold of the nave and find your knee bending toward the tabernacle almost without your permission. At the Sanctus your hands may move to fold without consulting you. You may be startled by it. You may even feel a small resistance — *"I am not sure I want to do this yet"* — and find that you are doing it anyway, because your body learned this before your doubts learned anything. If you were baptized, brought to Mass as a child, catechized even imperfectly, the gestures are laid down in a deeper layer than the arguments you have had with the faith since.

This is gift, not theatre. The Church knew what she was doing when she taught small children to kneel, to bow the head at the Holy Name, to strike the breast at *mea culpa*. She was inscribing the faith into muscle and bone so that on a morning like this morning — decades later, in a soul that has lived hard — the body could still find its way home while the mind was still arguing.

The translation, yes, will feel different. *"And with your spirit"* where your mouth still wants *"And also with you."* Do not let that snag you. Mumble through; the angels know what you mean. What has not changed — what has never changed, in the West or the East, in Latin or the vernacular — is **the Eucharist itself**. The words of institution are the same words Our Lord spoke in the upper room. What was placed on that table that Thursday is what will be placed on the altar this morning. You have not missed it. It has waited for you.

St. Augustine knew this kind of return. He was catechized as a boy, wandered far and hard for years, and when at last he came back he wrote the sentence that has kept countless returning souls in their pews: *"Late have I loved thee, O Beauty ever ancient, ever new, late have I loved thee."* The word to notice is *late*. Not *too late*. Late is still in time. Late still arrives. The Lord, Augustine says in the next line, was already within while he was running outside; the search was never the Lord's failure to be found.

St. Bonaventure put it more briefly: the Eucharist is *memoriale, signaculum, pignus* — a memorial of what Christ has done, a sign of present love, a pledge of what is to come. All three work on the returning soul at once, and not always in the order one expects.

*"Late have I loved thee, Beauty so ancient and so new, late have I loved thee."* (*Confessions X, 27*)
